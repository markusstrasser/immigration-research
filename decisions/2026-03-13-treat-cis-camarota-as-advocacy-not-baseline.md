# 2026-03-13: Treat CIS/Camarota as advocacy, not a baseline estimator

## Context

The repo was asked to evaluate Steven Camarota's January 11, 2024 congressional testimony, "The Cost of Illegal Immigration to Taxpayers". The testimony contains real citations, explicit caveats, and some useful category breakdowns. It also comes from the Center for Immigration Studies, which describes itself as pursuing a "pro-immigrant, low-immigration vision" and was presented in a House hearing framed around the costs of illegal immigration. The question was whether to treat the document as a legitimate fiscal source, propaganda, or both.

## Alternatives considered

1. **Treat it as a neutral fiscal estimate** — Simple. But it would blur the difference between direct measurement and advocacy-mode model construction.
2. **Discard it as propaganda** — Cleaner normatively, but would throw away real descriptive claims and the strongest structured version of a restrictionist fiscal case.
3. **Treat it as an adversarial brief with reusable subclaims** — More work, but preserves useful evidence while constraining how the repo may cite its headline numbers.

## Decision

Choose option 3.

The testimony is admissible as an adversarial source that surfaces cost categories, references, and the strongest version of a restrictionist framing. It is not admissible as a neutral baseline estimate for the fiscal impact of unauthorized immigration.

Use rules:

- Reusable with verification: local cost examples, the general education→earnings→taxes mechanism, and references to major programs or legal eligibility rules.
- Reusable only with heavy caveats: the `12.8M` population estimate, the `59.4%` welfare-use estimate, the `$42B` welfare-cost estimate, the `$68.1B` school-cost estimate, and the `$68,390` lifetime fiscal-drain estimate.
- Not to be quoted as settled fact: any claim that presents the testimony's modeled outputs as if they were direct government measurements or National Academies estimates.

## Evidence

- The House hearing and testimony are real and official. [SOURCE: https://docs.house.gov/Committee/Calendar/ByEvent.aspx?EventID=116727] [SOURCE: https://www.congress.gov/118/meeting/house/116727/witnesses/HHRG-118-JU01-Wstate-CamarotaS-20240111.pdf]
- CIS describes itself as pursuing a "pro-immigrant, low-immigration vision", which is enough to classify it as an advocacy organization rather than a neutral research clearinghouse. [SOURCE: https://cis.org/Center-For-Immigration-Studies-Background]
- The general mechanism tying education to fiscal effects is consistent with mainstream work. [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704] [SOURCE: https://www.nap.edu/catalog/23550/the-economic-and-fiscal-consequences-of-immigration]
- The biggest headline numbers in the testimony depend on the same fragile choice points already identified elsewhere in the repo: residual-status inference, child-cost attribution, and average-cost versus marginal-cost public-goods allocation. [SOURCE: https://www.ssa.gov/policy/docs/ssb/v85n2/v85n2p1.html] [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9107075/] [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12439705/] [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704]
- Recent CBO work supports the claim that local costs are real, but does not validate Camarota's specific scalar estimates. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60165] [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]

## Revisit if

- An independent academic or government replication reproduces Camarota's core scalar estimates with transparent methods.
- CIS releases a cleaner decomposition separating direct illegal-immigrant costs, costs of U.S.-born children, and pure public-goods allocations.
- Better status-identification data become available and materially reduce uncertainty in unauthorized-population denominators.
