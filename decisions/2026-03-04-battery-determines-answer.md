# 2026-03-04: Battery composition determines the sex-difference answer

## Context
Early literature review showed contradictory claims about whether males or females have higher g. The repo needed to decide whether to treat this as a "who's right?" adjudication or reframe the question.

## Alternatives considered
1. **Side with one camp** — Pick Lynn/Irwing (male advantage) or Keith/WJ-III (female advantage) and build from there. Pro: simpler narrative. Con: ignores that the contradiction is informative.
2. **Reframe as battery-dependent** — Treat the divergence across batteries as the primary finding and decompose by subtest/domain. Pro: explains all the data. Con: more complex, no single headline number.
3. **Declare question unanswerable** — Conclude the literature is too messy and move on. Pro: honest. Con: premature — the pattern is structured, not random.

## Decision
Option 2. The subtest decomposition across WAIS (3 countries), WJ-III, ASVAB, and BPR revealed that the sex-difference answer changes sign depending on battery composition. The 36-subtest meta-analysis showed spatial/fluid male-leaning, speed/fluency female-leaning, and the composite depends on the mix. This became the project's core framing: "battery determines answer" is itself the finding.

## Evidence
- WJ-III latent g: females +1-3.5 IQ pts (Keith et al. 2008, speed/fluency-heavy)
- WAIS: null to males +3 pts depending on analyst (Colom 2002 vs Irwing 2012)
- BPR: males +2-4.3 pts (mechanical/spatial, no speed)
- ASVAB sibling design: null d=0.07 (Deary et al. 2007)
- Core-cognitive-only pooled estimate (excluding speed and vocational knowledge): d=-0.177, ~2.7 IQ pts males
