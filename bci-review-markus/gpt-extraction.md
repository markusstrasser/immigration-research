P1. The essays change the problem definition midstream, shifting from attacking read-only BCI as a direct command channel to defending it as a low-bit preference sensor in a search loop. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P2. Essay 1's claim that BCI adds little if a thought already has an external channel contradicts Essay 5's design where BCI augments external channels by inferring local utility. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P3. The "paired-data paradox" (decoding needs labels, labels need external expression) is too strong; it ignores weak supervision, self-supervised alignment, and stimulus-timed contrastive learning. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P4. External grounding is the real requirement for decoding, not necessarily voluntary external expression. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P5. Essay 3’s “causal impossibility” claim regarding the nonstationary manifold is overstated; tracking is possible if drift covariance is bounded and SNR is adequate. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P6. The "nonstationary manifold" problem is an empirical assumption about drift scale and rank, not a causal necessity. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P7. Essay 2’s gating argument (muscles have native gating; BCIs do not) only applies to pure asynchronous BCI; Essay 5 solves this with hybrid inputs like gaze and micro-gestures. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P8. Pure read-only BCI is bad at commit semantics, but hybrid interfaces can offload commit/gating to non-neural channels. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P9. The claim that subconscious signals are useless for high-level vocabularies (Early Essays) contradicts the claim that pre-conscious signals provide value for preference navigation (Later Essays). (Logical Inconsistencies)
P10. Essay 5 describes a multimodal interface with a neural side-channel, not a standalone pure read-only BCI system. (Logical Inconsistencies)
P11. Meister’s ~10 bps limit applies to conscious serial processing/reportable thought, but does not bound all usable brain-derived physiological side information. (Information Theory)
P12. Essay 5’s 5-6 bit edit cycle count is an underestimate because it ignores region of effect, tool selection, reference binding, and error correction. (Information Theory)
P13. A "confirm" gesture is near-zero in semantic content bits but not free in terms of time, false-positive costs, or system state changes. (Information Theory)
P14. Pure EEG-based decoding for the proposed edit cycle would likely result in an effective rate of ~0.3-0.63 bps, which is much lower than the nominal bit count. (Information Theory)
P15. Branch selection in a compressed latent search tree requires very low bandwidth (sub-bps to low-bps), making Essay 5's search task plausible. (Information Theory)
P16. The relevant quantity for BCI utility is not raw sensor entropy, but mutual information with action-relevant latent utility. (Information Theory)
P17. Preference-navigation benefits from EEG exist only in high-uncertainty tasks where users are unsure of their goals until they see candidates. (Testable Predictions)
P18. EEG/BCI will not provide a quality or time benefit over conventional tools for precision-heavy execution tasks (e.g., masking, alignment). (Testable Predictions)
P19. Pure neural gating (submit detection) will fail to meet practical software thresholds (e.g., <1 false activation/hour) compared to hybrid gates. (Testable Predictions)
P20. Cross-session decoder drift will impose a performance tax; foundation-model initialization (ZUNA-class) may reduce but not eliminate this degradation. (Testable Predictions)
P21. The "nonstationary manifold" claim is testable by measuring if a low-rank model (3-5 dimensions) can explain >70% of within-task preference variation. (Testable Predictions)
P22. A semantic-pointer BCI paired with a generator will beat a raw low-bit cursor interface under an equal bit budget for high-ambiguity tasks. (Testable Predictions)
P23. "Intrapersonal RLHF collapse" (narrowing of creativity to decoder-compatible subspaces) is testable by measuring the novelty and diversity of accepted artifacts over time. (Testable Predictions)

P26. Usable decodable detail from internal imagery is likely much lower than introspection suggests ("imagery poverty"). (What the Author Got Right)
P27. The primary value of BCI is reducing preference uncertainty rather than execution uncertainty. (What the Author Got Right)
P28. Increasing the number of separable mental states (K) is more important for BCI capacity than chasing raw bandwidth. (What the Author Got Right)
P29. The break-even condition for BCI utility depends on the ratio of task time to calibration cost; short sessions rarely justify the setup tax. (What the Author Got Right)
P30. Modern 2026 research (ZUNA, Kirigami, CorTec) undermines the most pessimistic early rhetoric but supports the importance of gating and hybrid control. (What the Author Got Right)
