# Synthesis

## Design-space statement

Question:
1. Is low-skill immigration bad for a country, assuming AGI soon?

Current approach being escaped:
1. Single scalar verdict based mostly on labor-market averages or partial fiscal ledgers.

Hard constraints:
1. Need incidence by group and horizon.
2. Need explicit local-system capacity.
3. Need explicit counterfactuals.
4. Need AGI timing as a scenario variable, not an afterthought.

## Explore

1. `I01` + `I12`: replace the scalar national verdict with an institutional-resilience/incidence framework.
Why: this is the cleanest answer to the actual question. A country is not just GDP. It is solvency, housing adequacy, school capacity, order, and political legitimacy.

2. `I10`: elevate `federal positive / local negative` from nuance to headline result.
Why: this is already where the project has the strongest evidence.

3. `I08`: make housing-supply rigidity a core branch.
Why: if housing is fixed in the short run, average-output gains can coexist with real local harm.

4. `I13`: measure employer pass-through versus rent capture.
Why: the Clark-style claim only works if consumer surplus is broad rather than captured by employers, landlords, or incumbent capital.

5. `I03` + `I09`: split the answer by AGI horizon and capital-substitution path.
Why: if AGI/robotics arrive soon, the question shifts from long-run labor scarcity to transition management and capital deepening.

6. `I14`: force explicit counterfactuals.
Why: “bad or good” is meaningless without comparison to automation, pronatalism, internal mobility, training, and offshoring.

7. `I06`: test bottleneck relief instead of average wages.
Why: low-skill immigration may matter most where it unclogs high-leverage bottlenecks.

## Parked

1. `I07`: ecology/carrying-capacity framing.
Reason: useful language, but likely collapses back into housing and fiscal capacity once operationalized.

2. `I11`: AI-driven descendant upside.
Reason: plausible under AGI, but too speculative for current evidence.

3. `I05`: option-value framing.
Reason: conceptually useful, but secondary until the incidence picture is cleaner.

## Rejected for now

1. Pure wage-effect framing.
Reason: too narrow for the actual policy claim.

2. Pure GDP framing.
Reason: can stay positive while institutions degrade.

3. “Average citizen” as the primary unit.
Reason: hides too much incidence.

## Restated question

A better version is:
1. Under plausible AGI timelines, for which origin x destination x household-type inflows does low-skill immigration improve or degrade a country’s institutional resilience, local-system capacity, and net citizen welfare relative to the best available alternatives?

## Next measurements if we continue

1. Rank `origin_puma_household_stage2_context_2023` by school-age burden, county school spend, and housing stress.
2. Estimate whether local harms are broad-based or concentrated in a small number of receiving geographies.
3. Build a pass-through/capture framework for employer gains, consumer gains, renter losses, and landlord gains.
4. Add explicit counterfactual modules: automation, internal mobility, offshoring, pronatalism.
5. Treat AGI timing as three scenarios, not one belief.
