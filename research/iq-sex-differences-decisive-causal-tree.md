## Observation

The project now has a structured multi-surface pattern, not one generic "sex and IQ" result:

1. `NLSY79` quantitative is male-leaning.
2. `NLSY97` raw quantitative is slightly female-leaning, but the signal is concentrated in `Math Knowledge`, not in `PIAT Math` or `Arithmetic Reasoning`.
3. `PIAAC` adult numeracy is male-leaning across country, education, age, and broad occupation cells.
4. `HSLS` transcript math GPA is female-leaning while the standardized math surface is near parity to slightly male-leaning.
5. `TIMSS` broad school math moves femaleward from Grade 4 to Grade 8, especially on `knowing`.
6. `TIMSS Advanced` remains male-leaning, especially on `reasoning`.
7. Recent literature suggests some male-favoring broad school-math divergence can emerge very early after school entry, so late-school-only explanations are too narrow. [SOURCE: `research/iq-sex-differences-frontier-literature-audit.md`]

That geometry rules out simple slogans faster than it identifies one root cause.

---

## Null

If there is **no large battery-invariant sex difference in general ability**, then the current pattern can be generated by a combination of:

1. real domain differences
2. school-knowledge and evaluation surfaces
3. timing / item-family / adaptive-score effects
4. track and advanced-selection differences
5. adult accumulation and occupation sorting

The burden of proof is therefore on any stronger claim that a single latent `g` gap explains the pattern better than this surface-sensitive null.

---

## Generative Decomposition

The project should treat each observed gap as:

`ObservedGap(dataset, surface, age, sample) = LatentDomainGap + MeasurementSurface + EarlySchoolEmergence + SchoolPipeline + TrackSelection + AdultAccumulation + SampleSelection + Noise`

Where:

- `LatentDomainGap` means a real sex difference in the underlying domain being measured
- `MeasurementSurface` means item family, timing, adaptivity, score construction, DIF, and process effects
- `EarlySchoolEmergence` means divergence that appears in the first years of schooling before transcript, advanced-course, or adult accumulation mechanisms dominate
- `SchoolPipeline` means instruction, practice, grading, evaluation, and course-linked knowledge accumulation
- `TrackSelection` means who reaches advanced or selective math surfaces
- `AdultAccumulation` means post-school field, occupation, and use-dependent amplification
- `SampleSelection` means missingness, analyzability, attrition, and transcript coverage

The job is not to pick one story by vibe. It is to estimate which terms are clearly nonzero and which terms still absorb the disagreement.

## Decisive Causal Tree

### Node 1 - Is the disagreement mostly a measurement-surface problem?

**Question:** do the batteries disagree because they are not measuring equivalent quantitative surfaces?

**Current read:** yes, at least partly.

Why:

1. `NLSY97` splits inside the same cohort: `Math Knowledge` tilts female while `PIAT Math` and `Arithmetic Reasoning` do not.
2. `TIMSS` broad-population `knowing` moves femaleward faster than `applying`, while `TIMSS Advanced reasoning` remains male-leaning.
3. The broader project already established that timing, adaptivity, and battery composition can materially move conclusions.

**Current probability:** `0.55` for the `NLSY97` anomaly specifically; `0.72` for the broader project disagreement.

**Decisive test:**

1. multi-group measurement invariance / DIF on the highest-value batteries with item-level access
2. invariant-item rescoring
3. item-family decomposition in `PISA 2018`

**What would harden it:** if the female-looking `NLSY97` signal collapses on invariant-item or non-school-knowledge subsets while the male-leaning surfaces stay male.

**What would soften it:** if the same sign and magnitude survive across invariant-item rescoring, alternate youth math surfaces, and process-aware scoring.

### Node 2 - Is there a real school-surface wedge?

**Question:** do grades, placement, and school-knowledge-heavy test surfaces encode something different from alternate math tests?

**Current read:** yes.

Why:

1. `HSLS` shows a persistent grade/test wedge within common highest-math ladders.
2. `NLSY97` shows school-linked transcript math and `Math Knowledge` moving differently from `PIAT Math`.
3. The current footprint is too systematic to treat grades and course-linked surfaces as clean nuisance controls.

**Current probability:** `0.60`.

**Decisive test:**

1. same-cohort wedge modeling in `NLSY97` with transcript payload, `PIAT Math`, and `CAT-ASVAB`
2. replication in `ELS:2002`
3. item-family split in `PISA 2018` between school-knowledge-heavy and applied items

**What would harden it:** if transcript GPA / course-ladder / school-knowledge surfaces keep tilting female while alternate test surfaces do not, after baseline math and ladder alignment.

**What would soften it:** if the wedge vanishes once transcript ladder, missingness, and test-format differences are aligned carefully.

### Node 3 - Is early-school emergence a separate mechanism?

**Question:** does some of the broad school-math divergence appear early enough that late-school transcript and track mechanisms cannot be the whole story?

**Current read:** probably yes.

Why:

1. Recent frontier literature reports near-zero entry gaps followed by rapid male-favoring divergence after school entry.
2. The local project already sees male-leaning broad Grade 4 math in `TIMSS`, which is too early to reduce to high-school transcript or advanced-track stories.
3. Without an early-school node, the current tree over-credits adolescent school-surface mechanisms.

**Current probability:** `0.60`.

**Decisive test:**

1. repeated-wave early-school math gaps in `ECLS-K`, `ECLS-K:2011`, and `NLSCYA`
2. entry-versus-follow-up gap decomposition by early grade
3. comparison of broad school-math growth with later school-surface and advanced-track splits

**What would harden it:** if near-zero or female-leaning entry surfaces move male within the first years of schooling across at least two early-school cohorts.

**What would soften it:** if early-school cohorts already show the later male pattern at entry, or if broad early-school math does not widen male after aligned scaling and weighting.

### Node 4 - Is advanced-track selection a separate mechanism?

**Question:** are broad-population school-math surfaces and advanced-track mathematics different causal objects?

**Current read:** yes.

Why:

1. `TIMSS Grade 8` broad math is near parity to slightly female-leaning.
2. `TIMSS Advanced` is male-leaning across domains and strongest on `reasoning`.
3. This is exactly the footprint expected if track entry and advanced-domain composition matter.

**Current probability:** `0.65` that track / advanced-selection is a distinct node, not just noise around one math gap.

**Decisive test:**

1. transcript-rich course-ladder models in `HSLS` and `ELS`
2. advanced-math participation and performance models conditional on earlier math
3. upper-tail and upper-track analyses kept separate from broad-population math

**What would harden it:** if broad-population and advanced-track surfaces keep diverging after prior-math adjustment.

**What would soften it:** if advanced-track male lean disappears once earlier math, course ladder, and selection are aligned.

### Node 5 - Does adult accumulation amplify rather than create the male numeracy pattern?

**Question:** is `PIAAC` adult numeracy mostly a continuation of earlier differences, or a post-school amplification?

**Current read:** amplification is likely, but not the sole origin.

Why:

1. `PIAAC` remains male-leaning across age, education, and occupation cells.
2. The gap is already visible before adulthood in some youth surfaces.
3. But adult field, occupation, and use-dependent accumulation plausibly increase the adult magnitude.

**Current probability:** `0.60` that adult accumulation is an amplifier, not the main creator.

**Decisive test:**

1. younger-adult slices and field-of-study proxies
2. `PIAAC` log-file and process checks
3. transcript-to-adult follow-up datasets where available

**What would harden it:** if male numeracy is present in young adults and then grows with field or occupation intensity.

**What would soften it:** if the adult gap collapses once field, use, and process differences are aligned.

### Node 6 - Is there still evidence for a battery-invariant latent male `g` advantage?

**Question:** after the surface nodes are handled, is there a residual stable male general-ability advantage?

**Current read:** still open, but currently weak.

Why:

1. the project does see stable male-favoring spatial / mechanical surfaces
2. it does not see one stable sign across the broad quantitative and school-linked surfaces
3. the measurement layer is still too active to read the remaining disagreement as a clean `g` result

**Current probability:** `0.18` to `0.25`, depending on whether the target is "small male advantage on some highly `g`-loaded quantitative / spatial surfaces" or "battery-invariant general advantage"

**Decisive test:**

1. multi-battery measurement-invariant scoring
2. same-sign replication across balanced batteries
3. transcript-aware and process-aware models that leave the sign intact

**What would harden it:** a preregistered, measurement-invariant, transcript-aware, multi-battery result showing the same male-leaning residual across balanced cognitive surfaces.

**What would soften it further:** continued sign instability after invariant-item rescoring and cross-surface alignment.

## Best Statistics For The Next Tranche

The next tranche should stop chasing one preferred coefficient and instead use the smallest set of methods that can kill nodes.

### 1. Multi-group IRT / DIF / invariant-item rescoring

Use when item-level data exist.

Best targets:

1. `PISA 2018`
2. `ICAR`
3. `NLSY79` / `NLSY97` if the usable item layer can be recovered

Reason:

- this is the only clean way to separate latent-surface claims from item-family and measurement claims

### 2. Repeated-wave early-school growth models

Use:

1. `ECLS-K`
2. `ECLS-K:2011`
3. `NLSCYA`

Reason:

- this is the cleanest way to decide whether broad school-math divergence is already opening before transcript and advanced-track mechanisms dominate

### 3. Cross-surface same-cohort residual models

Use:

1. `NLSY97`: `PIAT Math`, `AR`, `MK`, transcript ladder, transcript GPA
2. `HSLS`: baseline math, later math, transcript math GPA, highest ladder
3. `ELS`: same design once staged

Reason:

- same-cohort surface disagreement is more diagnostic than comparing unrelated populations

### 4. Specification curves with frozen blocks

Always show:

1. unrestricted descriptive
2. same-sample base
3. design-only
4. mechanism block
5. bad-control stress test

Reason:

- this prevents attenuation theater and forces sample-composition movement into the open

### 5. Hierarchical cross-dataset model on surfaces, not one pooled gap

Model the gap as varying by:

1. age / school stage
2. domain family
3. item family
4. track status
5. adaptive vs non-adaptive design

Reason:

- the project no longer has one target parameter; it has a structured surface map

### 6. Upper-tail analysis kept separate from mean-gap analysis

Reason:

- upper-tail / advanced-track claims are not identified by the broad-population mean and should not be smuggled in through rhetoric

## The Highest-Leverage Next 5 Analyses

### 1. `PISA 2018` item-family and timing decomposition

**Why first:** it attacks Node 1 and Node 2 simultaneously on a large public school-age dataset with item and timing traces already local.

**Success condition:** female-tilting school-knowledge item families separate from applied / reasoning or time-sensitive families in a way that matches `NLSY97` + `TIMSS`.

### 2. Early-school emergence pass in `ECLS-K`, `ECLS-K:2011`, and `NLSCYA`

**Why second:** it decides whether the current tree is missing a real pre-transcript mechanism instead of overloading adolescent school-surface stories.

**Success condition:** broad school-math gaps are near zero at entry and move male early, or else the early-school node weakens materially.

### 3. Deep `NLSY97` transcript payload pass

**Why third:** it attacks Node 2 directly inside the cohort that generated the anomaly after the early-school node is made explicit.

**Success condition:** transcript-ladder and transcript-grade surfaces diverge from `PIAT Math` and `AR` in a way that keeps the female signal localized.

### 4. `ELS:2002` replication of the grade-test-course wedge

**Why fourth:** it decides whether `HSLS` is a one-off or a real U.S. school-pipeline footprint.

**Success condition:** the wedge replicates with similar sign geometry.

### 5. `PISA` / `TIMSS` broad versus upper-tail split

**Why fifth:** it attacks Node 4 and Node 6 separately instead of letting them blur together.

**Success condition:** broad-population school surfaces and upper-tail / advanced surfaces keep behaving as different objects.

## Decision Rule

If the next tranche shows all three of the following:

1. invariant-item or alternate-surface rescoring weakens the female-looking `NLSY97` signal
2. school-linked and school-knowledge-heavy surfaces keep diverging from alternate test surfaces
3. advanced-track and upper-tail math remain separable from broad school math

then the decisive causal tree is:

1. there is no single stable "sex gap in IQ" object here
2. the active disagreement is mainly between measurement surfaces and school-pipeline surfaces
3. early-school emergence is a real upstream node, not a late-school afterthought
4. any remaining male latent advantage claim must be made narrowly, not globally

If those conditions fail, then the tree has to be revised upward toward a more stable latent-domain story.

## Operational Bottom Line

The project is already past the point where another generic regression on a composite helps.

The shortest honest read is:

1. `g` may still be useful as a summary, but it is not the decisive object for this dispute yet
2. the decisive frontier is whether the `NLSY97` / `HSLS` / `TIMSS` female-tilting school-knowledge surfaces are measurement-school artifacts, real schooling-linked skill differences, or both
3. that frontier now includes an explicit early-school emergence branch, not just transcript and track branches
4. the right way to resolve that is item-family, repeated-wave early-school, transcript, and track-aware analysis, not one more pooled mean gap
