### Priority 1 — Mariel-boatlift wage disconfirmers (corpus's #1 blind spot)

**Papers fetched (full text):**
- Clemens & Hunt (2019, ILR Review), "The Labor Market Effects of Refugee Waves: Reconciling
  Conflicting Results." NBER WP w23433 (May 2017, rev. Jul 2017), 144 cites.
  [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w23433] **OA — full text in corpus.** **Grade: high**
  (top-field journal ILR Review; methods-replication paper; this is the *adjudicator*, not a partisan).
- Borjas (2017, ILR Review), "The Wage Impact of the Marielitos: A Reappraisal." NBER WP w21588
  (Sep 2015), 163 cites. [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.3386/w21588] **OA — full text in corpus.**
  **Grade: high** (Harvard; top journal; the spine the corpus leans on).
- Peri & Yasenov (2019, J. Human Resources), "The Labor Market Effects of a Refugee Wave:
  Applying the Synthetic Control Method to the Mariel Boatlift." DOI 10.3368/jhr.54.2.0217.8561R1,
  70 cites. [SOURCE: https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/161228/1/dp10605.pdf] **metadata
  saved; full text not yet fetched (econstor + NBER mirrors failed this session — [GAP], see below).**
- BONUS surfaced: Borjas (2019, ILR Review), "The Wage Impact of the Marielitos: The Role of Race."
  DOI 10.1177/0019793919825753, 16 cites — Borjas's *direct rebuttal* to the Clemens-Hunt black-share
  critique. This is the crux of the exchange; metadata captured, body [GAP].

**The exact disagreement (extracted from full text of CH + Borjas):**

Borjas's finding [SOURCE: w21588]: wage of low-skill (high-school-dropout) Miami workers dropped
**"dramatically, by 10 to 30 percent"** after Mariel, implying a wage elasticity w.r.t. labor supply
of **−0.5 to −1.5**. To get this, Borjas restricted to a *highly specific* subsample: **men only,
non-Hispanic only, prime-age 25–59, less than high school.**

Clemens-Hunt's reconciliation [SOURCE: w23433] — the disagreement is **entirely about subsample
selection + a survey-composition artifact**, with exact numbers:
1. **91% of observations discarded.** Borjas's four restrictions throw out women, Hispanics,
   under-25/over-59, and exactly-high-school workers — discarding 91% of Miami's low-skill workers
   in the peak-effect window.
2. **Sample collapses to ~17 obs/year.** The restricted Miami subsample averaged **17 observations
   per year** in the March CPS over 1983–1987 (annual N: 1981=18, 1982=20, 1983=27, 1984=18,
   1985=16, 1986=15, 1987=16).
3. **Black share of the subsample jumps 55 pp — the artifact.** In the March CPS the black fraction
   of Borjas's Miami subsample rose from **0.363 (1979) → 0.910 (1985)**, a 55 pp shift, driven by
   (a) the ~15,000 black Haitian refugees who arrived 1980 (≈6,150 male; +up to 36% to the
   subpopulation) and (b) a **March-1981 CPS sampling-frame change** that improved coverage of
   low-income black males. Because black workers earned much less at the same education (log wage
   gap **−0.487** in Miami), the rising sampled-black fraction *mechanically* produces a spurious
   wage decline. Adjusting for a black indicator attenuates Borjas's effect to insignificance.
4. **Falsification test.** Using "years of education" (fixed for adults) as the outcome, the boatlift
   spuriously "caused" a **−0.444-year** drop in schooling (1984–86) — proving negative compositional
   selection contaminates the subsample.

**Bottom line (CH):** the dramatic decline is an **artifact**, not a real effect. With the sample
broadened to all low-skill (HS-or-less) the wage effect is **"indistinguishable from zero"**; some
broadenings (men, Hispanics, prime-age) show a **+10–30% RISE** relative to controls. Even within
Borjas's own subpopulation the data are "compatible with a modest fall of roughly **−2% to −8%**"
*or* with zero. [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]: the headline "10–30% wage harm" survives only under one narrow
demographic knife-cut; the field-replication verdict is ≈zero-to-modest.

**Borjas's rebuttal (2019 "Role of Race", abstract only):** concedes the black-share rise but argues
it does not overturn the result once race is modeled correctly — i.e., he reads the same composition
fact as a covariate to control, not a fatal artifact. [GAP — body not fetched; represent as the live
counter, not settled.] Net: the profession has NOT fully closed this; but the *weight* of replication
(CH + Peri-Yasenov synthetic control, below) sits against the large-harm reading.

**Which corpus claim this updates:** the **Borjas low-skill-wage-harm spine.** The corpus treats
Mariel as supporting a sizeable negative wage effect of low-skill supply shocks. The cleanest
replication evidence says that specific Mariel magnitude is **fragile-to-artifactual**; the honest
representation is "contested, with the large effect resting on a 17-obs/yr subsample contaminated by
a 55 pp composition shift." This does NOT refute the broader CES/Borjas national wage framework — it
removes Mariel as a *strong* data point for large low-skill harm.

[GAP] Peri-Yasenov (2019 JHR) synthetic-control body not fetched (econstor/NBER PDF mirrors both
failed this session). Abstract-confirmed direction [SOURCE: S2 abstract, DOI 10.3368/jhr.54.2.0217.8561R1]:
synthetic-control Miami (control = cities best matching pre-boatlift trends) finds **no significant
negative wage or employment effect** on low-skill workers — a second, independent-method
disconfirmer aligned with CH. Suggested re-fetch: NBER w23330 PDF or JHR via library.

### Priority 4 — Cato / Nowrasteh Texas CONVICTION-rate gray literature

Nowrasteh/Cato is the most-cited **conviction**-based series — a higher bar than arrest, directly
answering "but arrests ≠ guilt." All use Texas DPS data (Texas is the only state recording immigration
status at booking AND conviction). Author: Alex Nowrasteh (Cato). **Grade: medium** (think-tank /

data is the same primary source the peer-reviewed PNAS study uses; the conviction counts are
checkable and have not been contested on the data, only on interpretation; treat the numbers as
reliable, the framing as one side).

The flagship 10-year (2013–2022) result: homicide conviction rate **2.2 (illegal) vs 3.0 (native)

convictions. Note: in 2022 alone the illegal-immigrant homicide rate rose to 3.1/100k (author flags

**Which corpus claim this updates:** the **crime-cost prior**, on the conviction margin. The corpus
can now say the immigrant-low-crime finding survives the move from arrests to **convictions** (the
stronger evidentiary bar), across 4 independent Cato vintages spanning 2015–2022, all pointing the
same direction. This *strengthens and triangulates* the existing Light/Gunadi arrest-based finding;
it does not overturn anything in the corpus — it closes the "arrests overcount, what about guilt?"
objection. [FRAMING-SENSITIVE] Cato is an advocacy source; cite the TX DPS conviction COUNTS as the
hard datum, attribute the rate framing to Cato.

### Priority 2 — Dustmann & Frattini (2014): UK EEA-immigrants net-positive (disconfirms US cost-skew)

**Paper:** Dustmann & Frattini (2014), "The Fiscal Effects of Immigration to the UK," *Economic
Journal* 124(580):F593–F643, Nov 2014. **DOI 10.1111/ecoj.12181** (verified — note: NOT .12180; the
gap memo's hint was right on the paper, DOI confirmed at primary). 246+ cites for the related survey;
this is the landmark UK fiscal paper. [SOURCE: https://www.cream-migration.org/files/FiscalEJ.pdf
(author's CReAM copy, full text OA)] [SOURCE: https://air.unimi.it/handle/2434/243972 (journal
record)]. **Grade: high** (top-5 field journal; UCL/CReAM; extensive published sensitivity analysis).

**Exact findings** [SOURCE: FiscalEJ.pdf / UCL press release / CEPR VoxEU column]:
- **Recent immigrants (arrived since 2000), 2001–2011:** net fiscal contribution POSITIVE for every
  origin group. Recent EEA immigrants paid in **34% more than they took out** (net +£22.1bn, 2011£);
  recent A10 (2004-accession) +£5bn; other recent EEA +£15bn; recent non-EEA +£2.9bn (+~2%). Total
  recent-immigrant net contribution ≈ **+£25bn** over a decade in which the UK ran deficits.
- **Natives, same 2001–2011 window:** net contribution NEGATIVE — payments only **89%** of what they
  received (−£624.1bn). So recent immigrants were net contributors precisely when natives were not.
- **All immigrants resident 1995–2011 (incl. older cohorts):** EEA contributed **~10% more than
  natives** in relative terms (net +£8.8bn); non-EEA ~9% lower (negative, like natives, driven partly
  by having more children + UK-side allocation of their education cost — a conservative choice that
  biases non-EEA DOWN).

**Which corpus claim this updates:** the **NAS US cost-skew** (low-skill/non-Western cohorts net-
negative). D&F is the strongest *positive-NPV* disconfirmer — but [FRAMING-SENSITIVE] the transfer is
**conditional**: the positive result is concentrated in EEA (largely high-participation, EU-internal)
migrants, and even D&F find non-EEA ≈ native ≈ negative. So D&F does NOT say "all immigration is
fiscally positive"; it says the *selection + labor-force-participation composition* drives the sign,
and a high-participation cohort flips positive even in a welfare state running deficits. The honest
corpus update: the US NAS net-negative finding is **regime- and composition-specific**, not a law of
low-skill immigration; a differently-composed inflow (higher participation, EU-style) is net-positive.
Pair with Hansen-Schultz-Nielsen-Tranæs (DK, corpus #10) for the *opposite* boundary case (generous
welfare + non-Western low-skill → net-negative even with descendants). The two bracket the answer:
**fiscal sign = f(welfare generosity × cohort participation/skill), not a constant.**

[NOTE] Frattini's *current* EU work surfaced and is a stronger modern companion: Fiorio, Frattini,
Riganti & Christl (2023), "Migration and public finances in the EU," *Int. Tax & Public Finance*,
DOI 10.1007/s10797-023-09787-9 [SOURCE: https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s10797-023-09787-9.pdf,
OA] — comprehensive net-fiscal-contribution evidence across EU states incl. indirect taxes + in-kind
benefits. [GAP] body not read this session; flagged for corpus addition (Priority-2 modern extension).

### Priority 3 — Current US dynamic / GE fiscal-NPV (the cost-skew's hardest disconfirmers)

Two distinct disconfirmers landed, plus the live policy projection:

**3a. Colas & Sachs (2024) — "The Indirect Fiscal Benefits of Low-Skilled Immigration."**
CORRECTION to gap memo: this is now **PUBLISHED**, *American Economic Journal: Economic Policy*
16(2):515–550, May 2024, **DOI 10.1257/pol.20220176** (WP was Minneapolis Fed IWP-38 / CEPR DP15325 /
CESifo 8604). [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20220176] [SOURCE WP full text:
https://epub.ub.uni-muenchen.de/93967/1/352.pdf]. **Grade: high** (top field journal, AEJ:Policy;
closed-form GE model + US tax-transfer quantification; replication package).
- **Central result:** one average low-skilled immigrant generates an **INDIRECT fiscal benefit of
  ~$750/yr** (published preferred estimate; WP range **$770–$2,100/yr** depending on the high/low-skill
  elasticity of substitution and labor-supply margins). The $753 figure ≈ **30% of the median
  low-skilled worker's annual federal tax payment.**
- **Mechanism:** low-skill immigrants raise the relative wage of (complementary) native high-skill
  workers and shift native labor supply → natives pay MORE tax. This is a pure general-equilibrium
  channel the static NAS per-capita accounting **omits by construction.**
- **The kill-shot vs NAS:** the authors state the indirect benefit is "of the same order of magnitude
  as the direct fiscal [cost] and of the opposite sign" — so adding it "turns the total fiscal effect
  from a fiscal burden to a fiscal surplus" in the more optimistic NAS scenarios, and "significantly
  reduces the burden" in the others. [FRAMING-SENSITIVE] sign-flip does NOT hold in *all* scenarios —
  honest read: the static (NAS) number is an **upper bound on the cost**, biased by ignoring GE
  complementarity; true net cost is materially smaller and can be positive.

**3b. Clemens (2021/2022) — "The Fiscal Effect of Immigration: Reducing Bias in Influential Estimates."**
SSRN DOI 10.2139/ssrn.3982027 (also IZA DP & related SSRN 4120116 on refugees). 7 cites.
[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.3982027] **Grade: medium-high** (Clemens is a leading migration
economist, CGD; SSRN/WP not yet top-journal-published as of capture; method is an accounting-bias
correction). [GAP — body not fetched: SSRN + IZA PDFs both blocked the fetcher this session;
abstract/title-level only]. Direction (from title + Colas-Sachs's framing of the same literature):
identifies that influential fiscal estimates (incl. NAS-style) understate immigrants' net contribution
through accounting choices — assignment of pure-public-good costs (defense, debt interest) at AVERAGE
rather than marginal cost, and treatment of descendants. **Represent as: [UNVERIFIED magnitude] —
direction is "NAS net cost is biased downward (too negative)"; fetch the body before quoting any
dollar correction.** Re-fetch target: SSRN PDF via institutional access or the IZA DP number.

**3c. CBO (2024) — the LIVE policy projection on the 2021–2026 surge.**
"Effects of the Immigration Surge on the Federal Budget and the Economy," CBO Publication **60165**,
July 2024 (companion 60569; summarized in the June-2024 Outlook Box 1-1, pub 60039). [SOURCE:
https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60165]. **Grade: high** (CBO = nonpartisan official scorekeeper;
the single most policy-relevant number in this whole memo). NOT a journal paper — primary government
projection.
- **Headline:** the 2021–2026 immigration surge (net +8.7M "other foreign nationals" above the
  200k/yr baseline) **LOWERS federal deficits by $0.9 trillion over 2024–2034**, on net.
- Decomposition: **+$1.2T federal revenues** (mostly income + payroll tax from surge immigrants),
  vs **+$0.3T** higher mandatory spending + net interest. Of the revenue, ~$387bn comes from
  *economy-wide* effects (productivity, higher rates) and ~$285bn of the net deficit reduction is
  the GE/economic channel — echoing Colas-Sachs's indirect-effect logic at national-accounts scale.
- Also: surge boosts nominal GDP by **+$8.9T cumulative (2024–2034)**, +3.2% in 2034.
- [FRAMING-SENSITIVE] This is FEDERAL only — CBO explicitly excludes **state & local** budgets, where
  the NAS finds the cost concentrates (education). So CBO $0.9T-positive and NAS state-level-negative
  are NOT contradictory; they measure different ledgers. The honest synthesis: **federal net-positive
  (strongly), state/local net-negative (education-driven), aggregate depends on the federalism split.**

**Which corpus claim this updates:** the **NAS cost-skew** as the corpus's fiscal spine. Three
independent disconfirmers (a published GE model, a bias-correction WP, and the official CBO score) all
push the same direction: the static NAS per-capita net-cost is an **upper bound** that omits (i) GE
complementarity raising native tax (Colas-Sachs ~$750/immigrant/yr), (ii) public-good cost-assignment
bias (Clemens), and (iii) the realized federal revenue/GDP boost (CBO −$0.9T deficit). The corpus
should down-weight any "low-skill immigration is a clear fiscal drain" framing to "**static federal+
state net cost is modest-to-negative for low-skill cohorts in the NAS accounting, but turns
neutral-to-positive at the federal level once GE effects and the official CBO score are included;
the residual net-negative is state/local education, a federalism-allocation artifact.**"

## Confidence-ladder updates (claim → suggested direction/magnitude)

Format: **corpus claim** → suggested change, with the disconfirmer driving it. "Direction" = which way
to move the credence; these are *suggestions for the parent*, not unilateral edits.

1. **"The Mariel boatlift shows low-skill immigration causes a large (10–30%) wage decline for
   competing low-skill natives" (Borjas wage spine).**
   → **DOWNGRADE hard, from supporting evidence to CONTESTED-likely-artifact.** The 10–30% figure
   survives only under a 4-way demographic knife-cut (non-Hispanic men 25–59, dropouts) that leaves
   ~17 obs/yr and rides a 55 pp black-share composition shift; the field-replication verdict (Clemens-
   Hunt) is ≈zero-to-modest (−2% to −8% at most, possibly zero), corroborated by Peri-Yasenov
   synthetic control (~null). Keep Borjas's *rebuttal* (2019 Role of Race) cited as the live
   counter — do not declare the question closed, but the WEIGHT moved against the large effect.
   Magnitude of move: large. [Driver: Clemens-Hunt 2019 ILR; Peri-Yasenov 2019 JHR.]

2. **"Low-skill immigration is a net fiscal drain" (NAS cost-skew, federal+state pooled).**
   → **DOWNGRADE / heavily qualify.** Add the GE channel: Colas-Sachs (AEJ:Policy 2024) indirect
   benefit ~$750/immigrant/yr (range $770–$2,100) can *flip the sign* in optimistic NAS scenarios and
   materially shrinks the cost in all. The static NAS number is an **upper bound on cost**, not a
   point estimate. Magnitude: moderate-large. [Driver: Colas-Sachs 2024; Clemens 2021 bias-correction
   (direction only, magnitude UNVERIFIED).]

3. **(New claim to ADD) "The realized 2021–2026 US immigration surge is fiscally positive at the
   federal level."**
   → **ADD at HIGH confidence (federal ledger).** CBO 2024 (pub 60165): −$0.9T deficit over 2024–2034,
   +$1.2T revenue, +$8.9T GDP. Caveat in the same node: FEDERAL only; state/local (education) excluded
   and is where NAS finds the cost. Magnitude: this is the strongest single fiscal datum in the corpus.
   [Driver: CBO Publication 60165, July 2024.]

4. **(New claim to ADD) "UK EEA immigrants are net fiscal contributors; the fiscal sign is
   composition- and regime-dependent, not a constant."**
   → **ADD at HIGH confidence.** Dustmann-Frattini 2014 (EJ): recent EEA +34% / +£22.1bn while natives
   were −11% over 2001–2011. Bracket with the DK case (Hansen et al., corpus #10, opposite sign for
   non-Western low-skill in a generous welfare state). The pair establishes **fiscal sign =
   f(welfare generosity × cohort participation/skill)** — retire any framing that treats low-skill
   immigration as fiscally negative *by nature*. Magnitude: moderate (it reframes rather than refutes).
   [Driver: Dustmann-Frattini 2014 EJ; pairs with corpus #10.]

5. **"Immigrants (incl. undocumented) have lower crime than natives" (crime-cost prior).**
   → **UPGRADE / strengthen on the conviction margin.** Previously arrest-based (Light, Gunadi);
