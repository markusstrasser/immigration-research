# Audit of the pro-immigration evidence base behind the Clark low-skill survey

Date: 2026-04-10

## Question

Are the papers behind the stronger `agree` responses on the Clark Center low-skilled immigration survey factually wrong?

Short answer:

No clear core paper I checked is factually wrong on its own stated object. The recurring problem is narrower scope than the survey claim, not obvious falsity.

The scientific weakness is usually this move:

1. identify a real positive channel
2. estimate that channel cleanly
3. talk as if that settles the broader question of whether the average U.S. citizen is better off overall

That last step is usually not supported by the paper itself.

## Survey-level problem

On the Clark page, many of the strongest `agree` panelists do not cite a specific paper at all. The most confident positive votes include Hilary Hoynes (`10`), and several `8` confidence `agree` votes such as Daron Acemoglu, David Autor, Janet Currie, Oliver Hart, Maurice Obstfeld, and Nancy Stokey. But the page mostly gives either no paper justification or a short theoretical comment. [SOURCE: https://kentclarkcenter.org/surveys/low-skilled-immigrants/]

That means the auditable object is not a neat set of `their own full papers`. It is the evidence base that this camp usually relies on: wage effects, complementarity, service-price effects, safety-net participation, and firm adjustment.

## Paper audits

### 1. David Card, `Immigration and Inequality` (2009)

What it studies:

1. city-level immigration and wage inequality among natives
2. substitutability between immigrants and natives in labor markets

What it actually claims:

1. the paper is an `overview and synthesis` of research on immigration and wage inequality using city comparisons
2. Card concludes immigration has had `very small impacts on wage inequality among natives`

Evidence:

1. the paper describes itself as an overview of `immigration and wage inequality` [SOURCE: https://davidcard.berkeley.edu/papers/immigration-and-inequality.pdf]
2. Card writes that immigration has had `very small impacts on wage inequality among natives` [SOURCE: https://davidcard.berkeley.edu/papers/immigration-and-inequality.pdf]
3. searches of the paper do not surface fiscal, housing, or congestion accounting [SOURCE: https://davidcard.berkeley.edu/papers/immigration-and-inequality.pdf]

Verdict:

1. `Not factually wrong`
2. `Scope-limited`

Why:

This is a labor-market inequality paper. If someone uses it to answer school costs, housing pressure, congestion, or full fiscal incidence, that overreach is outside the paper.

### 2. Cortes and Tessada, `Low-Skilled Immigration and the Labor Supply of Highly Skilled Women` (2011)

What it studies:

1. whether low-skilled immigration changes the time use and labor supply of high-skilled women
2. household-service substitution and labor supply at the top of the wage distribution

What it actually claims:

1. low-skilled immigration raises market work and long-hours work for high-skilled women
2. high-skilled women reduce household work and increase spending on housekeeping services

Evidence:

1. the abstract says the paper studies whether low-skilled immigration led high-skilled women to change time-use decisions [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fapp.3.3.88]
2. the paper finds increased hours of market work for women at the top quartile of the wage distribution [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fapp.3.3.88]
3. searches of the article page do not surface fiscal, school, housing, or congestion analysis [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fapp.3.3.88]

Verdict:

1. `Not factually wrong`
2. `Distribution-selective and scope-limited`

Why:

This is a clean positive channel for one beneficiary group. It is not a social-welfare ledger.

### 3. Bitler and Hoynes, `Immigrants, Welfare Reform, and the U.S. Safety Net` (2011)

What it studies:

1. immigrant eligibility changes after 1996 welfare reform
2. program participation, income, and poverty among immigrants and their children

What it actually claims:

1. it `comprehensively examine[s] the status of the U.S. safety net for immigrants and their family members`
2. it uses CPS data on participation, income, and poverty

Evidence:

1. the NBER abstract says the paper documents policy changes and analyzes participation, income, and poverty using CPS 1995-2010 [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w17667]
2. the object is clearly safety-net status, not total fiscal incidence [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w17667]
3. searches of the NBER page do not surface housing, school, or congestion channels [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w17667]

Verdict:

1. `Not factually wrong`
2. `Relevant to benefit use, not a net fiscal verdict`

Why:

This paper helps on one piece of the ledger. It does not tell you whether low-skill immigration is net positive or negative for the average citizen.

### 4. Clemens and Lewis, `The Effect of Low-Skill Immigration Restrictions on US Firms and Workers: Evidence from a Randomized Lottery` (2022, rev. 2024)

What it studies:

1. H-2B quota randomization at the firm level
2. output, investment, profit, and native employment effects when firms get access to low-skill immigrant labor

What it actually claims:

1. extra low-skill immigrant labor raises production, investment, and profits at the marginal firm
2. native employment is zero or positive overall and positive in rural areas

Evidence:

1. the abstract states that firms authorized to employ more low-skill immigrants increase production, investment, and profits [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w30589/w30589.pdf]
2. the abstract states native employment is `zero or positive overall, and positive in rural areas` [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w30589/w30589.pdf]
3. the paper is explicitly about `the marginal impact of the quota on firms entering this lottery` [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w30589/w30589.pdf]
4. searches do not surface congestion and only incidental references to schooling; this is not a local-public-cost paper [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w30589/w30589.pdf]

Verdict:

1. `Not factually wrong`
2. `High internal validity, narrow external validity`

Why:

This is one of the stronger causal papers, but it is a firm-level H-2B margin paper. It does not answer the whole local-fiscal question.

### 5. Colas and Sachs, `The Indirect Fiscal Benefits of Low-Skilled Immigration` (2024)

What it studies:

1. indirect fiscal effects through resident wage and labor-supply responses
2. how those indirect effects compare with direct fiscal effects from NAS-style accounting

What it actually claims:

1. NAS-style accounting focuses on direct fiscal effects and omits indirect general-equilibrium fiscal effects
2. one average low-skilled immigrant generates a positive indirect fiscal effect under their framework

Evidence:

1. the paper states the NAS report `focuses on direct fiscal effects` and `abstracts from indirect fiscal effects` [SOURCE: https://rationality-and-competition.de/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/352.pdf]
2. the paper quantifies positive indirect fiscal effects and discusses how they relate to direct fiscal effects [SOURCE: https://rationality-and-competition.de/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/352.pdf]
3. the paper explicitly notes additional channels like housing rents and resident migration could be analyzed in a spatial-equilibrium model [SOURCE: https://rationality-and-competition.de/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/352.pdf]
4. the paper conditions taxes on family status, age, location, and ages of children, which is stronger than a pure individual-only model [SOURCE: https://rationality-and-competition.de/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/352.pdf]

Verdict:

1. `Not factually wrong`
2. `Still incomplete as a total local-cost account`

Why:

This paper is often described too aggressively in public debate. It adds a real omitted channel, but it does not finish the ledger. The authors themselves acknowledge omitted spatial and housing channels.

## What I do think is wrong

### Wrong use of the evidence

1. Using wage papers as if they answer public-finance questions
2. Using service-price papers as if they answer average-citizen welfare
3. Using firm-level H-2B results as if they generalize mechanically to all low-skill immigration
4. Using indirect-fiscal papers as if they already incorporate the full local housing, schooling, and congestion ledger

### Not clearly wrong

1. The core empirical findings inside the papers above
2. The existence of real complementarities and service-price effects
3. The claim that some natives and firms benefit
4. The claim that some labor-market papers find small or null average wage effects for many natives

## Bottom line

The strongest criticism is not `bad econometrics` or `fabricated facts`.

It is this:

The positive evidence base behind the Clark `agree` camp is mostly a stack of valid but partial papers. Those papers identify real gains on specific margins. They usually do not justify the broader proposition that the average U.S. citizen is better off after all local public costs, housing pressure, and family-linked fiscal burdens are counted.
