# Immigration economists keep answering the wrong question — 2026-04-22

**Purpose:** Short public-facing one-pager.  
**Use:** Forwardable summary of the strongest fair critique of the mainstream pro-immigration economics line after the repo's current evidence.

## The core problem

The slick economist case for immigration usually works by answering a **different question** than the one ordinary people are asking.

When people ask:

1. `Will this be good for the country I live in?`
2. `Will this help or hurt current residents?`
3. `What happens to housing, schools, wages, and local capacity?`

the economist often answers:

1. `Migrants gain a lot`
2. `World output rises`
3. `Aggregate GDP goes up`

Those are real effects.

They are also **not the same ledger**.

That is the central bait-and-switch. [SOURCE: research/immigration-claims-matrix-2026-04-11.md] [SOURCE: research/immigration-reasoning-evolution-2026-04-21.md]

## The seven standard moves

### 1) Ledger switching

They move back and forth between:

1. migrant welfare
2. world output
3. national aggregate GDP
4. GDP per person
5. incumbent local welfare

Then they speak as if one positive result settles them all.

It does not. [SOURCE: research/immigration-smith-decker-friedman-comparative-quantitative-audit-2026-04-11.md]

### 2) Upper-bound laundering

They cite stylized open-borders papers and then talk as if near-doubling world GDP is a realistic baseline.

It is not.

The strongest repo reading is:

1. large gains are plausible in principle
2. the near-doubling rhetoric is an upper-bound extrapolation
3. it depends on destination-country capacity staying largely intact

[SOURCE: research/immigration-open-borders-double-world-gdp-and-apartheid-audit-2026-04-21.md]

### 3) Marginal-to-mass extrapolation

Economists often have evidence that moderate inflows are absorbed without obvious collapse.

They do **not** therefore have evidence that:

1. concentrated surge inflows
2. weak housing response
3. shelter overload
4. school and court strain
5. multi-year backlash

will also be absorbed cleanly.

That is a different regime. [SOURCE: research/immigration-frontier-rethink-2026-04-22.md]

### 4) Capacity erasure

Housing, shelter, schools, courts, and administrative systems are not one small friction term.

They are separate bottlenecks.

The repo's current evidence points to `flow x capacity x composition x regime`, not a simple stock-share story. [SOURCE: research/immigration-capacity-frontier-2026-04-21.md] [SOURCE: research/immigration-receiver-failure-atlas-2026-04-22.md]

### 5) Denominator masking

If a city expands beds or emergency capacity in response to a surge, then a ratio like `PIT/HIC` can look stable even while the actual crisis worsens.

That is why the receiver work had to check absolute load, not just ratios.

`Chicago` is the cleanest case. [SOURCE: research/immigration-receiver-counterfactuals-2026-04-22.md]

### 6) Political-economy erasure

The economist often reduces politics to:

1. immigrant voting
2. maybe welfare-state demand

But the real channels also include:

1. native backlash
2. legitimacy effects
3. assignment rules
4. emergency procurement
5. right-to-shelter obligations

[SOURCE: research/immigration-threshold-causal-levers-2026-04-21.md] [SOURCE: research/full-spectrum-costs-unauthorized-immigration-research-memo.md]

### 7) Aggregate-output trump card

If aggregate GDP rises, they act as if the argument is basically over.

But the official U.S. evidence already blocks that move:

1. aggregate GDP can rise
2. GDP per person can fall
3. federal fiscal effects can be positive
4. state/local effects can be negative

[SOURCE: research/immigration-claims-matrix-2026-04-11.md]

## What the current evidence actually supports

The honest current position is:

1. migrant gains are real
2. some national aggregate gains are real
3. crude collapse rhetoric is too strong
4. blanket economist optimism is also too strong
5. destination incidence depends on `flow x capacity x composition x regime`

[SOURCE: research/immigration-economist-rhetorical-failures-2026-04-22.md]

## The shortest fair summary

The economist case is not wrong because immigration has no gains.

It is wrong because it repeatedly uses a bounded efficiency argument to answer a different question:

`Is this good for incumbent residents once housing, local capacity, and political feedback are priced in?`

That question is not answered by:

1. place-premium gains
2. aggregate GDP
3. one-line “economists agree” survey rhetoric

## Best final line

**The economist case does not fail on first-order gains. It fails where it pretends those gains settle every ledger at once.**
