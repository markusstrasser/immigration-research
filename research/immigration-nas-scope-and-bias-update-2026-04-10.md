# NAS benchmark and pro-paper scope update

Date: 2026-04-10

## Purpose

This note updates the immigration docs on two points that were getting blurred:

1. whether the National Academies (`NAS`) lifetime fiscal benchmark is "biased"
2. whether papers saying low-skill immigration is good are actually wrong

## Bottom line

1. `NAS` is **not junk** and not obviously unusable.
2. `NAS` is **highly assumption-sensitive**, especially on public-goods assignment and descendant treatment. [A1/A2]
3. Many pro–low-skill-immigration papers are **not wrong on their own channel**.
4. Many of them are **wrong if used as a full answer** to the broader taxpayer / local-capacity / all-government welfare question. [A2/B1]

## What NAS is and is not

### What survives

`NAS` still looks useful as:

1. a transparent lifetime fiscal scaffold
2. a way to separate `individual` and `descendants`
3. a way to separate federal from state/local incidence
4. a benchmark showing that education is a major driver of fiscal impact

Primary source:
1. [The Economic and Fiscal Consequences of Immigration](https://d279m997dpfwgl.cloudfront.net/wp/2016/09/0922_immigrant-economics-full-report.pdf) [A1]

### What does not survive cleanly

`NAS` should **not** be treated as:

1. the one true lifetime number
2. robust to all reasonable public-goods assumptions
3. a full account of indirect general-equilibrium fiscal effects
4. a complete answer to "impact on natives" in every welfare sense

## Why NAS is assumption-sensitive

The largest sensitivity is the public-goods assumption.

Later summaries of the report explicitly note that immigration can look more negative when immigrants are assigned average costs of public goods such as defense and interest on the debt, and less negative when marginal-cost logic is used instead. [A2]

Source:
1. [Orrenius, 2017, Dallas Fed working paper](https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704) [A2]

A second important sensitivity is descendant treatment.

The benchmark totals already include descendants in a separate component. So the large negative numbers are not adult-only numbers. That is an intended model feature, not the same thing as the adult-linked child double-count bug we found and corrected in our local ACS work. [A1]

Primary source:
1. [NAS Table 8-13 / surrounding discussion](https://d279m997dpfwgl.cloudfront.net/wp/2016/09/0922_immigrant-economics-full-report.pdf) [A1]

## Strongest critique of NAS

The strongest critique is not that `NAS` fabricated the result.

The strongest critique is that the benchmark can be biased in direction and magnitude by omitting or underweighting some channels, especially:

1. indirect fiscal effects through labor markets and capital taxation
2. the choice of average-cost vs marginal-cost public-goods allocation
3. the mismatch between broad national accounting and local-incidence burdens

Two important critiques:

1. `Clemens` argues influential fiscal estimates understate immigrant fiscal contributions by omitting capital-tax channels generated by immigrant labor. [A2]
   Source: [The Fiscal Effect of Immigration: Reducing Bias in Influential Estimates](https://www.cgdev.org/publication/fiscal-effect-immigration-reducing-bias-influential-estimates)
2. `Colas and Sachs` estimate indirect fiscal benefits from low-skilled immigration through wage and labor-supply effects. [A2]
   Source: [Indirect Fiscal Benefits paper](https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/233504/1/1755432666.pdf)

These critiques do **not** make `NAS` useless. They mean `NAS` is a benchmark with strong framing assumptions, not neutral ground truth.

## Are the pro papers wrong?

Usually: **not on their own narrow claim**.

Often: **yes, if used as a total answer**.

### Valid partial claims

Many pro papers identify real channels, such as:

1. complementarity with some native workers
2. cheaper labor-intensive services
3. employer productivity gains
4. indirect fiscal gains
5. better fiscal performance than a comparable low-skill native

Those are real channels and should not be dismissed as fake. [A2/B1]

### Invalid total claim

The mistake is to use those channel papers to imply:

1. low-skill immigration is net good for the average native taxpayer
2. low-skill immigration is clearly positive after local schooling, housing, and capacity costs
3. the issue is settled by labor-market papers alone

That leap is often wrong. [A2/B1]

## Best current classification of the economist literature

### Mostly right, but narrow

These are often valid if interpreted as partial-channel arguments:

1. labor-market complementarity papers
2. consumer-price and service-cost papers
3. indirect-fiscal-effect papers
4. relative-to-comparable-native comparisons

### Wrong for the broader question

These become wrong when interpreted as answers to:

1. full lifetime taxpayer burden
2. all-government incidence
3. local public-capacity burden
4. descendant-inclusive fiscal effect
5. political backlash / legitimacy / `AGI-soon` transition effects

## Methodological safeguard for our own docs

Do **not** mechanically combine:

1. our corrected local household child-burden metrics
2. `NAS` descendant-inclusive lifetime totals

That would recreate double counting at the synthesis layer.

Correct wording:

1. `household-normalized local child burden`
2. `linked households containing at least one qualifying adult`
3. `NAS totals already include descendants by design`

## Updated project stance

The best current stance is:

1. `NAS` remains the main lifetime scaffold
2. our warehouse is the main heterogeneity and local-incidence correction layer
3. the strongest pro papers are usually valid partials, not omnibus verdicts
4. the strongest anti reading should attack assumptions and omitted channels, not pretend the benchmark is fabricated

## Practical implication

If we want to actually replace `NAS`, the next data/modeling targets remain:

1. `SIPP`
2. `Synthetic SIPP`
3. `MEPS`
4. `PSID`
5. `IRS SOI PUF`
6. eventually restricted linked data through `FSRDC`

That is the path from `assumption-sensitive benchmark` to `our own model`.
