# Dismantling "Streets of Gold" (Abramitzky & Boustan, 2022)

**Section of the consolidated dismantling memo.** Status: VERIFIED — primary checks complete (AER 2021 + PNAS Nexus pgae344 re-verified at primary by parent on integration; one attribution corrected — the Hispanic/Black non-convergence sentence is A&B *citing* Villarreal-Tamborini, while the Jamaican/Haitian/Mexican persistent-gap is in A&B's *own* AER data).

## Framing
Fairness is the weapon. The repo USES Abramitzky-Boustan's intergenerational-mobility research (AER 2021) and CITES it as a landmark. We GRANT their core finding (children of poor immigrants out-mobilize children of poor natives, then and now). We KILL the OVERCLAIMS in the book's "immigrants always succeed/assimilate" generalization. Per-claim: steelman → granted-or-overclaim → kill + name the exact move.

## The four headline claims of the book
[SOURCE: book jacket/synopsis, Hachette/Princeton/PNAS-Nexus; AER 2021 10.1257/aer.20191586]
1. **Upward mobility (THE CORE):** children of immigrants from nearly every sending country — *especially the children of poor immigrants* — out-earn the children of the US-born, and this held both in the Age of Mass Migration (1880s–1920s) AND today.
2. **Rapid assimilation:** groups accused of failing to assimilate (Mexicans now, the Irish/Italians then) actually assimilate *fast* — on names, language, residence, intermarriage.
3. **Economic benefit / demographic rescue:** immigration grows the economy in non-obvious ways and offsets the decline of an aging native population.
4. **Closing the door harms natives:** restriction hurts the economic prospects of the US-born it claims to protect (e.g., their Mexican-repatriation / Bracero-end natural experiments).

The book's evidentiary spine is the AER 2021 paper (Abramitzky, Boustan, Jácome, Pérez), built by linking millions of father→son pairs across Census waves and Ancestry.com genealogical records, measured on a **rank-rank intergenerational-mobility** metric.

## The analytical spine — which recurring move each overclaim is
(Same 6-move taxonomy as the consolidated economist-dismantling, `immigration-economist-dismantling-2026-06-25.md`.)
1. Coordinate/ledger-switching · 2. Upper-bound laundering · 3. Marginal-to-mass extrapolation · 4. Capacity erasure · 5. Denominator/metric masking · 6. Political-economy underspecification. Plus the instrument-level **classification confound** (entry 51).

## Per-claim analysis

### CLAIM 1 — Children of poor immigrants out-mobilize children of poor natives, then and now
**Steelman.** This is the book's strongest and best-identified result, and it is genuinely impressive: linked father→son data across *two centuries*, the upward-mobility advantage of immigrants' children is large (~for fathers at the 25th percentile, immigrants' sons reach a meaningfully higher adult rank than natives' sons), it holds for nearly every sending country including the supposedly "unassimilable" ones, and it is *stable* across the 1900s and the post-1965 era despite a total turnover in source countries and policy regime. The repo ADOPTS this (it anchors the 2nd-gen mobility expectation) — it is real, landmark, peer-reviewed (AER), and replicated in a 15-country follow-up.

**Granted.** GRANTED in full as stated. We do not relitigate the rank-rank upward-mobility finding. [SOURCE: 10.1257/aer.20191586]

**The kills are not against this finding — they are against what the BOOK does with it.** See Claims 1a–1d below: the metric-masking, the historical-validity stretch, the generational classification confound, and the selection mechanism. The finding survives; the *generalization* "immigrants always succeed" does not.

### CLAIM 1a — The rank-rank metric shows "mobility" while ABSOLUTE origin gaps can persist — **Move 5 (denominator/metric masking)**
**Steelman.** Rank-rank is the field-standard mobility metric (Chetty et al. use it); it correctly isolates *positional* movement and is robust to changing income distributions across a century — which is exactly why Abramitzky-Boustan need it to compare 1900 and 2000.

**Overclaim.** "Out-mobilize" is a statement about *rank change*, not about *closing the level gap*. A group can show high upward rank-mobility from a low origin and still sit below natives in absolute outcomes for generations; conversely the metric rewards starting low (more room to rise). The book's rhetoric ("streets of gold," "success") invites the reader to hear *level success*, while the evidence is *conditional rank ascent from the parental rank*. [INFERENCE — the metric is rank-based by construction; the gap between "rank mobility" and "absolute convergence" is a known property of rank-rank, not disputed]

**Kill + move.** *Denominator/metric masking* (Move 5): presenting a **relative-rank** result in the vocabulary of **absolute success**. The honest statement is narrower: "children of poor immigrants climb *more rank-positions* from their parents' low starting rank than children of equally-poor natives." That is true and notable. It does NOT establish that immigrant-origin populations reach parity in levels, nor that gaps don't persist — and segmented-assimilation's downward cases (Claim 2) live precisely in the level-and-identity space the rank metric averages over. [SOURCE: 10.1257/aer.20191586] [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]

### CLAIM 1b — Historical (Age of Mass Migration) evidence is doing work it cannot do for present policy — **Move 3 (marginal-to-mass / external validity)**
**Steelman.** The "then and now" parallelism is the book's signature contribution and is *partly* earned: they DO have post-1965 data, and the *rank-mobility* advantage really does replicate across eras. The point of the historical work is to puncture the *nostalgic myth* ("the old immigrants assimilated, today's don't") — and on that narrow target it lands: the old immigrants were *also* feared as unassimilable, and their *children* also rose. That is a fair and valuable correction.

**Overclaim.** The book's emotional and rhetorical center of gravity is the 1880–1920 European inflow, and the generalization "then and now" papers over absorption differences that are *structural, not attitudinal*: (i) **no welfare state** in 1900 (so the fiscal-burden question the repo's ladder 46–47 turns on literally could not arise — selection into work was forced); (ii) **open frontier + no zoning** (Move 4, capacity: housing/absorption was elastic in a way it is not in today's inelastic metros — repo's own `msa_fb_rent_panel`, ladder 50); (iii) **pre-civil-rights racial order** (the European ethnics eventually "became white" — a path structurally unavailable to many post-1965 arrivals; this is exactly Denton-Massey's racialization block); (iv) **composition** — the post-1965 inflow is differently skill- and origin-distributed. The *rank-mobility* statistic can replicate while the *policy-relevant* margins (fiscal, housing, racialized integration) differ entirely.

**Kill + move.** *Marginal-to-mass / capacity erasure* (Moves 3+4): the historical case establishes that 1900-era fears were overblown *for that inflow under those institutions*; it does NOT transfer to "therefore today's mass inflow assimilates equally well under a welfare state, zoning, and the modern racial order." The replication is of a *positional* statistic, not of the *absorption environment*. Naming it precisely: the book uses a real cross-era replication of one metric to license a much broader "immigration works the same then and now" conclusion that the *non-replicated* differences (welfare state, housing elasticity, racialization) directly undercut. [SOURCE: 10.1257/aer.20191586] [INFERENCE on institutional differences — uncontested historical facts] [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]

### CLAIM 1c — The success story rests on the 2nd generation being coded as "native"; the most-assimilated drop out of the sample — **the classification confound (entry 51) + ethnic attrition**
**Steelman.** Their core mobility result is *explicitly* a 1st-gen→2nd-gen (father→son) measurement, so it does not hide the 2nd generation — it *is about* the 2nd generation. And father→son linkage on objective records (not self-ID) is more robust than the self-ID generational tests the repo's crime side worries about.

**Overclaim / confound.** Two coding-level biases, both pushing measured immigrant progress UP:
- **(a) Ethnic attrition (Duncan-Trejo 2025, repo sociology frontier).** As immigrants' descendants assimilate and intermarry, their US-born grandchildren increasingly *stop self-identifying* as Mexican/Hispanic. Any study that tracks 3rd-gen+ by self-reported ethnicity loses the *most-assimilated* (highest-attaining) descendants from the ethnic sample → **understates** how far assimilation goes for high-intermarriage groups, OR (depending on design) makes the measured 2nd-gen look like the ceiling. Abramitzky-Boustan's father→son linkage sidesteps self-ID *for the pairs it links*, but the broader "assimilation" claims (names, identity, the Mexican case) inherit the attrition problem. [SOURCE: 10.2139/ssrn.5077491]
- **(b) 2nd-gen coded "native" (entry 51, operator-raised).** Birthright citizenship files the US-born children of immigrants under "native" in every nativity dataset. This is *direction-dependent*: for the FISCAL/mobility story it **credits the immigrant dynastic upside to "native,"** so the immigrant contribution is *understated* in cross-sectional native-vs-foreign comparisons — which the book exploits in the *opposite* direction from the crime literature. The pro-immigration case as a whole runs **first-gen framing for crime** (foreign-born look lowest-crime) and **dynastic framing for fiscal/mobility** (descendants look best) — coordinate-switching *at the generational level*. The book is on the dynastic side of that switch.

**Kill + move.** The book is internally consistent (it studies dynasties), but the *confound bites the book's headline rhetoric*: "immigrants succeed" is carried by the *children*, and the strongest version of that success (full assimilation) is precisely the part that **exits the ethnic sample** (attrition) or gets **recoded as native** (citizenship). Selection-on-the-dependent-variable risk: studying realized father→son *links* can over-weight families stable enough to be linked across records (linkage requires persistence/traceability), a known bias in the Ancestry linkage method. [SOURCE: 10.2139/ssrn.5077491] [INFERENCE on linkage-selection] [FRAMING-SENSITIVE — entry 51]

### CLAIM 1d — "Immigrants succeed" partly reflects SELECTION of who emigrates, not a universal newcomer property — **selection, not treatment**
**Steelman.** Abramitzky-Boustan partly *embrace* this: their AER 2021 explicitly attributes part of the advantage to immigrants **"choosing to settle in locations that offer better prospects for their children"** — i.e., they identify a *geographic-sorting* mechanism, which is a form of selection they measure rather than hide. This is intellectually honest and is one of the paper's genuine contributions.

**Overclaim.** But that same admission undercuts the *universalizing* book-level claim. If the advantage runs *partly* through (i) selection of who migrates (migration is costly and selective — emigrants are not a random draw of the origin population) and (ii) *where* they choose to settle, then "immigrants succeed" is not a property that would transfer to a *less-selected* or *differently-sorted* inflow (e.g., a mass humanitarian inflow, or one settling under modern housing constraints that foreclose the high-opportunity locations). The mechanism is *selection + sorting*, not a universal "newcomer drive." [SOURCE: 10.1257/aer.20191586]

**Kill + move.** *Marginal-to-mass extrapolation* (Move 3): a result driven by *selected* migrants *sorting* into *high-opportunity locations* cannot be generalized to "any immigration, at any scale, anywhere succeeds." The book's own identified mechanism is the boundary condition on its own generalization. [SOURCE: 10.1257/aer.20191586]

### CLAIM 2 — "Immigrants assimilate fastest" — engages the success cases; under-engages segmented assimilation's downward cases
**Steelman.** Broadly TRUE historically and a fair myth-puncture: on names, English, residence, and intermarriage, the feared groups (Irish, Italians then; Mexicans now) DO assimilate, often faster than the nostalgic myth admits. The repo's sociology frontier agrees the *catastrophic* downward-assimilation prediction is **the exception, not the rule** (Drouhot 2024, Kasinitz 2025, Waldinger-Feliciano) — so the book is on the right side of the modern consensus here.

**Overclaim.** The book tells the *success* distribution and under-weights the documented *downward* tail. Segmented-assimilation theory (Portes-Zhou; Haller-Portes-Lynch 2011, CILS) finds a real downwardly-assimilating MINORITY concentrated by family disadvantage + racialized reception context; Denton-Massey + Xiong-Pfeifer find spatial assimilation **blocked by racialization** for Afro-descended and some Southeast-Asian groups. Telling only the rising cases is **selection on the dependent variable** at the narrative level — the same error the disconfirmation principle exists to catch.

**Kill + move.** Not a data error (their averages are right) but a *framing* one (Move 5/narrative selection): an average upward trend coexists with a racialized downward tail, and a book titled *Streets of Gold: ...Immigrant Success* structurally foregrounds the former. The honest statement: assimilation is *real and usually upward, but stratified by race and reception context*, and the downward tail is concentrated, not absent. [SOURCE: sociology frontier — Haller-Portes-Lynch PMC3819936; Denton-Massey; Xiong-Pfeifer] [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]

### CLAIM 4 — "Closing the door harms natives" — directionally supported, but a federal-average that does not address the cost coordinates
**Steelman.** Their restriction natural experiments (Mexican repatriation in the 1930s; ending the Bracero program) are clean and find that restriction did NOT help — and sometimes hurt — native workers, consistent with the repo's ladder 44 (Mariel "harm" is a ~17-obs artifact) and the lump-of-labor refutation. The *aggregate/average* direction is defensible.

**Overclaim.** "Harms natives" is an *aggregate/average* verdict that ledger-switches away from the coordinates where immigration's costs concentrate — the *distributional* (which natives), the *fiscal-at-which-level* (state/local education is net-negative even where federal is positive, repo ladder 46), and the *housing-incidence* margins (repo ladder 50). "Closing the door harms natives on average" and "low-skill mass inflow burdens state/local budgets and inelastic housing markets" are both true on their respective coordinates; the book asserts the first as if it settled the second.

**Kill + move.** *Coordinate-switching* (Move 1) + *political-economy underspecification* (Move 6): an average-effect result presented as a complete policy verdict, with the distributional and fiscal-federalism losers unaddressed. [SOURCE: repo ladder 44/46/50] [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]

## A note on reception (instrument-bias check)
The book's *economics* reception is overwhelmingly favorable (e.g., the CEME/Kroencke review offers **no analytical critique**, only mild style notes; faster-please/Substack is a rave). [SOURCE: theceme.org/john-kroencke-streets-of-gold...] This matters two ways: (i) it confirms the granted core is *not* fringe — the mobility finding is mainstream-accepted; (ii) it means the critiques above come from *outside* the cheering section (the segmented-assimilation sociologists, the ethnic-attrition methodologists, the fiscal-federalism literature) — they are *complements/boundaries*, not refutations. The LLM instrument tilts *toward* this book's conclusions (`notes/llm-bias-caveat.md`), so the grants here are load-bearing and the critiques are specific analytical/scope errors.

## What survives (generous — most of it)
- **The headline mobility finding survives intact:** children of poor immigrants out-mobilize children of poor natives, then and now, on rank-rank, across nearly all sending countries. Landmark, peer-reviewed, repo-adopted, replicated in 15 countries. **Not dented.**
- **The myth-puncture survives:** yesterday's feared groups assimilated; the "old good / new bad" nostalgia is empirically wrong. **Survives.**
- **The lump-of-labor refutation survives:** restriction does not reliably help natives in the aggregate. **Survives (ladder 44).**
- **What does NOT survive is the generalization the *title* sells** — "immigrant SUCCESS" as a universal property. The evidence is: a *relative-rank* ascent (not absolute parity), of a *selected, self-sorting* inflow, measured on *dynasties* (with the most-assimilated attriting out or recoded native), whose cross-era replication is of one *statistic* and not of the *absorption environment* (welfare state, housing elasticity, racialization), and whose *downward racialized tail* is real but under-told. The book is right about what it measured and over-reaches on what it claims that measurement means for present mass-immigration policy.

## Primary-source verification — the authors' OWN admissions (PNAS Nexus 2024 review)
[SOURCE: Abramitzky, Boustan et al., PNAS Nexus 3(10):pgae344, 2024 — academic.oup.com/pnasnexus/article/3/10/pgae344/7795945]

Three admissions from the authors' own synthesis sharpen the kills above — each is *against interest* and so weighted high:

**(i) The magnitudes (rank-rank, confirming Claim 1a's metric reading).** Historical: sons of US-born fathers at the 25th percentile reached the **40th** percentile as adults; sons of Portuguese/Italian immigrant fathers reached nearly the **60th**. Modern (born ~1980): natives' sons from the 25th pct reached just above the **45th**; children of Hong Kong/China/India parents reached almost the **65th**. → The advantage is real and large, AND it is explicitly a *rank-position* statement from a low origin rank — confirming Claim 1a (this is positional ascent, not a claim of absolute-level parity for all groups).

**(ii) The downward tail is one A&B ACKNOWLEDGE (citing Villarreal-Tamborini) and then reframe away from — the reframe is itself the [FRAMING-SENSITIVE] move (Claim 2, now load-bearing).** Verbatim from the PNAS Nexus review (parent re-verified at primary, SemScholar mirror of pgae344): *"Recent work by **Villarreal and Tamborini** (26) shows that the children of some immigrant groups today—particularly second-generation Hispanic and Black immigrants—do not converge with the earnings of children of US-born parents in the modern data. Although these findings point to existing racial and ethnic inequalities that certainly exist in the United States, they **do not directly address the issue of intergenerational mobility** of immigrants…"* So the non-convergence sentence is A&B **citing** Villarreal-Tamborini, not reporting their own estimate — the precise attribution matters and the parent corrected it. **But the move that makes it load-bearing is still A&B's:** they acknowledge the cited non-convergence, reframe it as *"existing racial and ethnic inequalities"* rather than assimilation failure, and **pivot back to the *relative* (within-dynasty rank) measure** where the advantage survives. That pivot is precisely the **coordinate-switch** Claim 1a/2 names: when the *absolute-convergence* coordinate shows the downward, racialized tail, retreat to the *relative-rank* coordinate where the headline holds. And the downward tail is *independently in A&B's OWN AER 2021 data*: verbatim, *"There are only a few cases in which the gap remained above 5 log points by the second generation (Norwegians in 1910 and 1940; Finns in 1940; and **Jamaicans, Haitians, and Mexicans in the modern data**)"* — i.e. their own linked data carries the persistent-gap cases (Jamaican/Haitian/Mexican 2nd-gen) = Denton-Massey's racialization block, observed. The book foregrounds the relative-rank success; the absolute non-convergence is real, is cited-and-reframed in the review, and is present in their own AER data.

**(iii) Two limitation admissions that strengthen Claims 1b/1d/4.**
- *"the tax records used to study modern immigrant families do not contain undocumented immigrant parents."* → The MODERN half of the "then and now" parallel **excludes the undocumented entirely** — i.e., the present-day inflow most relevant to current fiscal/housing/enforcement debate (ladder 46/49/50) is the part the modern data cannot see. The "then and now" symmetry is between (selected historical Europeans) and (legally-recorded modern immigrants), not the full present inflow. Direct support for Claim 1b's external-validity kill, in their own words.
- *"[they] do not yet have access to household-level data to evaluate the role of geography in the modern period."* → The geographic-sorting mechanism — "immigrants choose locations with better prospects for their children," the AER 2021 mechanism and the backbone of Claim 1d — is documented HISTORICALLY but **not confirmed for the present**. So the *mechanism* generating the advantage is itself only historically established; assuming it operates identically today (under modern housing inelasticity that forecloses the high-opportunity locations, ladder 50) is exactly the marginal-to-mass/capacity stretch of Claims 1b/1d.

**Net of verification:** the kills are not speculative — A&B (a) acknowledge a racialized downward tail (citing Villarreal-Tamborini on Hispanic/Black 2nd-gen non-convergence) *and carry their own persistent-gap cases — Jamaican/Haitian/Mexican 2nd-gen — in their AER 2021 data*, (b) concede the modern tax data omit the undocumented, and (c) concede the causal *geography* mechanism is unmeasured in the present. The dismantling is "fair" in the strongest sense: it is built substantially from the authors' own admissions and own data, simply *not retreated-from to the relative-rank coordinate*.
