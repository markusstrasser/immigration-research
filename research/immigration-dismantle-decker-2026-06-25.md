## Decker's actual position (steelmanned in one paragraph)

Decker is sharper and more interesting than Noah Smith on this topic, and he deserves to be engaged at his strongest. His core move is **methodological, not empirical**: he argues that the entire anti-immigration economics rests on an unexamined modeling convenience — **constant returns to scale (CRS)** — and that CRS is "obvious nonsense" because trade itself logically implies increasing returns (IRS). From IRS via three channels (specialization, ideas, fixed costs), he concludes immigrants "must" make us richer. He layers on: high-skill immigration is unambiguously good (immigrant inventors → 23% of inventive output, ~36% with spillovers, Bernstein et al.); brain drain is "probably just totally wrong" (emigration options *raise* origin human capital — Abarcar-Theoharides Filipino nurses); the gains from open borders "would approximately double world GDP"; and the only *coherent* objection left is political (immigrants voting for bad policy). The steelman is real: he is correctly attacking a genuine weak point (naive CRS reasoning *does* bake in null long-run effects), and several of his empirics are repo-confirmed. **His failure is not the premises — it is the universal-quantifier conclusion ("must") and the ledger he silently swaps in to reach it.**

The asymmetry: **Decker is largely right about the MECHANISMS and largely wrong about the WELFARE CONCLUSION he draws from them.** The gap between "increasing returns are real" and "immigrants must make *us* richer" is exactly where all six rhetorical moves live.

## Claim-by-claim

### Claim 1 — "The assumption of constant returns to scale is nonsense; trade implies increasing returns; therefore immigrants MUST make us richer."

> "I think the assumption of constant returns to scale is nonsense. Not only that, it is obvious nonsense… There are three main channels by which returns simply have to be increasing: specialization, ideas, and fixed costs… of course immigrants make us richer." [SOURCE: yes-immigrants-must-make-us-richer]

**(a) Steelman:** Anti-immigration fiscal models (DiMartino/Manhattan, Borjas, CBO) assume CRS, which mechanically forces the result "poorer short-run, no change long-run"; CRS is theoretically wrong because specialization + non-rival ideas + amortizable fixed costs are genuine IRS channels, so a model assuming them away has assumed its anti-immigration conclusion.

**(b) Verdict: PARTLY SOLID (the critique of CRS) wrapped around an OVERCLAIM (the word "must").** GRANT the first half; kill the second.

**What is GRANTED (the repo agrees):**
- IRS channels are real and routinely undercounted. The repo's own effects-matrix lists task complementarity, idea/innovation spillovers, and scale as real positive channels that anti-immigration ledgers omit. [SOURCE: research/immigration-economist-effects-matrix.md]
- The single most important repo-verified vindication: **Colas-Sachs 2024 (AEJ:Policy, primary-verified)** finds one average low-skilled immigrant generates an **indirect fiscal benefit ≈ $750/yr** — *of the same order as and opposite sign to* the direct fiscal cost — precisely via the complementarity channel Decker invokes (immigrants raise complementary native high-skill wages/labor supply → natives pay more tax). This is the IRS-adjacent channel that static CRS-style NAS accounting omits *by construction*. Decker is **right** that the standard ledger has a hole exactly where he says. [SOURCE: 10.1257/pol.20220176] [confidence-ladder entry 45]

**Why the conclusion is an OVERCLAIM — the EXACT move:** This is **Move 1 (ledger switching)** executed in a single word. "Increasing returns exist" → "immigrants must make *us* richer" silently swaps the ledger from **aggregate output** (where IRS operates) to **incumbent per-capita welfare** (the "us"). They are different objects, and the gap is not rhetorical — it is the repo's central verified finding.

**The primary evidence that kills "must":**
1. **The aggregate-vs-per-capita wedge is officially demonstrated, not speculative.** CBO 2024 Outlook: higher immigration leaves **real GDP ~2% larger** but **real GDP per person ~0.8% SMALLER** in 2034, with average real wages slightly lower. IRS can be fully real and the "must make *us* richer" claim still false on the per-person ledger. The wedge is a *level fact*, not a modeling artifact — and it directly instantiates the thing Decker says cannot happen. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-02/59710-Outlook-2024.pdf]
2. **Colas-Sachs, his best friend, is also his limiting case.** The same paper that vindicates the complementarity channel finds the residual net-negative **concentrates in state/local education** — a federalism-allocation cost the $750 indirect benefit does *not* erase. "Must make us richer" requires the positive channel to dominate *every* ledger; the GE paper itself says it does not dominate the state/local education ledger. [SOURCE: 10.1257/pol.20220176] [confidence-ladder entry 45]
3. **The logical defect is a quantifier error, not a sign error.** "Trade implies *some* increasing returns somewhere" does not imply "increasing returns dominate the net incumbent-welfare ledger of *this* inflow." Decker needs the negative terms (state/local burden, housing incidence, low-skill wage pressure, congestion) to be zero or dominated. He asserts this; he never shows it. The repo's decisive-test inequality (`Δmacro + Δfederal > Δlow-skill-wage + Δstate/local + Δhousing + Δbacklash`) is exactly the thing "must" assumes away. [SOURCE: research/immigration-smith-decker-friedman-comparative-quantitative-audit-2026-04-11.md]

**Bonus kill — the CRS strawman is partly a strawman.** Decker says Borjas/CBO "assume CRS" and thereby "assume the answer." But CBO 2024's *actual* result is **not** the CRS-implied "no long-run change" — it finds **higher aggregate GDP with lower GDP-per-person**, a richer structure than CRS allows. The model he says bakes in null effects in fact produces a non-null aggregate gain *and* a per-capita loss. His "they assumed their conclusion" charge misfires against the very official source he names. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-02/59710-Outlook-2024.pdf]

### Claim 2 — "Open borders would approximately double world GDP."

> "estimates of the removal of transnational labor barriers, or open borders, would approximately double world GDP." [SOURCE: the-gains-from-trade-are-large]

**(a) Steelman:** The structural migration literature (place-premium + GE models) implies that removing global labor barriers would unlock gains on the order of ~100% of world GDP — making migration restriction one of the largest remaining global inefficiencies, dwarfing the 1-8% gains from removing all trade barriers.

**(b) Verdict: OVERCLAIM — textbook upper-bound laundering.** The repo already audited this exact slogan against the primary papers.

**The EXACT move: Move 2 (upper-bound laundering), with a self-inflicted wound.** Decker presents "double world GDP" as a settled magnitude ("would approximately double") — a realistic baseline. The repo's full-paper audit found this is a **stylized upper-bound** that requires assumptions Decker *himself elsewhere rejects*.

**The primary evidence that kills it (all papers read directly in the repo audit):**
1. **Clemens 2011 JEP** — the source slogan — explicitly requires "epic movements of people": *at least half the population of poor countries must move* for the complete-elimination numbers. Clemens distinguishes complete vs partial relaxation; the doubling rests on the complete case. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.83]
2. **Bradford 2012** gets ~75% of world GDP — but says it implies **94-97% of the poor-region workforce moves**, which he himself calls **"unrealistic."** [SOURCE: https://freit.org/WorkingPapers/Papers/Immigration/FREIT432.pdf]
3. **Docquier-Machado-Sekkat 2012** — the paper the doubling-slogan omits — adds incompressible moving costs, congestion, endogenous productivity, imperfect substitution, network effects → gains fall to **~4% of world GDP** long-run. [SOURCE: https://sites.uclouvain.be/econ/DP/IRES/2012023.pdf]
4. **World Bank 2018 (Moving for Prosperity)** — the policy-scaled benchmark — moving 100M young people yields ~**$1.4T/yr**, enormous but *nowhere near* doubling. [SOURCE: World Bank Moving for Prosperity]
5. **Repo's own conservative calibration:** at $7,500/migrant, you need **+3 BILLION** additional migrants to reach Clemens-style ~20% of world GDP territory; a policy-sized +200M-to-+1B yields 1.4-6.8% of world GDP. Doubling is off the realistic map. [DATA: sources/immigration-causal/data/clemens/open_borders_calibration_baseline.json]
6. **The break-even/absorption stress test:** +200M migrants implies ~80M housing units (~55% of current US stock), requiring ~1.9× current US housing starts for 30 straight years; housing/construction **binds in year 1** of every large-flow scenario. [SOURCE: research/immigration-open-borders-break-even-bounds-2026-04-22.md]

**The internal contradiction:** In the *same essay*, several paragraphs later, Decker writes: **"the structural estimates of immigration are likely high estimates. They generally assume that labor is identical… Obviously, if labor is not identical, this breaks down! And especially since we would expect the most skilled people to move first…"** [SOURCE: the-gains-from-trade-are-large]. He correctly identifies that the doubling models assume homogeneous labor and ignore selection — and then **uses the doubling figure anyway as his headline comparison against trade.** He cites the upper bound as fact in one breath and debunks its key assumption in the next; **he documents it himself and proceeds regardless** — the laundering is in his own text.

### Claim 3 — "The only coherent anti-immigration view is that immigrants will vote for bad things; the economic arguments are incoherent and irrational."

> "The only coherent anti-immigration view is that they will vote for bad things… What is incoherent and irrational are the economic arguments against immigration." [SOURCE: yes-immigrants-must-make-us-richer]

**(a) Steelman:** Once you accept IRS, every *economic* objection (wage loss, fiscal burden) dissolves in the long run, leaving only the political-economy channel (immigrant voting → worse institutions/policy) as a logically coherent reason to restrict.

**(b) Verdict: FALSE — the repo refutes it with its own data.**

**The EXACT move: Move 4 (capacity erasure) + Move 6 (aggregate-output trump card).** By collapsing all economic objections into "incoherent," Decker erases the destination as a capacity system (schools, shelter, housing) and treats the aggregate-output gain as if it answers the local-incidence question.

**The primary evidence that kills it:**
1. **CBO 2025 (official, nonpartisan) finds the 2021+ surge net-negative for state/local budgets** — −$9.2B direct / −$9.8B potential in 2023 — with the largest buckets **education, shelter, and border security**, plus explicit nonbudgetary crowding (crowded classrooms, crowded transit). These are *economic* costs, identified by the official scorekeeper, requiring **zero** appeal to voting behavior. The objection Decker calls "incoherent" is the one CBO scored. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-06/61256-immigration-state-local.pdf]
2. **The repo's own warehouse shows the local-capacity channel is operational, not decorative:** real school-burden heterogeneity by origin (Afghanistan 2.63, Honduras 1.29 school-age children per linked household) and real within-state rent-sorting (Colombia +$184/mo above state-average rent). Coherent local-fiscal objections exist in the repo's primary data. [SOURCE: research/immigration-household-weighted-correction.md] [SOURCE: research/immigration-local-burden-puma-layer.md]
3. **Housing incidence is now CAUSALLY identified, not hand-waved:** Wilson-Zhou 2026 (Dallas Fed WP2607, primary-verified) — unauthorized inflow = 1% of initial employment → **+2.2% house prices, +1.4% rents**, with permit/supply response statistically null. A real renter-incidence cost, causal, with no voting channel invoked. [SOURCE: 10.24149/wp2607] [confidence-ladder entry 50]
4. **The federalism split is the coherent economic objection in one sentence:** the federal ledger is positive (CBO: −$0.9T deficits) *while* the state/local ledger is negative — an **incidence mismatch**, not a contradiction. A homeowner in a destination metro and a renter in the same metro face opposite signs. That is a coherent, non-political, distributional economic objection — and it is the repo's central verified finding. [SOURCE: confidence-ladder entries 46, 50] [SOURCE: research/immigration-smith-decker-friedman-comparative-quantitative-audit-2026-04-11.md]

**Note [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]:** Decker frames restriction-by-voting as the *only* coherent objection partly to dismiss it as illiberal ("restrictionism is itself one of those bad things people vote for"). But the existence of coherent *fiscal-incidence* objections does not require endorsing them — the repo's stance is "incidence split, not scalar verdict," which is precisely the coherent economic position Decker's sentence declares impossible. His error is a claim about the **space of coherent arguments**, and it is empirically false.

### Claim 4 — High-skill immigration is unambiguously good: immigrant inventors → 23% of inventive output (~36% with spillovers); "no solid argument against allowing high-skilled immigration."

> "they are directly responsible for 23% of all inventive output, and plausibl[y] responsible [for] 36% of all innovation through their spillovers… There is no solid argument against allowing high-skilled immigration." [SOURCE: should-we-restrict-immigration-to; on-the-measurement-of-human-capital] *(Decker's original reads "plausible responsible 36%"; "[y]" and "[for]" are editorial completions of his grammar, meaning unchanged.)*

**(a) Steelman:** Immigrant inventors are disproportionately productive (Bernstein-Diamond-Jiranaphawiboon-McQuade-Pousada; Prato on patent spillovers), have positive spillovers onto collaborators, and won't plausibly degrade institutions — so high-skill immigration has large IRS-driven innovation gains and essentially no downside.

**(b) Verdict: LARGELY SOLID — GRANT it, with two scoping caveats.** This is Decker's strongest and best-targeted argument, and intellectual honesty requires conceding it.

**Why granted:**
- The repo's effects-matrix and fiscal updates concur that **high-skill immigrants drive the average positive fiscal result** (Orrenius-Viard-Zavodny AEI 2025; NAS by education/age). The innovation-spillover channel is exactly the kind of IRS effect the repo flags as real and undercounted. [SOURCE: research/immigration-economist-effects-matrix.md]
- This is the case where Decker's IRS framework is *most* applicable: specialization + idea-generation + agglomeration are strongest at the high-skill frontier, and the empirical inventor-productivity literature supports it. The repo has no primary evidence contradicting the direction.

**The two caveats (scoping, not refutation):**
1. **The 23%/36% figures are not repo-verified at primary** (I did not independently confirm Bernstein et al.'s decomposition or Prato's spillover magnitude in this pass) — [GAP], flagged below. The *direction* is solid; the exact spillover share is [UNVERIFIED] and Decker himself hedges it ("plausibly").
2. **"No solid argument against high-skill immigration" overreaches at the seam.** Even high-skill inflow has *local* housing/congestion incidence (high-earners sort into the most inelastic metros — the repo's Saiz panel shows FB-share concentrates in the lowest-elasticity quartile, and high-skill immigrants amplify, not relieve, that). "No solid argument" should be "the net case is strongly positive and the burden of proof shifts to the restrictionist" — which is true — not "literally no coherent cost exists." [SOURCE: confidence-ladder entry 18] [SOURCE: research/immigration-open-borders-double-world-gdp-and-apartheid-audit-2026-04-21.md]

**This is a place the dismantling concedes hard and moves on.** Inflating a quibble here would be the dishonest one-sidedness the framing forbids.

### Claim 5 — "Brain drain is probably just totally wrong"; emigration *raises* origin-country human capital.

> "skilled emigration would actually leave the nation more skilled in the long run, not less… Immigration is just so big and so good, it is insane that we restrict it." [SOURCE: brain-drain-is-probably-just-totally]

**(a) Steelman:** Emigration prospects raise the *returns* to skill acquisition in the origin country (Abarcar-Theoharides: US nurse visas → Philippines ended up with *more* skill, not less; Kremer O-ring escape from low equilibrium), and remittances (~6% of low-income-country GDP) further offset, so the "brain drain impoverishes the source" worry is theoretically and empirically weak.

**(b) Verdict: DIRECTIONALLY DEFENSIBLE on the GLOBAL/ORIGIN ledger — but IRRELEVANT to the incumbent-destination question, and the kicker overclaims.** GRANT the origin point; flag the ledger swap.

**Why partly granted:** The brain-gain literature is real and the repo's global-ledger frame (Friedman audit) already credits large migrant + origin gains. The Abarcar-Theoharides Filipino-nurse result is a legitimate datum. On the *global/origin* ledger, Decker is on solid ground. [SOURCE: research/immigration-smith-decker-friedman-comparative-quantitative-audit-2026-04-11.md §global ledger]

**The EXACT move on the kicker: Move 1 (ledger switching) again.** "Immigration is just so big and so good, it is insane that we restrict it" — the brain-drain argument is entirely about *origin-country* welfare; the policy conclusion ("insane that *we* restrict it") is about *destination* policy. The origin-gain finding says nothing about destination incumbent incidence. He proves a claim on ledger A and bills it to ledger B.

**Secondary kill (overclaim within the origin ledger):** "Probably just *totally* wrong" overshoots his own evidence. The literature is **mixed**; Decker concedes "officially, the evidence is mixed" and then dismisses the drain papers as "not particularly rigorous." That is upper-bound laundering at the within-topic level — selecting the favorable half of a genuinely contested literature and declaring the question closed. The Puerto Rico example (richest LatAm country "were it a country") is a single observational case with massive selection/confounding (US citizenship, currency union, federal transfers), not identification. [SOURCE: brain-drain-is-probably-just-totally] [INFERENCE]

### Claim 6 — The selected-legal-inflow / mass-surge elision (cross-cutting; Decker's deepest structural flaw)

This is not a single quote — it is the **load-bearing equivocation running under his whole corpus**, and it is the single most important thing to name.

**The pattern:** Decker's vivid, persuasive cases are **selected, marginal, often high-skill** inflows:
- the Harvard professor freed to specialize by an unskilled worker [yes-immigrants-must-make-us-richer]
- immigrant *inventors* at 23% of patents [should-we-restrict-immigration-to]
- Filipino *nurses* (a skilled, visa-selected stream) [brain-drain]
- H-1B *software engineers* earning the place premium [on-the-measurement-of-human-capital]
- Spanish-speaking *snow-shovelers* doing jobs natives won't at current wages [yes-immigrants-must-make-us-richer]

**The conclusion he draws** is about immigration *in general* — "immigrants are good for the exact same reasons that more people are good, of any kind" — and toward *maximizing* it ("does not maximize immigration in the long run" is his only conceded constraint).

**The EXACT move: Move 3 (marginal-to-mass extrapolation) feeding Move 1 (ledger switching).** Evidence about **selected, marginal, gradual, often high-skill** movers is extrapolated to a **mass, concentrated, surge, composition-heterogeneous** regime. The repo's frontier moved *away* from generic stock-share arguments for exactly this reason — the live object is `flow × capacity × composition × regime`, not a scalar "more people good." [SOURCE: research/immigration-economist-rhetorical-failures-2026-04-22.md] [SOURCE: research/immigration-frontier-rethink-2026-04-22.md]

**The primary evidence that the elision matters:**
1. **Composition flips the fiscal sign.** Dustmann-Frattini 2014 (primary-verified): recent UK EEA immigrants paid **34% more than they took out** (+£22.1bn) — *while* non-EEA and native ran negative. The sign is `f(welfare generosity × cohort participation/skill)`, **not a constant of "immigration."** Decker's "of any kind" is exactly the framing the evidence retires. [SOURCE: 10.1111/ecoj.12181] [confidence-ladder entry 47]
2. **The crime advantage he'd lean on is first-gen + authorized-carried, and decays.** The repo's strongest grant to the pro side (immigrants lower-crime) is itself scoped: the protective signal is carried by **authorized** immigrants (undocumented ≈ ecological null), is **first-generation**, and **decays toward native rates by the 2nd generation**. "Immigrants of any kind, forever" is not what even the favorable crime evidence supports. [SOURCE: 10.1257/jep.38.1.181] [SOURCE: confidence-ladder entry 48]
3. **Marginal-mover place-premium ≠ mass-flow gain.** Place Premium (Clemens-Montenegro-Pritchett) is a fact about a *marginal/moderate-skill mover under current institutions*; it does not survive when destination housing, schools, and absorption are stressed by orders of magnitude (Docquier et al. → ~4%). Decker's H-1B and nurse examples are marginal-mover evidence doing mass-policy work. [SOURCE: research/immigration-open-borders-double-world-gdp-and-apartheid-audit-2026-04-21.md]
4. **Surge-specific local costs are real and were scored.** The selected-legal inflow Decker pictures is not the 2021-2026 surge CBO scored at −$9.2B state/local with education/shelter/border bottlenecks. The elision between "a Filipino nurse" and "a 4.4M-person surge in one year" is the whole ballgame. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-06/61256-immigration-state-local.pdf]

**Best short-form attack line:** *"Every persuasive example you give is a selected, marginal, often high-skill mover. Your conclusion is about mass immigration 'of any kind.' Which one is the argument — because the evidence for the first does not transfer to the second, and the official scoring of the second (CBO state/local, Wilson-Zhou rents) is exactly the cost you call incoherent."*

## What survives (honestly)

Decker is the **most intellectually aggressive and the most overstated** of the commentators here, but not a fool — a great deal survives:

**Survives (grant these without hedging):**
1. **The CRS critique is legitimately sharp.** Naive constant-returns modeling *does* mechanically bias toward null long-run immigration effects, and the IRS channels (specialization, ideas, fixed costs) are real and undercounted — the repo's own Colas-Sachs vindication ($750/yr indirect benefit via complementarity) proves there is a real hole in the static ledger exactly where Decker points. [SOURCE: 10.1257/pol.20220176]
2. **High-skill immigration is strongly net-positive** and the burden of proof rightly sits on the restrictionist there. Claim 4 is granted. [SOURCE: research/immigration-economist-effects-matrix.md]
3. **The global/origin ledger is large and favorable** — migrant gains, brain-gain, remittances are real; crude "brain drain dooms the source" and "open borders is obvious nonsense" rhetoric both fail. [SOURCE: research/immigration-open-borders-double-world-gdp-and-apartheid-audit-2026-04-21.md]
4. **The repo agrees immigrants are not higher-crime** (direction; first-gen TX, conviction-margin) and that the realized federal surge is fiscally positive (CBO −$0.9T) — Decker is directionally right on both. [SOURCE: confidence-ladder entries 46, 48]

**Does NOT survive (the kills):**
1. **The word "must."** "Increasing returns exist" → "immigrants must make *us* richer" is a quantifier error and a ledger switch. CBO's own GDP-up / GDP-per-person-down result is the counterexample Decker says cannot exist. [SOURCE: CBO 2024 Outlook]
2. **"Double world GDP" as a realistic figure** — it is a stylized upper bound requiring half-of-poor-countries-move; he cites it as fact and **discredits its key assumption in the same essay**. [SOURCE: the-gains-from-trade-are-large; research/immigration-open-borders-...-2026-04-21.md]
3. **"The only coherent objection is voting."** Flatly false — CBO scored the coherent economic (state/local fiscal-incidence) objection at −$9.2B; Wilson-Zhou identified the causal rent cost. [SOURCE: CBO 2025; 10.24149/wp2607]
4. **"Immigrants are good… of any kind," toward maximization.** The sign is composition-, status-, generation-, and capacity-contingent (Dustmann-Frattini; first-gen/authorized crime; surge-specific local costs), not a constant. The marginal-to-mass extrapolation is the structural failure under everything. [SOURCE: 10.1111/ecoj.12181; confidence-ladder entries 47, 48]

**The one-sentence verdict:** Decker wins the argument he is actually having — *against naive constant-returns reasoning* — and loses the argument he *thinks* he is having — *that this settles incumbent-citizen welfare and licenses maximal immigration of any composition.* His mechanisms are mostly right; his welfare conclusion does not follow from them; and on his single most-cited empirical slogan ("double world GDP") he refutes himself in his own text.
