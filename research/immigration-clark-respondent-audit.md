# Clark survey respondent audit: strongest agree votes

Date: 2026-04-10

Primary source:

1. https://kentclarkcenter.org/surveys/low-skilled-immigrants/

## Scope

This note audits the strongest high-confidence `agree` votes on the Clark low-skilled immigration survey.

Question:

Are these economists relying on evidence that is factually wrong?

Answer:

Usually no. The more common problem is that the evidence base is partial and then rhetorically stretched into a broader claim than the paper supports.

## Strongest cases

1. Hilary Hoynes is the strongest evidence-based `agree` in this subgroup, but still partial. The relevant work helps with safety-net participation and low-income household incidence, not the full housing-school-congestion ledger.
2. Kenneth Judd states a narrow and testable labor-market mechanism. That is more defensible than broad theory talk, but still only one channel.
3. Oliver Hart is explicit about broad efficiency logic, but that is not empirical proof of the full average-citizen welfare claim.

## Main conclusion

The strongest critique is not that these economists are obviously lying or using fabricated results.

The stronger critique is:

1. many of the strongest agree votes do not cite a concrete immigration paper on the survey page
2. the underlying evidence base usually identifies only one slice of the problem
3. those slices are then used as if they settle the full welfare question

So the right label is usually:

1. `scope-limited`, or
2. `externally overgeneralized`

not `factually wrong`.
