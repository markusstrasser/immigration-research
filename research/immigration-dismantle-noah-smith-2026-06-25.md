## The single most important finding: Noah Smith has TWO registers, and he never reconciles them

This is the master key to dismantling him fairly, and it is what the prior April 2026 audit missed.

**Register A — the "evidence" essays** (`Why immigration doesn't reduce wages`, 2020; `Mass deportation would accomplish nothing`, 2024-08; `Is immigration raising rents in America?`; `Did immigration bring down inflation?`). Here Smith deploys steady-state, average-effect, national-aggregate machinery built to defeat restrictionist slogans. The signature move: "the increases in labor demand and labor supply roughly counteract each other… the total effect on wages is…not much" and "The overall effect on the cost of living should be relatively small." [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/mass-deportation-would-accomplish, verbatim]

**Register B — the "reasonable" essays** (`America, please be reasonable on immigration`, 2024-01; `On immigration, what Americans want is democratic control`, 2024-10). Here Smith CONCEDES nearly everything a disciplined restrictionist would want conceded:

> "But to be fair to the Republicans here, immigration, unlike the economy, actually is a big problem right now. Since 2021, there has been an absolute flood of people crossing the southwestern border illegally… **This is a massive problem.** It's a short-term logistical problem in terms of how to house and feed the millions pouring into the country — a task that is now straining the resources of many heavily Democratic cities. It's **a long-term fiscal problem, because these people will require heavy government support for their health care, housing, and education; this will end up coming from city and state governments**, since they're barred from federal welfare programs." [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/america-please-be-reasonable-on-immigration, verbatim, 2024-01-29]

And he reclassifies low-skilled inflow itself:

> Smith advocates prioritizing high-skilled legal immigration while accepting "some low-skilled immigrants, but far less than 2 million per year," characterizing mass low-skilled migration as **an "altruistic luxury" versus an economic necessity.** [SOURCE: same essay]

**The dismantling is not "Smith is wrong about X."** On most empirics he is right. The dismantling is: **Smith's own Register B concedes the exact local-incidence, state/local-fiscal, capacity, and composition costs that Register A's machinery is built to wave away — and he never tells the reader that Register A's reassuring conclusions do not survive the conditions Register B admits are now reality.** The reader who absorbs `Mass deportation would accomplish nothing` walks away thinking the cost-of-living and fiscal questions are settled-small; the same author, in another tab, calls the situation "a massive problem" and "a long-term fiscal problem." Both can't be the headline. [INFERENCE] [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]

That is **ledger-switching across essays** — move (1) — operating at the level of his whole corpus, not a single paragraph. It is the most defensible serious charge against him, and it is fair because it is built entirely from his own words.

## Per-claim dismantling

### Claim 1 — "Immigration doesn't reduce native wages; supply and demand shocks cancel"

**(a) Steelman (one sentence).** Immigrants are simultaneously a labor-supply shock (pushing wages down) and a consumer-demand shock (buying housing, food, haircuts, healthcare → raising labor demand), they are often task-complementary to natives, and global capital is elastic — so the average native wage effect of observed inflows is empirically near zero. [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/why-immigration-doesnt-reduce-wages]

**(b) SOLID — grant it (with one scope flag).** The repo's own evidence agrees on the AVERAGE and on the DESIGNS Smith implicitly invokes. The E-Verify TWFE (QWI 2003-2023, 9 treated states) found +0.51% on entry-wages, 95% CI excluding gains above ~2.1% — *aligns with Card direction, against large Borjas magnitudes* (ladder entry 17). Mariel's "10-30% harm" is artifact-fragile: Clemens-Hunt 2019 shows Borjas 2017's result survives only under a 4-way knife-cut leaving ~17 obs/yr, riding a 55pp black-share jump; broadened sample → ≈ zero (ladder entry 44, primary-verified 10.3386/w23433). Foged-Peri Denmark agrees. The lump-of-labor fallacy Smith implicitly attacks IS a real fallacy. **So Smith is broadly right that native-wage-doom narratives are overstated. This is his strongest claim and it should be conceded without hedging.**

**(c) The kill — move (3), marginal-to-mass extrapolation + one hidden ledger-switch.** The "cancel out" result is bounded to the *marginal policy variation* it was estimated on. The repo's own meta-update (ladder entry 29) is explicit: "The 2021-2024 surge is a regime shift OUTSIDE that variation. Linear extrapolation is not warranted." Smith does two things the evidence doesn't license:

1. **He extends a marginal-flow average to a surge regime.** In `Mass deportation` (2024) he reasserts "the total effect on wages is…not much" with no acknowledgment that the 8.7M-above-baseline surge he elsewhere calls "a massive problem" is outside the support of every study he cites. The mechanism (elastic capital, smooth complementarity, demand keeping pace) is a *steady-state* mechanism; concentrated surges into specific low-wage labor markets and specific cities are exactly where it's least guaranteed to hold. [INFERENCE]

2. **"Average native" silently swaps for "all natives."** Smith treats a near-zero *average* as if it answered the *distributional* question. The repo never closes Borjas-vs-Peri on low-skill native displacement (it is logged as *unresolved*, not settled — claims-audit Claim 2). A zero average is consistent with competing low-skill workers (including prior immigrants — a loss Smith himself cites from the German/East-German study) losing while complemented natives gain. Smith does NOT distinguish average from distribution; the prior audit confirms "Smith does NOT explicitly distinguish between average wage effects and distributional impacts across worker groups." That is **denominator/aggregation masking** (move 5) riding on top of the extrapolation: the group that bears the incidence is dissolved into the national average that reads ≈ 0.

**Verdict: directionally right on the average, illegitimately stretched to (i) the surge regime and (ii) the distributional question.** Not a factual error — a scope overclaim. The fair sharp question: *"Your 'cancel-out' estimate comes from marginal policy variation. Why should it survive an 8.7M concentrated surge you yourself call a 'massive problem,' and why does a zero average tell us anything about the low-skill natives and prior immigrants competing directly with the inflow?"*

### Claim 2 — "Immigration's effect on the cost of living / rents is small; it pushes some prices up but pushes others down"

**(a) Steelman.** Immigrants raise housing demand (rents up) but their labor supply lowers the price of construction, childcare, lawn care, food service (those prices down), so the *net* cost-of-living effect is small; nationally, once you adjust for unit size, rents haven't measurably risen. [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/is-immigration-raising-rents-in-america]

**(b) PARTLY SOLID, PARTLY OVERCLAIM.** Grant: the symmetric-price logic is real and the lump-of-labor-on-prices error he attacks is real. Grant also that Smith is MORE honest here than the prior audit credited — in the rents essay he concedes "Immigration… might be straining the housing markets in a few big cities" and in `Mass deportation` he writes "These prices should go up (and in the case of housing, **it definitely does**)." He does not deny local housing incidence. **That concession should be stated plainly.**

**(c) The kill — move (4) capacity erasure + move (5) denominator masking, now MEASURED.** Smith's error is not denial; it's *netting*. He treats the housing price rise as **offset** by cheaper services into a small net — and that netting hides three things the repo has now measured:

1. **Incidence is not symmetric across people.** The rent rise lands on renters (disproportionately low-income, disproportionately immigrants themselves and competing natives); the service-price savings accrue broadly, often to higher-income households who consume more childcare/lawn care/restaurants. Smith's "net is small" is a *sum across a population* that masks a *transfer between groups*. The repo's distributional frame (ladder entries 18, 50; claims-audit data-check #2: PUMA rent for sorted immigrant groups exceeds state-average rent by up to +$184) is precisely the renter-incidence Smith's net conceals. This is the classic **denominator/aggregation mask** (move 5).

2. **The "few big cities" are where immigrants actually are — so the local case is the central case, not the exception.** The repo's `msa_fb_rent_panel` (2026-06-25): **corr(foreign-born share, rent LEVEL) = +0.69**, amplified to **+0.74 in the inelastic-supply tercile** (Miami 43% fb/$1914, San Jose 41%/$2773, LA 33%, SF 33%, NYC 30%). Immigrants concentrate in exactly the high-rent, supply-inelastic metros where a demand shock capitalizes hardest into rents (ladder entry 18: top FB-share quintile median Saiz elasticity 1.51 vs 3.40 bottom). Smith waves this to "a few big cities… not a huge problem" — but those cities are the modal immigrant destination. He erases destination *capacity* (move 4) as a first-order stress system. [SOURCE: research/immigration-msa-rent-elasticity-panel-2026-06-25.md]

3. **There IS now a causal magnitude, and it is not negligible.** Wilson & Zhou 2026 (Dallas Fed WP2607, primary-verified 10.24149/wp2607): unauthorized inflow = 1% of initial employment → **+2.2% house prices, +1.4% rents**, with the supply/permit response statistically *null* (confirming the inelastic mechanism); explains ~30% of house-price / ~20% of rent growth in the average MSA over the boom. "Relatively small" is defensible only by netting against service prices and never naming who bears each side.

**The honest counter-weight (disconfirmation, mandatory).** Smith's *growth*-margin instinct survives: the repo's same panel finds **corr(fb-share, rent GROWTH 2016-25) = −0.17** (null-to-negative — the 2021-25 run-up was rates/remote-work, not immigration-timed; matches Harvard JCHS, Yale Budget Lab), and the +0.69 LEVEL correlation is **sorting-confounded** (immigrants *choose* high-amenity high-cost metros — Borjas area-studies critique). So Smith is right that immigration is NOT the main driver of the recent national rent spike. **He is wrong that the local renter-incidence is small enough to net away** — it is real, causal, concentrated, and falls on the least-able-to-pay. The binding policy variable is housing-supply elasticity (Hsieh-Moretti), which Smith elsewhere champions — so the fix is "build housing," not "deny incidence," but he should say the incidence is real first.

**Verdict: the symmetric-netting move is the failure.** A small *net average* is being used to dismiss a real, measured, *concentrated, regressive* incidence. Fair sharp question: *"Your 'net is small' sums rent-payers and service-buyers into one number. Who pays the +1.4% rent — and is it the same household that saves on lawn care?"*

### Claim 3 — "Immigration is mostly unrelated to inflation; it didn't drive the 2023 disinflation"

**(a) Steelman.** The strong partisan claim that immigration was the *main reason* 2023 inflation fell is unsupported — the timing doesn't fit (immigrants' relative wages dropped 2021-22 at peak inflation), and the Fed + fading supply shocks explain disinflation better. [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/did-immigration-bring-down-inflation]

**(b) SOLID — grant it, and credit the symmetry.** This is Smith *resisting* a pro-immigration overclaim from his own side (Krugman's). The repo agrees immigrants are not a clean all-purpose inflation explanation (claims-audit Claim 3). Crediting this is important: it demonstrates Smith is not a pure cheerleader, which is exactly why the fair critique lands harder.

**(c) Minor kill — move (1) ledger-switch between "headline" and "housing."** The only overreach is the slide from "immigration didn't drive *headline disinflation*" to the broader impression that immigration is "mostly unrelated to the inflation issue." The IMF 2025 WP finds higher immigration *lowers* local goods inflation but *raises* housing-and-utilities inflation (claims-audit Claim 3). "Mostly unrelated to inflation" is true at the headline level and false at the shelter-CPI level — and shelter is ~⅓ of CPI. This is the same netting problem as Claim 2 wearing an inflation label. Smith is right on the narrow claim he actually defends; the broad-brush summary is too broad.

**Verdict: correct on the claim he defends; the generalization quietly switches the headline ledger for the shelter ledger.** Minor, but it's the same move.

### Claim 4 — "Immigrants — even illegal ones — are probably a net fiscal positive"

**(a) Steelman.** Per the 2016 National Academies report, immigration's fiscal impact is positive for everyone except the very-least-educated; since ~half of recent Mexican/Central American migrants finished high school, the positive contributions cancel the small negative from the less-educated; and immigrants are largely barred from federal welfare, illegal ones even more so. [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/mass-deportation-would-accomplish, verbatim]

**(b) SOLID at the FEDERAL level — grant the realized-surge magnitude.** The repo's strongest recent fiscal datum AGREES: CBO 2024 (pub 60165) finds the realized surge (net +8.7M) **lowers federal deficits ≈ $0.9T over 2024-2034** and boosts GDP +$8.9T (ladder entry 46, primary-verified). Colas-Sachs 2024 adds the indirect channel: one low-skilled immigrant generates ≈ **+$750/yr** in indirect fiscal benefit via raising complementary native wages → more native tax (ladder entry 45, 10.1257/pol.20220176). The federal welfare-bar Smith cites is real. **At the federal cash-flow ledger, "net positive" is correct and CBO-backed. Grant it without hedging.**

**(c) The kill — move (1) ledger-switch, federal → state/local, which SMITH HIMSELF makes correctly in Register B but drops in Register A.** This is the cleanest catch in the whole memo, because Smith proves he knows the answer and then writes as if he doesn't:

- In `Mass deportation` (Register A) the operative impression is "net positive," full stop, with the federal welfare-bar doing the reassurance.
- In `America, please be reasonable` (Register B) he states the *correct* incidence himself: the surge is "a long-term fiscal problem, because these people will require heavy government support for their health care, housing, and education; **this will end up coming from city and state governments**, since they're barred from federal welfare programs."

He has the federalism split *exactly right in Register B* — the federal welfare-bar that makes the federal ledger positive is the SAME mechanism that pushes the cost down to states (CBO 2025, pub 61256: state/local net-negative in 2023, concentrated in education; ladder entry 46 CAVEAT). The repo's own warehouse shows the load-bearing channel: school-age-children-per-household up to 2.63 (Afghanistan), 1.29 (Honduras), 1.01 (Mexico) among recent low-skill adults (claims-audit data-check #1; ladder entry 41). **"Net fiscal positive" is true federally and false-or-unresolved at the state/local ledger where the education cost lands — and Smith demonstrably knows this.** The charge is not error; it's *selective ledger presentation* depending on which essay he's writing. [INFERENCE]

Two further bounds that tighten the grant (both cut toward Smith, stated for honesty): (i) the NAS *static* per-capita cost is an UPPER BOUND — GE complementarity (Colas-Sachs) shrinks or flips it (ladder entry 45); (ii) the sign is regime-specific, not a constant of low-skill immigration — Dustmann-Frattini 2014 UK EEA migrants paid 34% *more* than they took (ladder entry 47, 10.1111/ecoj.12181). So the restrictionist "low-skill = fiscal drain by nature" is ALSO wrong. The honest frame is *neither* Smith's "net positive" headline *nor* the restrictionist "drain" — it's **federal-positive, state/local-negative-on-education, composition-and-horizon-dependent.**

**Verdict: federally correct, but the "net positive" headline laundered across the federal/state-local boundary — a boundary Smith crosses correctly in his own other essay.** Fair sharp question: *"You wrote that the surge is 'a long-term fiscal problem' falling on 'city and state governments.' Which is the headline — that, or 'net positive'? And does the federal welfare-bar that makes Washington's ledger positive not mechanically push the cost onto the states?"*

### Claim 5 — "Immigrants, including illegal immigrants, have LOWER crime rates than native-born"

**(a) Steelman.** Light/He/Robey (2020) Texas data: undocumented immigrants have substantially lower crime rates than the native-born; US-born are >2× more likely to be arrested for violent crime, 2.5× for drug crime, >4× for property crime; and high-illegal-immigration cities didn't see more crime 2007-2016. [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/mass-deportation-would-accomplish, verbatim citing Light et al.]

**(b) SOLID — grant it fully; his numbers are the repo's numbers.** The repo's crime memo independently confirms every figure Smith cites: Light et al. 2020 (PNAS, 10.1073/pnas.2014704117), the >2×/2.5×/4× arrest gradient, robustness to convictions-vs-arrests, and the null surge-city result. The conviction-margin upgrade (Cato/Nowrasteh TX 2013-2022: illegal-immigrant homicide-conviction rate 2.2 vs 3.0/100k native, −26%) closes the "arrests ≠ guilt" objection on the most-reported crime (ladder entry 48). SPI incarceration 0.86× agrees (ladder entry 42). **The DIRECTION is repo-strong. Smith is correct. Concede it without reservation — this is where a one-sided hit job would self-destruct against our own data.**

**(c) The kill is NOT of the claim — it's of two missing caveats Smith never states, where the repo is MORE careful than he is.** Smith cites the raw 2× gap as the headline. The repo flags two things that *bound* it (neither reverses the sign):

1. **Race-composition confound.** The 2× violent gap compares predominantly-Hispanic undocumented immigrants to ALL native-born (including Black Americans). Race-matched (vs non-Hispanic-white native), the gap roughly *halves* — ~30% lower, not ~50% lower (Cato PA994; repo crime memo: "the headline '2x lower' overstates the gap"). Smith uses the bigger number; the honest number is smaller. The sign holds; the magnitude he quotes is inflated.

2. **First-gen-only + generational decay + documentation heterogeneity.** The protective effect is a *first-generation* phenomenon that decays toward native rates by the 2nd generation (RTI 2024), and ecologically the UNAUTHORIZED share is a *null* arrest predictor once controlled — the signal is carried by *authorized* immigrants (ladder entry 48; NIJ 310356). So "illegal immigrants are low-crime" is true for the first generation but is not a durable, generation-proof, or cleanly-unauthorized-specific fact.

**Verdict: the claim is CORRECT and the repo backs it; the only fault is that Smith quotes the *inflated* (non-race-matched) magnitude and omits the first-gen/decay caveat that the repo insists on.** This is the rare case where the pro-immigration writer is *insufficiently* hedged in his own favor's-direction overstatement. Honest note: this is a weak hit — it's a magnitude-and-caveat quibble on a claim that is fundamentally right. Say so.

### Claim 6 — "Mass deportation would accomplish nothing / its economic benefits would be negative"

**(a) Steelman.** Removing ~1M people wouldn't lower the cost of living (the cancel-out logic), would worsen the federal fiscal position, and would inflict large unrest/fear costs — so the economic benefits are negative or nil and the policy is largely pointless (the essay's own phrasing: the economic benefits "would likely be negative overall"). [SOURCE: noahpinion.blog/p/mass-deportation-would-accomplish]

**(b) MOSTLY SOLID — grant the core.** The repo does not support a story where removing immigrants makes the average citizen better off; mass deportation of 7M unauthorized is modeled at ~$1.45T first-order output loss / ~5% GDP (ladder entry 24). Smith's "it won't be the clean windfall the GOP platform promises" is correct, and his federal-fiscal-worsening point is the CBO-backed flip-side of Claim 4. The lump-of-labor error in "deport them and wages/housing improve" is real.

**(c) The kill — move (3) again + a rhetorical overreach in the title.** "Accomplish NOTHING" is the same absolute-ization as Claim 1. The repo's own caveats: (i) removing workers from construction/agriculture/cleaning is NOT a zero-effect event (entry 24: Construction −5.9%, Other services −8.8%); the cost is *to GDP and to consumers*, but "nothing changes" is false — prices and output in those sectors move. (ii) Smith's own Register B concedes deportation "might take some fiscal pressure off of some local governments" — i.e., it accomplishes *something* on the exact state/local education ledger he flagged. The honest claim is "mass deportation is a costly policy whose benefits are far smaller than advocates claim and are outweighed by output and humanitarian costs" — NOT "nothing." The title oversells via the same marginal-average machinery, ignoring the concentrated sectoral and local-fiscal effects he elsewhere acknowledges. [INFERENCE]

**Verdict: directionally right against magical-windfall promises; "nothing" is a rhetorical absolute the evidence (and his own Register B) contradicts.** Fair sharp question: *"You concede deportation would relieve some local-government fiscal pressure and would shrink construction/agriculture output — so it accomplishes something, at a cost. Why frame it as 'nothing' rather than 'costly, with benefits smaller than advertised'?"*

## What survives (the honest concession — be specific)

A fair reckoning has to state plainly how much of Noah Smith is *correct*, because most of it is, and because the repo's own evidence is his witness:

1. **The average native-wage effect of observed inflows is small.** Repo-confirmed (E-Verify TWFE, Mariel-artifact, Foged-Peri). The lump-of-labor fallacy is real. **Smith wins this outright.**
2. **The realized 2021-26 surge is fiscally POSITIVE at the federal level.** CBO −$0.9T deficits, +$8.9T GDP (ladder entry 46). Plus the indirect channel (Colas-Sachs +$750/yr). **Smith's federal "net positive" is correct.**
3. **Immigrants, including the undocumented first generation, have LOWER crime rates.** Light et al. + Cato convictions + SPI incarceration, all repo-confirmed (ladder entries 42, 48). **Smith is right; the sign is robust.**
4. **Immigration did NOT drive the 2023 disinflation, and did NOT drive the recent rent spike.** Smith resisting Krugman is correct; the repo's growth-margin rent null (−0.17) and JCHS/Yale-Budget-Lab timing both back him. **Smith wins the national-aggregate-causation question.**
5. **Mass deportation is a costly policy whose advertised windfalls are fictional.** Repo's $1.45T sim agrees on the cost direction. **Smith's core debunk of the GOP-platform promises holds.**
6. **He is more honest than his reputation** — he concedes the surge is "a massive problem," a state/local "long-term fiscal problem," that housing "definitely does" rise, that low-skill mass inflow is an "altruistic luxury," and he criticizes a pro-immigration overclaim (Krugman) on the merits. A pure hit job that ignored Register B would be both dishonest and refutable from his own text.

**What does NOT survive is the IMPRESSION his corpus leaves**, assembled from Register A while Register B sits in another tab: that the local-incidence, distributional, state/local-fiscal, and capacity questions are settled-small. They are not. They are real, measured (rent incidence, school-age intensity, state/local education cost), concentrated on renters/competing-low-skill-workers/receiving cities, and Smith *demonstrably knows it* — he wrote it down in "America, please be reasonable." The dismantling is therefore not "Smith is wrong" but **"Smith's reassuring essays answer the national-average question and can then leave readers with the impression they answered the local-incidence and distributional question — which his own honest essay concedes they don't."** That is ledger-switching at corpus scale, and it is the fair, self-sourced charge.
