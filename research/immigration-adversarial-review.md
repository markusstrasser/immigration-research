# Immigration local/federal incidence analysis: adversarial review

Date: 2026-04-10

## Question

What would most seriously weaken or falsify the current working picture?

Current working picture:
1. Local burden heterogeneity is real.
2. Some recent low-skill origin groups are `school-heavy` in household structure; others are `housing-heavy` in destination mix.
3. The strongest Clark Center "agree" arguments are usually scope-limited rather than factually false.

## Adversarial target

The most vulnerable claim is not the existence of local burden heterogeneity. The most vulnerable claim is any stronger statement like:

1. `low-skill immigration is net negative overall`, or
2. `the economist papers are wrong`, or
3. `our current warehouse is already a full welfare ledger`.

Those stronger claims do not survive contact with the best counterarguments.

## Strongest adversarial objections

### 1. We still do not have a full national welfare ledger

This is the strongest attack.

Why it matters:
1. Our current warehouse is much better on local pressure than on federal tax incidence.
2. A real national-welfare statement needs direct taxes, indirect taxes, transfers, employer-side capital taxation, and general-equilibrium responses.
3. We do not have that yet.

Why this objection is strong:
1. Clemens argues influential cash-flow estimates undercount immigrant-linked capital-tax payments and therefore bias fiscal estimates downward. [SOURCE: https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/15592/the-fiscal-effect-of-immigration-reducing-bias-in-influential-estimates]
2. Colas and Sachs argue there are positive indirect fiscal effects from low-skilled immigration that standard direct-accounting frames miss. [SOURCE: https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/282044/1/352.pdf]
3. The National Academies framework is a serious long-run fiscal baseline, but even it is still a model with closure assumptions, not revealed truth. [SOURCE: https://d279m997dpfwgl.cloudfront.net/wp/2016/09/0922_immigrant-economics-full-report.pdf]

What follows:
1. We can defend `local-pressure heterogeneity`.
2. We cannot honestly defend `overall net negative` yet.

### 2. Housing-cost exposure is not the same thing as housing welfare loss

Why it matters:
1. Our PUMA rent layer measures exposure to expensive rental markets.
2. It does not by itself measure net welfare loss.
3. Higher rents can reflect amenity, productivity, supply constraints, transfers to owners, or actual crowding pressure.

Why this objection is strong:
1. HUD CHAS explicitly separates cost burden and overcrowding; our current stack does not yet. [SOURCE: https://www.huduser.gov/portal/datasets/cp.html]
2. AHS measures crowding, doubling up, unit quality, and tenure in ways that raw rent levels do not. [SOURCE: https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/ahs.html]
3. BEA RPP and FHFA HPI can help distinguish price level from asset-price dynamics, which our current rent-only framing compresses. [SOURCE: https://www.bea.gov/data/prices-inflation/regional-price-parities-state-and-metro-area] [SOURCE: https://www.fhfa.gov/house-price-index]

What follows:
1. The current `housing-heavy` label is directionally useful.
2. It is not yet a welfare number.

### 3. School burden is still too coarse without district and English-learner structure

Why it matters:
1. State-average school spending is too blunt.
2. Districts with high recent inflow can have very different per-pupil cost, English learner intensity, and migrant-student exposure.
3. The cost of one additional school-age child is not constant across districts.

Why this objection is strong:
1. NCES CCD and F-33 provide district-level membership and finance rather than state averages. [SOURCE: https://nces.ed.gov/ccd/pubageninfo.asp] [SOURCE: https://nces.ed.gov/ccd/f33agency.asp]
2. CCD/EDFacts explicitly track English language learner counts and migrant-student counts, which are first-order for the school-cost question. [SOURCE: https://www.ed.gov/data/edfacts-initiative] [SOURCE: https://nces.ed.gov/StatProg/handbook/ccd.asp]
3. SAIPE provides school-district estimates for children ages `5-17` and child poverty, which helps size local child concentration. [SOURCE: https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/saipe/technical-documentation/methodology/school-districts/overview-school-district.html]

What follows:
1. The broad finding that some origin mixes are more school-heavy probably survives.
2. The magnitude is still under-resolved.

### 4. ACS coverage and undercount still limit precision

Why it matters:
1. ACS is the right public microdata base for person/household structure.
2. It is not a census of immigrants.
3. It can undercount harder-to-reach populations and get thin in smaller origin cells.

Why this objection is strong:
1. The ACS PUMS User Guide makes clear these are survey microdata with weighting and confidentiality procedures, not administrative universe records. [SOURCE: https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs/microdata/documentation.html]
2. Brookings documents a related but separate warning that CPS is unusable for foreign-born population levels because of fixed population controls. That does not kill ACS, but it is a general warning against sloppy population inference from the wrong survey. [SOURCE: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/macroeconomic-implications-of-immigration-flows-in-2025-and-2026-january-2026-update/]

What follows:
1. Large-origin directional findings are usable.
2. Small-origin exact rankings should carry sample-risk flags.

### 5. We do not yet model native relocation, capital response, or local congestion directly

Why it matters:
1. Local burdens are endogenous.
2. Housing supply expands or does not.
3. Native households move out or in.
4. Congestion depends on actual commute networks, not just mean commute time.

Why this objection is strong:
1. LODES gives origin-destination commuting flows and worker/job geography at high spatial resolution; we are not using it yet. [SOURCE: https://lehd.ces.census.gov/data/#lodes]
2. IRS SOI migration data track state-to-state and county-to-county address changes on tax returns, which is a direct way to measure relocation rather than infer it. [SOURCE: https://www.irs.gov/statistics/soi-tax-stats-migration-data]
3. Current PUMA rent exposure is therefore still a partial equilibrium proxy. [INFERENCE]

What follows:
1. The local-pressure story is real.
2. The adjustment margins are not yet priced.

### 6. Legal-status and admission-channel heterogeneity is still mostly missing

Why it matters:
1. Refugee-like, family-based, and labor-based arrivals have different household profiles and short-run program exposure.
2. Origin alone is not enough.

Why this objection is strong:
1. OHSS administrative lawful permanent resident files already show admission-channel structure by country of birth and year. [SOURCE: https://www.dhs.gov/ohss]
2. ORR refugee arrivals and the Annual Survey of Refugees can identify flows whose early local-burden profile is structurally different from standard labor-migrant inflows. [SOURCE: https://acf.gov/archive/orr/data/refugee-arrival-data] [SOURCE: https://acf.gov/orr/programs/refugees/annual-survey-refugees]

What follows:
1. Some of our current origin-level school-heavy results may partly be admission-channel results.
2. That is a refinement issue, not a reason to ignore the pattern.

## Adversarial review of the economist-paper critique

### What does not hold up

1. `The main economist papers are factually wrong.`

I do not think that is the right claim.

The strongest papers we checked mostly do one of three things:
1. labor-market incidence,
2. beneficiary-group effects,
3. fiscal-accounting corrections.

On those narrower questions, they are usually not false.

### What does hold up

1. `These papers are often used to answer a broader question than they were built to answer.`

That critique survives.

Examples:
1. Card is about immigration and inequality, not a full local-fiscal ledger. [SOURCE: https://davidcard.berkeley.edu/papers/immigration-and-inequality.pdf]
2. Cortes and Tessada estimate the labor-supply effect on highly skilled women, not average-citizen welfare. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/app.3.3.88]
3. Bitler and Hoynes analyze safety-net interactions, not total incidence. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w17667]
4. Clemens and Lewis study firm/worker effects under specific restriction shocks, not the full general local-welfare question. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w30589]
5. Colas and Sachs expand the fiscal story, but they still do not substitute for a local school-housing-congestion account. [SOURCE: https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/282044/1/352.pdf]

## What survives the adversarial review

These claims still look defensible:

1. `Local burden heterogeneity is real.` [INFERENCE]
2. `Household composition matters at least as much as individual wage.` [INFERENCE]
3. `Some recent low-skill origin groups are clearly more school-heavy than others.` [INFERENCE from `acs_origin_household_national_2023`]
4. `Some other groups sort disproportionately into high-rent PUMAs and therefore face more local housing exposure than state averages imply.` [INFERENCE from `origin_puma_context_2023`]
5. `Using pro-immigration labor-market papers as though they settle the full local-fiscal question is an overreach.` [INFERENCE]

## What does not survive the adversarial review

These stronger claims should not be made yet:

1. `We have already shown low-skill immigration is net negative overall.`
2. `We have a complete federal-versus-local incidence decomposition.`
3. `Rent exposure equals housing welfare loss.`
4. `The economist papers are wrong rather than narrow.`

## Verdict

The right adversarial conclusion is:

1. Our current work is strong on discovering heterogeneity and scope mismatch.
2. Our current work is not yet strong enough for a clean national-welfare verdict.
3. The next stage should focus on district school burden, housing crowding/incidence, and a federal microsimulation.

## Best next datasets to defeat the remaining critiques

1. `NCES CCD/F-33/EDFacts` for district finance and English learner burden.
2. `HUD CHAS + AHS + SAFMR` for actual crowding and renter pressure.
3. `LEHD LODES + IRS SOI` for commuting and relocation adjustment.
4. `SIPP` for transfer/tax structure beyond ACS proxies.
5. `ORR/OHSS` for admission-channel decomposition.
