## 1. Immigration & Crime  (corpus has almost NONE — highest-priority gap)

1. **Ousey & Kubrin (2018), "Immigration and Crime: Assessing a Contentious Issue,"** *Annual Review
   of Criminology*. DOI 10.1146/annurev-criminol-032317-092026. [SOURCE: https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/pdf/10.1146/annurev-criminol-032317-092026] **OA.**
   THE field meta-review (310 cites): synthesizes ~50 macro-level studies, finds the immigration-crime
   association is null-to-negative on average. Anchors the whole crime section; tests the corpus's
   implicit "crime is a cost" prior.

2. **Light, He & Robey (2020), "Comparing crime rates between undocumented immigrants, legal
   immigrants, and native-born US citizens in Texas,"** *PNAS* 117(51):32340. DOI 10.1073/pnas.2014704117.
   [SOURCE: https://www.pnas.org/content/pnas/117/51/32340.full.pdf] **OA.**
   The single strongest US admin-data study (TX DPS arrests, immigration status at booking). VERIFIED
   this session: undocumented have ~half the violent-arrest, ~0.4× property, ~0.4× drug rate of natives.
   Directly tests the central crime claim with the cleanest available data. Disconfirms "illegal =
   high crime."

3. **Abramitzky, Boustan, Jácome, Pérez & Torres (2024), "Law-Abiding Immigrants: The Incarceration
   Gap Between Immigrants and the US-born, 1870–2020,"** SSRN/NBER w31440. DOI 10.2139/ssrn.4878020.
   [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w31440] **OA (NBER WP).**
   First nationally representative 150-yr incarceration series: immigrants below US-born for 150 years,
   gap WIDENING since 1960. Long-run, sending-country-robust. (Note: a 2023 SSRN draft used 1850 start
   and shows immigrants ABOVE US-born *whites* early-on — read both for the framing nuance.)

4. **Butcher & Piehl (2007), "Why Are Immigrants' Incarceration Rates So Low? Selective Immigration,
   Deterrence, and Deportation,"** NBER w13229. DOI 10.3386/w13229. [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.3386/w13229] **OA.**
   Landmark mechanism paper (181 cites): decomposes the low-incarceration fact into selection +
   deterrence (fear of deportation), not just composition. The mechanism the corpus needs to weigh
   any crime finding.

5. **Hausman (2020), "Sanctuary policies reduce deportations without increasing crime,"** *PNAS*
   117(44):27262. DOI 10.1073/pnas.2014673117. [SOURCE: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7959582] **OA.**
   Causal (matched county) test of the sanctuary-city crime claim — finds no crime effect. Directly
   tests a politically central assertion the corpus currently can't speak to.

6. **Kang & Song (2021), "Did Secure Communities Lead to Safer Communities? Immigration Enforcement,
   Crime Deterrence, and Geographical Externalities,"** *J. Law, Econ. & Org.* DOI 10.1093/jleo/ewab013. **PW.**
   The enforcement-side complement: staggered rollout of Secure Communities → tests whether deporting
   "criminal aliens" reduced crime. Largely null. Steel-mans AND tests the enforcement-works frame.

7. **Gunadi (2019), "On the association between undocumented immigration and crime in the United
   States,"** *Oxford Economic Papers*. DOI 10.1093/oep/gpz057. **PW (abstract OA).**
   Economist's take using institutionalization rates of the likely-undocumented; finds no positive
   crime link. Independent method/data from Light → triangulation.

   [GAP] Cato (Nowrasteh) TX-conviction-rate working papers did NOT surface in S2/OpenAlex (gray lit).
   Suggested next: direct fetch from cato.org "Criminal Immigrants in Texas" (Nowrasteh 2018, 2021
   updates) via WebFetch/save_source — these are the most-cited *conviction*-based (not arrest) numbers.

## 2. Second-generation / intergenerational mobility & long-run fiscal

8. **Abramitzky, Boustan, Jácome & Pérez (2021), "Intergenerational Mobility of Immigrants in the
   United States over Two Centuries,"** *American Economic Review* 111(2):580. DOI 10.1257/aer.20191586.
   (NBER w26408 OA: https://doi.org/10.3386/w26408) **WP OA, journal PW.**
   THE landmark (117 cites): children of immigrants from nearly every origin out-mobilize children of
   the US-born, historically and today. Decisive against the static "low-skill parent → permanent
   fiscal drain" assumption — the 2nd-gen catch-up is the load-bearing fiscal-NPV variable.

9. **Boustan, Jensen, Abramitzky, Jácome, Manning, Pérez et al. (2025), "Intergenerational Mobility
   of Immigrants in 15 Destination Countries,"** SSRN. DOI 10.2139/ssrn.5142323. **PW.**
   2025 cross-country extension — tests whether the US 2nd-gen advantage generalizes (it largely does
   in mobile-society destinations). Bounds external validity of paper #8.

   [GAP] Dustmann's long-run/return-migration + 2nd-gen fiscal work (e.g. Dustmann & Frattini 2014
   "The Fiscal Effects of Immigration to the UK," *Economic Journal*; Dustmann, Frattini & Halls on
   A8) did not surface cleanly in these queries. Suggested next: targeted S2 query "Dustmann Frattini
   fiscal effects immigration UK Economic Journal" — UK EEA-positive-NPV finding is a key disconfirmer
   of the US cost-skew when transferred carefully.

## 3. Recent dynamic / GE fiscal-NPV models

10. **Hansen, Schultz-Nielsen & Tranæs (2017), "The fiscal impact of immigration to welfare states of
    the Scandinavian type,"** *J. Population Economics*. DOI 10.1007/s00148-017-0636-1.
    [SOURCE: https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007%2Fs00148-017-0636-1.pdf] **OA.**
    Lifetime-NPV by origin in a generous-welfare state — the hard case for fiscal-positive claims;
    non-Western low-skill cohorts net-negative even with descendants. The disconfirming-direction
    fiscal model the corpus's NAS-centric view should be stress-tested against (with US-vs-DK welfare
    caveat).

    [GAP] A current (2022-26) US-specific dynamic/GE fiscal-NPV with second-generation feedback did NOT
    cleanly surface (search burst cut off). Suggested next queries: "Colas Sachs immigration fiscal
    descendants" (corpus already has Colas-Sachs wage piece — check for a fiscal companion); "Storesletten
    dynamic fiscal immigration selective" (corpus has the 2000 piece — find post-2020 successors);
    "CBO 2024 immigration surge budgetary effects dynamic" (the live policy-relevant projection).
