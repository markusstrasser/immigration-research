# Immigration Fiscal Data: Why the Averages Mislead

Date: 2026-03-13

Question: what articles, essays, and studies explain why simple immigration fiscal averages can be deceptive, especially for unauthorized or mixed-status flows?

Instrument note: this is a politically charged topic. The repo’s bias caveat applies. I tried to counter that by mixing government reports, academic papers, and ideologically opposed policy shops. [SOURCE: https://www.nap.edu/catalog/23550/the-economic-and-fiscal-consequences-of-immigration] [SOURCE: https://www.aei.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/The-Fiscal-Impact-of-Immigration-An-Update.pdf?x85095=] [SOURCE: https://media4.manhattan-institute.org/wp-content/uploads/the-lifetime-fiscal-impact-of-immigrants.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.cato.org/sites/cato.org/files/2024-11/working-paper-82-update.pdf]

## Bottom line

The data are not fake. They are lower-dimensional than the argument people want to make from them. [INFERENCE]

The main reasons the headline numbers mislead are:

1. unauthorized status is not directly observed in survey data and is inferred with residual methods that are sensitive to assumptions about emigration, coverage, and legal-status classification. [SOURCE: https://www.ssa.gov/policy/docs/ssb/v85n2/v85n2p1.html] [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9107075/] [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12439705/]
2. fiscal results move a lot depending on whether you assign immigrants the average cost of public goods or the marginal cost. [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704] [SOURCE: https://www.nap.edu/catalog/23550/the-economic-and-fiscal-consequences-of-immigration]
3. federal and state/local ledgers point in different directions, so a single national average conceals who actually bears the burden. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60165] [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256] [SOURCE: https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/90796/state_and_local_fiscal_effects_of_immigration.pdf]
4. national averages wash out local shock-load effects in shelters, schools, and county public services. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w33655] [SOURCE: https://www.banqueducanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/swp2023-57.pdf]
5. static one-year or point-in-time estimates overstate current child-related costs if they do not also count future tax contributions from the children being educated. [SOURCE: https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/90796/state_and_local_fiscal_effects_of_immigration.pdf] [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704]

## Best Reading Pack

### 1. National Academies (2017), *The Economic and Fiscal Consequences of Immigration*

This is the canonical baseline. Most later policy papers are arguing about its assumptions, extensions, or updates. The key thing to take from it is not one number but the structure of the problem: public-goods allocation, short-run versus long-run accounting, federal versus state/local incidence, and first-generation versus second-generation attribution. [SOURCE: https://www.nap.edu/catalog/23550/the-economic-and-fiscal-consequences-of-immigration]

Use it for: the original map of the terrain.

### 2. Pia Orrenius (2017), *New Findings on the Fiscal Impact of Immigration in the United States*

This is the cleanest short paper on why the fiscal numbers swing so much. Orrenius states directly that many fiscal studies are incomplete or misleading, that the public-goods assumption is pivotal, and that to the extent net costs exist they are concentrated at the state and local level and largely tied to schooling. [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704]

Use it for: average-cost versus marginal-cost reasoning, and the federal-positive/state-local-negative split.

### 3. Kim Rueben and Sarah Gault (2017), *State and Local Fiscal Effects of Immigration*

Best explainer for why local ledgers look worse than national ones. The authors emphasize that state/local results depend heavily on cost attribution, tax structure, and the number of dependent children. They also note that their static state/local estimates do not capture future tax contributions from educated children and are therefore conservative in the sense of overstating current costs and understating later benefits. [SOURCE: https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/90796/state_and_local_fiscal_effects_of_immigration.pdf]

Use it for: fiscal federalism, cross-state heterogeneity, and why local snapshots are incomplete.

### 4. Congressional Budget Office (2024), *Effects of the Immigration Surge on the Federal Budget and the Economy*

Read this because it fixes one common mistake in online debate: CBO’s recent federal result is about the `2021–2026` surge of “other foreign nationals,” not a pure all-illegal ledger. It finds a large federal net positive over `2024–2034`, but it also says the main deficit estimate omits discretionary responses and explicitly notes likely pressure on immigration agencies, housing assistance, WIC, and school-related aid requests. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60165]

Use it for: mixed-status denominator caveat, federal-positive result, and omitted discretionary pressures.

### 5. Congressional Budget Office (2025), *Effects of the Surge in Immigration on State and Local Budgets in 2023*

This is the best primary source for recent direct local costs. It separates direct effects from a broader “potential effects” measure that tries to account for crowding and costs borne without matching budget outlays. It identifies education, shelter, and border security as the biggest recent cost categories. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]

Use it for: recent state/local costs, direct versus potential effects, and category breakdowns.

### 6. Harriet Duleep, Dave Shoffner, Robert Gesumaria, and Christopher Tamborini (2025), *Measuring the Number of Unauthorized Immigrants in the United States: A Review of the Residual Estimation Method*

This is the best recent methods overview. It walks through the residual method step by step and makes the core measurement problem explicit: “illegal” status is inferred by subtracting estimated legal immigrants from estimated total foreign-born residents. That should make you suspicious of any false precision in “average illegal immigrant costs X” claims. [SOURCE: https://www.ssa.gov/policy/docs/ssb/v85n2/v85n2p1.html]

Use it for: measurement discipline and the basic reason status-specific averages are hard.

### 7. Jennifer Van Hook, Anne Morse, Randy Capps, and Julia Gelatt (2021), *Uncertainty About the Size of the Unauthorized Foreign-Born Population in the United States*

If you want the best paper on uncertainty itself, read this. It argues residual estimates often cluster in a relatively narrow range, but that plausible uncertainty bands are still wide and are driven mostly by assumptions about emigration among legal permanent residents, naturalized citizens, and humanitarian entrants. [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9107075/]

Use it for: how much uncertainty is ordinary, and why small differences in headline estimates should not be overread.

### 8. Robert Warren, John Robert Warren, and Ping Zheng (2023/2025 PMC availability), *A New Residual Approach for Estimating Undocumented Populations*

This is the best paper on why different organizations started disagreeing more after 2010. The authors argue that much of the divergence comes from stale emigration assumptions. That matters because if your denominator is unstable, your per-person fiscal average is unstable too. [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12439705/]

Use it for: why different unauthorized-population estimates diverge even before the fiscal argument starts.

### 9. Anna Maria Mayda, Mine Z. Senses, and Walter Steingress (2023), *Immigration and Provision of Public Goods: Evidence at the Local Level in the U.S.*

This is the most useful adjacent-domain academic paper for your “multicult burdens” intuition, because it studies local public-service provision rather than only taxes and transfers. The key result is heterogeneous: low-skilled inflows reduce per-capita public expenditures while high-skilled inflows increase them, and these effects offset on average but produce unequal county outcomes where immigrant skill mix differs. [SOURCE: https://www.banqueducanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/swp2023-57.pdf]

Use it for: local public-goods strain, heterogeneity by skill mix, and why national averaging hides county-level losers.

### 10. Bruce Meyer, Angela Wyse, and Douglas Williams (2025), *Asylum Seekers and the Rise in Homelessness*

This is the clearest recent paper on local shock-load costs. It estimates that asylum seekers accounted for about 60 percent of the `2022–2024` rise in sheltered homelessness, with about three-quarters of the increase concentrated in four localities. That is exactly the kind of concentrated burden a national per-person average smooths away. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w33655]

Use it for: shelter-system overload and why “the average national cost” can miss the places actually absorbing the surge.

## Debate Pieces Worth Reading After the Core Pack

### 11. Pia Orrenius, Alan Viard, and Madeline Zavodny (2025), *The Fiscal Impact of Immigration: An Update*

This is the best concise recent policy synthesis from a center-right, economics-first perspective. It argues that immigration is overall fiscally positive, that low-skilled immigration can still be directly negative, and that indirect fiscal effects from capital taxation improve the picture relative to older models. It also states plainly that smaller inflows might reduce state/local pressure while increasing federal pressure and slowing growth. [SOURCE: https://www.aei.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/The-Fiscal-Impact-of-Immigration-An-Update.pdf?x85095=]

Use it for: the most defensible recent “overall positive, locally mixed” synthesis.

### 12. Daniel Di Martino (2024), *The Lifetime Fiscal Impact of Immigrants*

This is the strongest sophisticated pessimistic model I found. It is worth reading because it shows the best version of the high-cost case rather than a caricature. But note that even this report says the average new immigrant is federally positive overall, while specific subgroups are deeply negative. [SOURCE: https://media4.manhattan-institute.org/wp-content/uploads/the-lifetime-fiscal-impact-of-immigrants.pdf]

Use it for: the strongest structured argument for a large long-run fiscal downside.

### 13. David Bier (2024), *Manhattan Institute’s “Lifetime Fiscal Impact of Immigrants” Report Shows Upside to Immigration*

Read this immediately after Di Martino. Bier accepts that Manhattan built a serious model, then argues the big negative results rely on unlikely assumptions like immediate defense-spending increases and zero corporate-tax feedback. This is the cleanest adversarial audit of a high-cost model. [SOURCE: https://www.cato.org/sites/cato.org/files/2024-11/working-paper-82-update.pdf]

Use it for: stress-testing whether dramatic negative totals survive assumption changes.

### 14. Steven Camarota (CIS) (2024), *The Cost of Illegal Immigration to Taxpayers*

This is worth reading only as an adversarial brief. It is useful because it shows the strongest structured version of the restrictionist fiscal case in congressional-testimony form. It is not useful as a neutral baseline estimate. In particular, do not treat its `$68,390` lifetime drain estimate as a National Academies result; it is a CIS-constructed number built from NAS scenarios plus CIS adjustments. See the local audit for exact use rules. [SOURCE: https://www.congress.gov/118/meeting/house/116727/witnesses/HHRG-118-JU01-Wstate-CamarotaS-20240111.pdf] [SOURCE: https://cis.org/Center-For-Immigration-Studies-Background] [SOURCE: https://www.nap.edu/catalog/23550/the-economic-and-fiscal-consequences-of-immigration]

Use it for: adversarial framing, cost-category surfacing, and checking what the strongest anti-immigration brief actually claims.

## What these sources say is actually deceptive

The following claims are well supported across the pack:

1. `“Average illegal immigrant fiscal cost”` is usually not directly observed. It is built on an inferred status population, so denominator uncertainty enters before the spending side is even estimated. [SOURCE: https://www.ssa.gov/policy/docs/ssb/v85n2/v85n2p1.html] [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9107075/] [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12439705/]
2. `“National net fiscal impact”` is not the same thing as `“who gets stuck paying now.”` Federal and local ledgers differ, and recent surge costs are concentrated in a small number of jurisdictions. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60165] [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256] [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w33655]
3. `“Static current costs”` can overstate long-run net burden if they count schooling now but do not count later tax contributions from the same children. [SOURCE: https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/90796/state_and_local_fiscal_effects_of_immigration.pdf] [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704]
4. `“Per-person average cost”` depends heavily on whether you allocate pure public goods at average cost or marginal cost. That is a modeling choice, not a discovered fact. [SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.24149/wp1704] [SOURCE: https://www.nap.edu/catalog/23550/the-economic-and-fiscal-consequences-of-immigration]
5. `“No hidden local burden exists because the national average is modest”` is wrong. Shelter, school, and local public-service congestion can be very real and very concentrated. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w33655] [SOURCE: https://www.banqueducanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/swp2023-57.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]
6. `“NAS found each illegal immigrant costs about $68k over a lifetime”` is false. That scalar is a CIS construction layered on top of NAS scenario outputs, not a National Academies estimate for unauthorized immigrants. [SOURCE: https://www.congress.gov/118/meeting/house/116727/witnesses/HHRG-118-JU01-Wstate-CamarotaS-20240111.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.nap.edu/catalog/23550/the-economic-and-fiscal-consequences-of-immigration]

## Disconfirmation

To avoid turning “the data are deceptive” into a lazy slogan, here is what cuts the other way:

1. the residual method is imperfect, but not worthless. Multiple papers treat it as the standard starting point, and the broad order of magnitude of the unauthorized population is still usable. [SOURCE: https://www.ssa.gov/policy/docs/ssb/v85n2/v85n2p1.html] [SOURCE: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12439705/]
2. average effects can still be informative for macro questions. Mayda, Senses, and Steingress find offsetting low-skill and high-skill effects on average even while county outcomes diverge. [SOURCE: https://www.banqueducanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/swp2023-57.pdf]
3. some primary-source work already tries to capture hidden burdens. CBO’s 2025 local report includes a broader “potential effects” measure rather than only explicit spending. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]
4. the strongest pessimistic model I found still says the average new immigrant is federally positive overall; the dispute is mainly over subgroup composition and assumption stacks, not whether every immigrant is a net drag. [SOURCE: https://media4.manhattan-institute.org/wp-content/uploads/the-lifetime-fiscal-impact-of-immigrants.pdf]

Adjacent-domain axis: local public-goods provision and homelessness/shelter overload rather than only tax-and-transfer ledgers. [SOURCE: https://www.banqueducanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/swp2023-57.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/papers/w33655]

## Practical takeaway

If we keep using one national per-person number as the answer, we will keep arguing past the real question. The better frame is:

1. how uncertain is the unauthorized denominator,
2. what happens under average-cost versus marginal-cost public goods,
3. which ledger we mean: federal or state/local,
4. which places are absorbing nonlinear shelter, school, or border-response costs,
5. which assumption stack is required to produce very large negative totals.

That is the stack the current debate keeps collapsing into one fake-clean scalar. [INFERENCE]
