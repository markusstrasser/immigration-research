# Full-Spectrum Costs of Unauthorized Immigration — Research Memo

**Question:** What are the real long-tail, long-term costs of unauthorized immigration if we stop pretending the problem is only a tax-and-transfer ledger?
**Tier:** Deep | **Date:** 2026-03-13
**Scope:** This memo broadens the prior fiscal ledger into a full-spectrum cost map: direct public budgets, congestion and adaptation costs, informal-economy distortions, and political-economy / social-capital externalities.

**Instrument caveat:** Immigration is a framing-sensitive topic. Hard numerical claims and official budget data are more trustworthy than narrative claims about cohesion, identity, or institutional decay. [SOURCE: `notes/llm-bias-caveat.md`]

---

## Bottom Line

The strongest additional long-tail costs beyond the standard fiscal ledger are not abstract "civilizational" costs. They are:

1. **Local congestion and adaptation costs** in schools, shelters, courts, health systems, and agency language-access systems. These are real and partly measurable. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256] [SOURCE: https://languageaccess.courts.ca.gov/system/files/2025-07/2025%20Language%20Need%20and%20Interpreter%20Use%20Study.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.courts.wa.gov/content/Financial%20Services/documents/2025_2027/Biennial/BD%20Stabilize%20Interpreter%20Reimbursement%20Program.pdf]
2. **Informal-economy distortions**: wage theft, worker misclassification, off-books labor, and tax erosion in sectors that rely heavily on precarious labor. These are real but are usually measured through employer behavior rather than immigrant status directly. [SOURCE: https://www.umass.edu/labor/research/working-paper-series/social-and-economic-costs-illegal-misclassification-wage-theft-and-tax-fraud-residential/4-path-citizenship-immigrant-workers] [SOURCE: https://clje.law.harvard.edu/app/uploads/2019/06/misclassification_and_payroll_fraud.pdf]
3. **Political-economy backlash**: lower support for redistribution, stronger anti-immigration politics, policy churn, and litigation / compliance overhead. These effects are real in the literature, but they are weak candidates for a clean per-person dollar estimate. [SOURCE: https://www.hbs.edu/ris/download.aspx?name=Alesina+and+Tabellini_May2022.pdf] [SOURCE: https://economics.uci.edu/files/docs/micro/s11/Edmark.pdf]

The weakest part of the "full-spectrum cost" discourse is vague talk about "loss of cohesion" without a mechanism. If the mechanism cannot be decomposed into courts, schools, policing, housing, public-goods support, or institutional compliance, it is not yet a research variable. [INFERENCE]

The evidence currently supports **widening the negative tail** relative to a narrow fiscal ledger, mainly through local congestion and adaptation costs. It does **not** support inventing a single precise giant number for "true total cost." [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256] [SOURCE: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2959985]

## What Counts as a "Real" Cost

There are at least four different ledgers:

1. **Public-budget costs**: what governments spend.
2. **Private real-resource costs**: waiting, crowding, displacement, lower service quality, extra compliance time.
3. **Market-distortion costs**: informal labor, wage theft, tax evasion, misallocation of labor and housing.
4. **Political-economy costs**: lower willingness to redistribute, backlash, administrative and litigation churn.

If these are collapsed into one scalar without separating mechanisms, double counting becomes unavoidable. For example, school crowding can appear in education budgets, teacher time, test outcomes, parental flight, and later politics. That does not make them five independent costs. [INFERENCE]

## High-Confidence Long-Tail Costs

### 1. Local congestion and adaptation costs

This is the cleanest answer to "what is missing from narrow fiscal ledgers?" CBO's 2025 state/local report is explicit that there are costs that are **borne without immediately appearing as spending**, including crowding in public schools and public transportation systems. That is exactly the sort of long-tail cost people usually mean but rarely define. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]

This class includes:

- larger class loads and more English-learner support burden in K-12 systems, [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]
- shelter and related services, including local emergency intake systems, [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]
- border-security response at the state/local level, [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]
- interpreter scheduling, reimbursement, and recruitment in courts and agencies, [SOURCE: https://languageaccess.courts.ca.gov/system/files/2025-07/2025%20Language%20Need%20and%20Interpreter%20Use%20Study.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.courts.wa.gov/content/Financial%20Services/documents/2025_2027/Biennial/BD%20Stabilize%20Interpreter%20Reimbursement%20Program.pdf]
- paperwork, compliance, and outreach obligations under language-access law. [SOURCE: https://www.uscis.gov/sites/default/files/document/brochures/USCIS_Language_Access_Plan-November_2024.pdf] [SOURCE: https://ohr.dc.gov/sites/default/files/dc/sites/ohr/OHR_LanguageAccess_FY23_Report_Nov2024-Final2.pdf]

The key point is that these are **real but local**. They do not necessarily show up in federal net-present-value exercises, and they do not distribute evenly across places. [INFERENCE]

### 2. Court-system friction

Immigration and related court systems experience backlog, counsel shortages, language barriers, and notice failures. TRAC documents enormous immigration court backlogs and judge workload pressure. Newer legal scholarship documents serious language and notice barriers in accelerated dockets. These are not just humanitarian problems; they are resource-allocation costs inside adjudication systems. [SOURCE: https://trac.syr.edu/immigration/reports/637/] [SOURCE: https://trac.syr.edu/immigration/reports/558/] [SOURCE: https://journals.law.harvard.edu/lalr/wp-content/uploads/sites/85/2026/01/HLALR-Vol.-28-Hidden-Barriers-to-Entry-4.pdf]

### 3. Informal-economy tax erosion and labor-law degradation

The strongest version of this argument is not "immigrants evade taxes"; it is that undocumented status creates bargaining asymmetries that employers can exploit through misclassification, wage theft, payroll fraud, and off-books subcontracting. UMass documents this mechanism in residential construction, and Harvard's labor-law review shows that misclassification and payroll fraud are a substantial enforcement problem generally. [SOURCE: https://www.umass.edu/labor/research/working-paper-series/social-and-economic-costs-illegal-misclassification-wage-theft-and-tax-fraud-residential/4-path-citizenship-immigrant-workers] [SOURCE: https://clje.law.harvard.edu/app/uploads/2019/06/misclassification_and_payroll_fraud.pdf]

This is a real long-tail cost channel because it hits:

- payroll tax collection,
- workers' compensation and unemployment systems,
- compliant employers who face unfair competition,
- downstream workplace injury and enforcement costs.

[INFERENCE from the two reports above]

## Medium-Confidence Long-Tail Costs

### 4. Health-system distortion

The health-care story is more complicated than popular rhetoric suggests. A national AJPH analysis found immigrants had substantially lower per-capita health expenditures than U.S.-born persons overall, although immigrant children had much higher ED expenditures. More recent work finds that restrictive enforcement environments can worsen ambulatory-care-sensitive hospitalizations among children, suggesting that immigration policy can create inefficient care pathways. [SOURCE: https://hia.berkeley.edu/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/2005_healthcare_exp.pdf] [SOURCE: https://scholars.duke.edu/publication/1476491]

The defensible claim is therefore:

- **yes**, emergency and delayed-care channels are real,
- **no**, the best evidence does not support treating immigrants as a uniformly disproportionate health-cost burden,
- **yes**, fear, exclusion, and weak primary-care access can shift care into more expensive settings.

[INFERENCE]

### 5. Housing crowding and code enforcement

Housing pressure is real, but this is one of the easiest channels to over-attribute. CBO counts shelter and related services directly. Local housing and code-enforcement systems document real administrative burden from overcrowding and maintenance violations. But without local microdata linking occupancy patterns, legal status, and enforcement records, this remains a partially measured channel rather than a clean unauthorized-immigration estimate. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256] [SOURCE: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/OLO/Resources/Files/2025_reports/OLOReport2025-6.pdf]

The serious version of the argument is "local capacity and housing scarcity turn migration inflows into code-enforcement and overcrowding costs," not "immigration automatically raises housing costs by X everywhere." [INFERENCE]

## Low-Confidence but Potentially Important Costs

### 6. Redistribution backlash and political churn

The political-economy literature is much stronger than the simple trust literature. Alesina and Tabellini review evidence that immigration often increases support for anti-immigrant parties and can lower preferences for redistribution and diversity among natives. Dahlberg, Edmark, and Lundqvist find that increased immigrant shares reduced support for redistribution in Sweden under plausibly exogenous placement patterns. [SOURCE: https://www.hbs.edu/ris/download.aspx?name=Alesina+and+Tabellini_May2022.pdf] [SOURCE: https://economics.uci.edu/files/docs/micro/s11/Edmark.pdf]

That is a **real long-run cost channel** if it causes:

- worse public-goods provision,
- more policy oscillation,
- more litigation and compliance churn,
- lower willingness to fund schools, transit, and welfare systems.

But this is a poor candidate for a per-person lifetime dollar estimate. It is aggregate, path-dependent, and mediated by media systems, party competition, and local contact conditions. [INFERENCE]

### 7. Generalized trust and "cohesion"

This is where most bad arguments live. Portes and Vickstrom's review explicitly pushes back on simplistic claims that immigration and diversity straightforwardly destroy social cohesion. Cohesion in complex societies does not require homogeneity, and contact can offset or reverse some negative priors. [SOURCE: https://www.ias.edu/sites/default/files/sss/BordersPortes-Diversity-Social-Capital.pdf]

The responsible conclusion is:

- some diversity shocks can reduce trust or redistribution support in some settings, [SOURCE: https://economics.uci.edu/files/docs/micro/s11/Edmark.pdf]
- but "less cohesion due to unshared norms" is not yet a monetized variable unless it is decomposed into operational mechanisms. [INFERENCE]

Mark such claims `[FRAMING-SENSITIVE]`.

## What the Narrow Fiscal Memo Probably Missed

Relative to a narrow federal-plus-state/local budget ledger, the likely omitted negative channels are:

1. **Nonbudgetary congestion costs** already acknowledged by CBO but not fully priced in many advocacy ledgers. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]
2. **Interpreter / language-access systems** that are real administrative overhead but usually not allocated per immigrant in national estimates. [SOURCE: https://languageaccess.courts.ca.gov/system/files/2025-07/2025%20Language%20Need%20and%20Interpreter%20Use%20Study.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.courts.wa.gov/content/Financial%20Services/documents/2025_2027/Biennial/BD%20Stabilize%20Interpreter%20Reimbursement%20Program.pdf]
3. **Informal-labor tax erosion** and employer-side fraud enabled by precarious status. [SOURCE: https://www.umass.edu/labor/research/working-paper-series/social-and-economic-costs-illegal-misclassification-wage-theft-and-tax-fraud-residential/4-path-citizenship-immigrant-workers] [SOURCE: https://clje.law.harvard.edu/app/uploads/2019/06/misclassification_and_payroll_fraud.pdf]
4. **Political backlash effects** that can reduce support for redistribution and raise governance costs. [SOURCE: https://www.hbs.edu/ris/download.aspx?name=Alesina+and+Tabellini_May2022.pdf]

The narrow memo probably **did not** miss a well-established giant hidden number for "civilizational decline." That part of the discourse is mostly a failure to specify mechanism and a temptation to convert mood into arithmetic. [INFERENCE]

## What We Still Cannot Know Cleanly

1. **Unauthorized-status attribution problem:** many overhead costs are observed for LEP populations, low-income households, asylum seekers, or foreign-born populations, not unauthorized Mexican immigrants specifically. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/publication/61256]
2. **Residual-method error:** the underlying status identification problem remains substantial in public microdata. [SOURCE: `research/immigration-fiscal-impact-unauthorized-memo.md`]
3. **Double-counting risk:** a cost can appear in budgets, wait times, school outcomes, and later politics. Adding them all as if independent is wrong. [INFERENCE]
4. **Policy-response endogeneity:** some of the measured "costs of immigration" are really costs of the way governments choose to administer immigration, benefits, detention, or access to care. [SOURCE: https://scholars.duke.edu/publication/1476491] [SOURCE: https://www.uscis.gov/sites/default/files/document/brochures/USCIS_Language_Access_Plan-November_2024.pdf]
5. **Context dependence:** political backlash and trust effects vary by pace of inflow, local institutions, media, and exposure to intergroup contact. [SOURCE: https://www.hbs.edu/ris/download.aspx?name=Alesina+and+Tabellini_May2022.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.ias.edu/sites/default/files/sss/BordersPortes-Diversity-Social-Capital.pdf]

## Best Current Judgment

If the question is "what are the real long-tail costs that narrow fiscal ledgers understate?", the answer is:

- **Yes**: local congestion, language-access overhead, court backlog, shelter pressure, housing/code-enforcement burden, and informal-labor tax erosion are real and belong in the picture.
- **Yes**: immigration can generate political backlash and lower support for redistribution; that is a real long-run social cost in some settings.
- **No**: the current literature does not justify converting all of that into a clean per-person lifetime NPV with high confidence.
- **No**: the strongest evidence does not support replacing one narrow advocacy number with a bigger and more rhetorically satisfying advocacy number.

The honest upgrade is not "find the true giant number." It is to move from a narrow fiscal ledger to a **stacked cost framework**:

1. budgetary costs and revenues,
2. nonbudgetary congestion and adaptation costs,
3. informal-economy distortion costs,
4. political-economy and trust costs, clearly marked as weakly identified.

That framework is more negative than a narrow tax-transfer ledger, but still does not license fake precision. [INFERENCE]
