## The position, steelmanned in one paragraph

The Card-Peri canon is **the strongest empirical edifice in the whole pro-immigration corpus, and the repo's own evidence is built on it.** Card (1990) showed the Mariel boatlift increased the Miami labor force by **7%** essentially overnight yet had "virtually no effect on the wages or unemployment rates of less-skilled workers" — a clean natural experiment that broke the textbook supply-curve prediction. Peri (with Ottaviano, Sparber, Foged) supplied the *mechanism*: immigrants and natives are **imperfect substitutes** even within an education-experience cell, because immigrants specialize in manual/physical tasks and push natives toward communication/language-intensive tasks they hold a comparative advantage in. Put together: a labor-supply shock that would crater wages under perfect substitution instead reallocates *tasks*, so the average native wage is unmoved or slightly *raised*. Ottaviano-Peri (2012) quantify it: the average US-born wage rose **+0.6% to +0.7%** over 1990-2004 from immigration. This is not ideology — it is a documented, replicated, mechanism-backed empirical regularity, and **the repo confirms every load-bearing piece of it** (Mariel-as-artifact ladder 44; E-Verify null ladder 17; Foged-Peri ladder 19). The honest verdict, stated up front: **Card-Peri are right that wage-doom is overstated.** The canon fails only where a *small average over observed inflows* is laundered into *"no one is hurt"* and where *marginal-policy results* are stretched to a *mass/surge* regime they were never estimated on.

**The crucial asymmetry that makes the dismantling fair AND devastating: Card-Peri are right about the AVERAGE and right about the MECHANISM, and the canon is wrong only in three specific scope-stretches — the *area-study method's* fragility, the *distribution* hidden inside the average, and the *marginal→surge* extrapolation. Every kill is one of those three, and none touches the granted core.**

## Claim-by-claim

### Claim 1 — "The Mariel boatlift proves a huge labor-supply shock has no effect on native wages." (Card's area-study method as proof)

> "Mariel immigrants increased the Miami labor force by 7%, and the result was… virtually no effect on the wages or unemployment rates of less-skilled workers, even among Cubans who had immigrated earlier." [SOURCE: Card 1990, verbatim]

**(a) Steelman.** A 7% overnight labor-force shock concentrated in exactly the low-skill segment (Cuban dropouts) is the closest thing labor economics has to a controlled supply experiment. Card compared Miami to four demographically-similar comparison cities (Atlanta, Houston, LA, Tampa-St. Pete) and found the wage and unemployment paths of less-skilled Miami workers — Black, Hispanic, white, *and* earlier Cuban immigrants — were indistinguishable from the controls. The textbook downward-sloping-demand prediction simply failed.

**(b) Verdict: the FINDING is GRANTED and repo-confirmed; the METHOD has a real, valid endogeneity critique that the canon waves away.** The Mariel *null itself* survives — and the repo went further and **killed the main attack on it.** Borjas (2017) reanalyzed the same data and claimed a −10 to −30% wage drop for HS dropouts; ladder entry 44 establishes (Clemens-Hunt 2019, full text) that this magnitude **survives only under a 4-way knife-cut** (non-Hispanic men 25-59, dropouts) discarding **91% of low-skill Miami workers** down to **~17 observations/year**, riding a **55pp black-share jump** from the simultaneous Haitian-refugee arrival and a March-1981 CPS sampling-frame change; a schooling-as-outcome falsification (the boatlift spuriously "causes" −0.444 years of schooling) confirms compositional contamination. **So the repo's verdict is that Card is right and Borjas's Mariel reappraisal is the artifact.** [SOURCE: 10.3386/w23433; ladder 44]

**What is GRANTED:** Card's Mariel wage null is the *correct reading of that episode*. Peri-Yasenov's synthetic control agrees (~null). This is the granted core — do not touch it.

**Where the canon OVERCLAIMS — the EXACT move: Move 3 (marginal-to-mass extrapolation) riding on a real method critique.** The canon uses Mariel-as-*method* — "city-comparison studies show no effect" — as a general proof that supply shocks don't move wages. But the *area-study research design itself* has an endogeneity problem that is **Borjas's one genuinely valid methodological point**, and it is logically independent of whether his Mariel *magnitude* is right (it isn't):

- **Native out-migration + capital mobility wash out the *local* signal.** Borjas (w11610, full text, verbatim): *"the native migration response can account for between 40 to 60 percent of the difference in the measured wage impact of immigration between the national and local labor market level."* Natives respond to an immigrant inflow by **lower in-migration, higher out-migration, and slower native-workforce growth** in the affected city — so a cross-city comparison captures only the *residual* wage effect after arbitrage has already redistributed the shock nationally. A local null is therefore *consistent with* a non-zero national effect; the area study cannot distinguish "no effect" from "effect dissipated geographically." [SOURCE: nber.org/papers/w11610.pdf, verbatim]
- This is the methodological point the repo **grants as valid even while rejecting Borjas's Mariel number** (`immigration-restrictionist-arguments-steelman-2026-06-15.md` lines 38-39, ladder-adjacent). It is *not* a defense of "10-30% harm"; it is the reason a single-city null is not a *general* proof.

**Verdict on Claim 1: the Mariel null is GRANTED (repo-confirmed; Borjas's contrary magnitude is the artifact). The OVERCLAIM is treating area-study nulls as a clean *general* proof — the design's native-out-migration endogeneity (40-60% local attenuation, Borjas w11610) means a local null under-identifies the national effect.** The fair sharp question: *"Card's Miami null is real — but your design lets natives and capital flee the treated city. Borjas shows native migration washes out 40-60% of the local-vs-national gap. How do you know Mariel measured 'no effect' rather than 'effect exported to the rest of the labor market'?"* (Note: this grants Borjas the *method* point while the repo simultaneously denies him the *Mariel magnitude* — these are separable, and holding both is the honest position.)

### Claim 2 — "Task complementarity means the average native wage effect is ~zero or positive, so immigration doesn't hurt native workers." (Peri's mechanism → welfare conclusion)

> "in the short run (as of 2004) the average wage of U.S. native workers increased moderately (+0.7%)… in the long run the average wage of U.S.-born workers [rose]… This result stems from the imperfect substitutability between U.S.- and foreign-born workers." [SOURCE: Ottaviano-Peri w12497, verbatim]

**(a) Steelman.** Ottaviano-Peri build a nested-CES general-equilibrium model and *estimate* (rather than assume) the immigrant-native substitution elasticity within education-experience cells, finding it finite — immigrants are imperfect substitutes for natives even at the same skill level (σ ≈ 20, not ∞). With capital adjusting in the long run, the average US-born wage *rises* +0.6%. Peri-Sparber (2009) supply the micro-foundation: immigrants concentrate in manual tasks, natives reallocate to communication-intensive tasks, so the two groups don't compete head-to-head. The mechanism is real, estimated from data, and the repo's own E-Verify TWFE (ladder 17) and Foged-Peri Denmark (ladder 19) confirm the small-average prediction on the inflows we can observe.

**(b) Verdict: the MECHANISM and the AVERAGE are GRANTED; the welfare conclusion "doesn't hurt native workers" is a textbook DENOMINATOR/AGGREGATION MASK — and the mask is exposed by Peri's OWN model.** This is the central kill, and its force comes entirely from primary text: **Ottaviano-Peri's model does not say no one loses. It says the losses are *reallocated* — and it names exactly who absorbs them.**

**What is GRANTED (the repo agrees):**
- Imperfect substitution is real and the average native effect is small/positive. OP w12497 (verbatim): the average US-born gain "stems from the imperfect substitutability between U.S.- and foreign-born workers." The repo's effects-matrix lists task complementarity as a real positive channel anti-immigration ledgers omit. [SOURCE: research/immigration-economist-effects-matrix.md; ladder 17, 19]

**Why "doesn't hurt native workers" is an OVERCLAIM — the EXACT move: Move 5 (aggregation masking).** A near-zero *average* is the *sum across a population* that conceals a *transfer between groups*. Three pieces of primary evidence — two of them from Peri's own paper — show the average hides concentrated losers:

1. **The losers are PRIOR IMMIGRANTS — and OP's own model says so explicitly.** Verbatim from w12497: the average US-born gain comes *"at the expense of a decrease in wages of foreign-born workers (namely, previous immigrants)"*; and *"the group [most adversely] affected by immigration is… the group of previous immigrants."* The mechanism is structural: new immigrants substitute *closely* for *old* immigrants (same tasks, same niches), so the incidence falls on the foreign-born already here. OP quantify it (preferred specification 2, Table 7, verbatim): *"the group whose wages are hurt most by immigration are foreign-born workers, i.e., previous immigrants. On average they lost **19%** of their real wages while some groups (i.e., college graduates) lost up to **24%** of their wage."* The canon's "natives unharmed" headline is *literally true in OP's model precisely because the model shoves the loss onto prior immigrants* — a group of ~real, low-income workers the "average native" frame dissolves. [SOURCE: nber.org/papers/w12497.pdf, verbatim; parent re-verified the −19%/−24% figures against the PDF on integration after an Exa proxy falsely flagged them]
2. **Competing low-skill NATIVES also lose, even in OP's optimistic model.** Verbatim: the long-run effect on **native high-school dropouts is −1.1%** (and "can be as large as −2%"); under imposed *perfect* substitutability it is −4.2%. So even the *most favorable* GE specification in the pro-immigration canon shows the bottom native skill group losing — small, but non-zero and the *wrong sign* for "no one is hurt." The zero average is a weighted mix of a complemented majority gaining and a competing minority (dropouts + prior immigrants) losing. [SOURCE: nber.org/papers/w12497.pdf, verbatim]
3. **The distributional question is logged as UNRESOLVED in the repo, not settled.** The repo never closes Borjas-vs-Peri on low-skill native displacement — `immigration-dismantle-noah-smith-2026-06-25.md` (Claim 1) and the claims-audit record it as *unresolved*. A canon that presents the small average as having *answered* the distributional question is claiming a closure the evidence does not support.

**Live methodological counter that the canon must also clear — Move 5's foundation is contested.** The imperfect-substitution elasticity that *generates* the +0.6% is itself attacked at primary. Borjas, Grogger & Hanson 2008, *"Imperfect Substitution between Immigrants and Natives: A Reappraisal"* (NBER w13887, quote-verified): *"their finding of imperfect substitution is fragile and depends on the way the sample of working persons is constructed… the finding of immigrant-native complementarity evaporates simply by removing high school students from the data… we cannot reject the hypothesis that comparably skilled immigrant and native workers are perfect substitutes once the empirical exercise uses standard methods."* [SOURCE: nber.org/papers/w13887.pdf, verbatim (the abstract) — corrected on quote-verification from the trio's 2012 *JEEA Comment*, which makes the equivalent argument but not in these words] This does **not** overturn Peri — Dustmann-Preston and others side with OP, and the repo's *direct* tests (E-Verify, Foged-Peri) independently support the small-average reading — but it means the *specific elasticity* doing the welfare work is **not** a settled constant, and a canon that treats σ as nailed-down is overclaiming the precision of its own keystone.

**Verdict on Claim 2: the mechanism (imperfect substitution / task complementarity) and the small-positive AVERAGE are GRANTED and repo-confirmed. The OVERCLAIM is "doesn't hurt native workers" — Move 5 aggregation masking, exposed by Peri's OWN model, which reallocates the loss onto prior immigrants (−19% on average, up to −24% for some foreign-born groups e.g. college graduates) and competing native dropouts (−1.1%), while the elasticity generating the average is itself contested (Borjas-Grogger-Hanson).** The fair sharp question: *"Your model gives +0.6% on average — but your own paper says that average exists *because* the loss is pushed onto previous immigrants and native dropouts. Why is 'natives unharmed' the headline when (a) your model harms the bottom native skill group −1.1%, (b) it harms prior immigrants −19% on average, and (c) the repo logs the distributional fight as unresolved? A zero average is not 'no one loses' — it's 'the losers were averaged away.'"*

### Claim 3 — "The Card-Peri small-effect result holds for immigration generally, including current levels." (marginal-policy results stretched to the surge)

> [Implicit in canonical usage:] the body of imperfect-substitution / area-study evidence shows immigration's wage effect is small *as a general property of immigration*, so concerns about the 2021-2024 inflow are misplaced.

**(a) Steelman.** The convergent evidence — Card 1990 Mariel, Ottaviano-Peri 1990-2004, Foged-Peri Denmark, the repo's E-Verify TWFE 2003-2023 — spans multiple countries, decades, and designs, and all point the same way: small average native-wage effects. A regularity that robust across settings is a reasonable default prior for *any* inflow, including the present one.

**(b) Verdict: the cross-design convergence is GRANTED for the inflows it covers; the extension to the 2021-2024 SURGE regime is an explicit OVERCLAIM the repo's own meta-update forbids.** This is the cleanest kill because the repo *already adjudicated it against itself.*

**The EXACT move: Move 3 (marginal-to-mass extrapolation).** Ladder entry 29 is explicit and was written specifically to stop this: *"Static-cycle Card-side wage finding is BOUNDED to marginal-policy variation; surge is OUTSIDE that variation… The 2021-2024 surge is a regime shift outside that variation. Linear extrapolation is not warranted."* The evidence base is built on:
- **Marginal policy variation** (E-Verify mandates — ladder 17 itself flags the result is for "the observed static-TWFE mandate margin," with surge regimes "unresolved"),
- **A historical 7% one-city shock** (Mariel) that *dissipated* via the very native-out-migration channel (Claim 1) that a *nationwide* surge cannot offer — there is no "comparison city" for the whole country, and the 40-60% local attenuation (Borjas w11610) is exactly the escape valve that does *not* exist at national scale,
- **Steady-state mechanisms** (capital adjusts, demand keeps pace, tasks reallocate) that are *least* guaranteed to hold for an **8.7M-above-baseline** inflow concentrated into specific low-wage labor markets and specific cities over a few years.

The surge is **out of sample** for every study in the canon. Surge-period wage estimates have *not been run* (they require ACS PUMS 2023 — ladder 29 lists this as open work). A canon that says "immigration's wage effect is small, so the surge is fine" is asserting external validity the repo explicitly denied and never re-established. [SOURCE: research/immigration-causal-surge-2021-2024.md; ladder 29]

**Verdict on Claim 3: the small-effect convergence is GRANTED for observed marginal inflows; extending it to the 2021-2024 surge is Move 3, and ladder 29 already rules the extrapolation out.** The fair sharp question: *"Every study in your canon is either a single-city shock that escaped via native out-migration or marginal policy variation. The 2021-24 surge is 8.7M above baseline, nationwide (no comparison city, no 40-60% local escape valve), and concentrated. Your own steady-state mechanism is least reliable exactly there. Why should a marginal/local result survive a mass/national shock no one in your canon estimated?"*

## What survives (large — the honest accounting)

This target is **mostly granted**, and the repo would contradict itself otherwise. What survives the dismantling, intact and repo-confirmed:

1. **The Mariel wage null is correct.** Card (1990) is right; Borjas's −10 to −30% reappraisal is the ~17-obs/yr compositional artifact (ladder 44, Clemens-Hunt). [SURVIVES]
2. **Task complementarity / imperfect substitution is a real mechanism.** Immigrants and natives are not perfect substitutes; natives reallocate toward communication-intensive tasks (Peri-Sparber). The effects-matrix lists it as a genuine positive channel anti-immigration ledgers omit. [SURVIVES — modulo the contested *precision* of σ, Borjas-Grogger-Hanson]
3. **The AVERAGE native-wage effect of observed inflows is small or slightly positive.** +0.6-0.7% in OP; null in the repo's own E-Verify TWFE (ladder 17); positive in Foged-Peri Denmark (ladder 19). Convergent across designs. [SURVIVES]
4. **Therefore "immigration causes large native wage DOOM" is FALSE,** and the restrictionist large-harm wage claim is **not supported** by the observed evidence. Card-Peri win that fight. [SURVIVES]

## What is killed (the three scope-stretches only)

The canon is misused — never refuted — when it is stretched past those four survivors:

- **KILL A (Move 3, on method).** The area-study *design* is treated as a clean *general* proof. It isn't: native out-migration + capital mobility wash out **40-60%** of the local-vs-national wage gap (Borjas w11610, verbatim), so a single-city null under-identifies the national effect. *Borjas's method point is valid even though his Mariel magnitude is the artifact* — these are separable, and the honest position holds both.
- **KILL B (Move 5, the central one).** The small AVERAGE is laundered into "no one is hurt." Peri's OWN model reallocates the loss: **prior immigrants −19% on average** (up to −24% for some foreign-born groups, e.g. college graduates), **native dropouts −1.1%** — and the repo logs the low-skill distributional fight as **unresolved**, not settled. A zero average is "the losers were averaged away," not "no losers." The elasticity that produces the average is itself contested (Borjas-Grogger-Hanson: complementarity "evaporates" without HS students).
- **KILL C (Move 3, on regime).** Marginal/local results are extended to the 2021-2024 **surge** (8.7M above baseline, nationwide, concentrated) — exactly where the steady-state mechanism is least reliable and where there is *no comparison city and no 40-60% local escape valve*. **Ladder 29 already forbids this extrapolation; surge wage estimates remain unrun.**

## One-line honest verdict

**Card-Peri are right that wage-doom is overstated — the average native-wage effect of observed inflows is small, and the mechanism (task complementarity) is real. The canon is misused only when a small AVERAGE is presented as "no one is hurt" (it harms prior immigrants and competing native dropouts, by its own model), when an area-study NULL is read as a clean general proof (native out-migration washes out 40-60% of it), and when MARGINAL/local results are stretched to the SURGE regime (out of sample, ladder-29-forbidden).** Three surgical scope-kills on an otherwise-granted, repo-confirmed canon.
