# Immigration unified scenarios memo

Date: 2026-04-10

## Question

`Under explicit horizons and counterfactuals, which low-skill immigration flows, into which destinations, improve or degrade institutional resilience, local-system capacity, and net citizen welfare, and on which ledger?`

## Executive synthesis

The cleanest current answer is not a scalar.

What the evidence supports now:
1. `Federal versus state/local split` is real. Recent immigration can improve aggregate output and some federal fiscal channels while still imposing local schooling, shelter, and housing-capacity costs. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-07/60165-Immigration.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-06/61256-immigration-state-local.pdf]
2. `Local burden heterogeneity` is real. Our warehouse consistently shows that some origin groups are more school-heavy, some are more housing-heavy, and some are mixed. [SOURCE: /Users/alien/Projects/research/research/immigration-household-weighted-correction.md] [SOURCE: /Users/alien/Projects/research/research/immigration-local-burden-puma-layer.md]
3. `Economist-agree papers` are mostly not false, but many are narrow papers being used as if they were full welfare ledgers. [SOURCE: /Users/alien/Projects/research/research/immigration-economist-effects-matrix.md]
4. `Political backlash` is not a side issue. Immigration often triggers backlash under some conditions, and that can change welfare through institutions, redistribution, and legitimacy. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.20221643]
5. `[FRAMING-SENSITIVE]` If AGI soon is live, lifetime-NPV debates matter less than short-run transition management, local capacity, and option value under uncertainty. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/research/imf-and-g20/2025/g20-background-note-on-aging-and-migration.pdf] [SOURCE: https://docs.iza.org/dp18218.pdf]

What the evidence does not support now:
1. A clean `national net negative` verdict from our current warehouse.
2. A clean `average citizen better off` verdict from Clark-style labor-market papers.
3. Precise magnitude claims for local school burden or county exposure using the current bridge.

## Scenario 1: Static labor-market and consumer-price view

Question:
Does low-skill immigration lower prices and help employers enough to look positive in a standard market frame?

What supports it:
1. Immigration increases labor supply and can lower prices in labor-intensive sectors. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-07/60165-Immigration.pdf]
2. Recent work on local inflation finds that more immigration lowers overall local inflation slightly while raising housing and utilities inflation, with larger effects for working-age and low-education immigrants. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2025/01/10/Immigration-and-Local-Inflation-560285]
3. Some indirect fiscal channels may be positive because immigrant labor changes native labor supply and wage structure. [SOURCE: https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/282044/1/352.pdf]

What cuts against it:
1. The same local-inflation paper says the housing channel runs the opposite way. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2025/01/10/Immigration-and-Local-Inflation-560285]
2. Labor-market papers do not price crowded schools, shelter, transit, or local political backlash.

Current read:
1. Positive for employers and some consumers.
2. Not sufficient as a country-level welfare account.

## Scenario 2: Direct and broader local public-finance view

Question:
Does recent low-skill immigration impose net state/local costs once education, shelter, and nonbudgetary crowding are counted?

What supports it:
1. CBO 2025 estimates that the 2021+ immigration surge imposed a direct `-$9.2B` state/local net cost in 2023 and a broader-potential `-$9.8B` cost, with education, shelter, and border security the largest categories. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-06/61256-immigration-state-local.pdf]
2. CBO explicitly includes costs borne without matching spending increases, such as more crowded classrooms and public transportation. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-06/61256-immigration-state-local.pdf]
3. The NAS long-run framework already finds low-education immigrant cohorts negative in absolute fiscal terms on many state/local-relevant paths. [SOURCE: https://d279m997dpfwgl.cloudfront.net/wp/2016/09/0922_immigrant-economics-full-report.pdf]

What cuts against it:
1. CBO’s 2025 state/local report is about the recent surge, not all low-skill immigration.
2. State/local fiscal sign can vary with destination housing markets, tax structure, and whether immigrant-driven property values lift local revenues. [SOURCE: https://www.cato.org/briefing-paper/immigrants-housing-wealth-local-government-finances]

Current read:
1. The local-capacity-negative side is real.
2. Magnitude is sensitive to location and accounting choices.
3. Our local warehouse is aligned with the direction of this story, but not yet strong enough to estimate local magnitudes cleanly. [SOURCE: /Users/alien/Projects/research/research/immigration-stage2-county-bridge-batch.md]

## Scenario 3: Housing and local-capacity incidence view

Question:
Is housing a net welfare loss, a transfer, or both depending on who you are?

What supports a negative renter/local-capacity read:
1. Immigration can raise local rents and utilities inflation even if overall inflation falls. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2025/01/10/Immigration-and-Local-Inflation-560285]
2. Our own PUMA work shows that some origin groups sort into materially higher-rent local submarkets than state averages imply. [SOURCE: /Users/alien/Projects/research/research/immigration-local-burden-puma-layer.md]
3. County CHAS stress and PUMA rent layers suggest local capacity is binding in some destinations. [SOURCE: /Users/alien/Projects/research/research/immigration-stage2-county-bridge-batch.md]

What supports a positive owner/local-government read:
1. Cato/Vigdor/Bier/Howard argue immigration raises or preserves housing wealth and property-tax revenue. [SOURCE: https://www.cato.org/briefing-paper/immigrants-housing-wealth-local-government-finances]
2. Property-value effects can partially offset a narrow local-budget read if homeowners and local tax receipts are in the objective function. [SOURCE: https://www.cato.org/briefing-paper/immigrants-housing-wealth-local-government-finances]

Current read:
1. Housing is not a pure loss.
2. It is an incidence problem: renters and capacity-constrained service users can lose while owners and parts of the tax base gain.
3. Our current warehouse captures `stress exposure`, not the full renter-owner incidence split. [INFERENCE]

## Scenario 4: Descendants and intergenerational mobility view

Question:
Do children and later descendants offset earlier local costs?

What supports a positive long-run descendant channel:
1. The NAS framework includes descendants and is the baseline long-horizon fiscal benchmark. [SOURCE: https://d279m997dpfwgl.cloudfront.net/wp/2016/09/0922_immigrant-economics-full-report.pdf]
2. New NBER evidence shows immigration can raise education and earnings for children from poorer U.S.-born households while reducing outcomes for children from more affluent households, increasing intergenerational mobility overall. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w33961/w33961.pdf]

What cuts against overclaiming:
1. Descendant benefits arrive on a longer horizon than school and shelter costs.
2. Descendant effects are heterogeneous; they are not a blanket immediate offset to local public-finance strain. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w33961/w33961.pdf]

Current read:
1. Descendants are an important positive channel.
2. They should not be used to deny short-run local burden.

## Scenario 5: Political economy and backlash view

Question:
Even if some economic channels are positive, does immigration degrade political support for institutions, redistribution, or local legitimacy?

What supports it:
1. Alesina and Tabellini review evidence that immigration often triggers backlash, raises support for anti-immigrant parties, and lowers preferences for redistribution and diversity among natives, depending on conditions and perceptions. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.20221643]
2. Recent working-paper evidence on unauthorized Mexican migration finds local political and socioeconomic effects, including higher GOP vote share and changes in local poverty and turnout patterns. [SOURCE: https://scholar.karaross.com/working-papers/localreactions-current.pdf]
3. Municipal finance work on unauthorized immigration suggests local bond markets can price immigration differently depending on labor-market tightness and sanctuary context. [SOURCE: https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/6a_CCI-Immigration-2025-07-22.pdf]

What cuts against simplistic backlash claims:
1. Backlash is mediated by media, contact, misperceptions, and local institutional context. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.20221643]
2. Political effects are not always negative and are not reducible to one economic mechanism.

Current read:
1. Political economy belongs in the main analysis, not the appendix.
2. A flow that is mildly positive on GDP can still be negative on institutional resilience if it produces strong local backlash. [INFERENCE]

## Scenario 6: AGI-soon / automation-interaction view

Question:
If automation accelerates, is low-skill immigration a bridge, a complement, or a delay mechanism?

What supports a bridge/complement view:
1. Aging economies need labor supply in care, logistics, and other sectors that may not automate fast enough. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/research/imf-and-g20/2025/g20-background-note-on-aging-and-migration.pdf]
2. Migration can help offset demographic drag in advanced economies. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/research/imf-and-g20/2025/g20-background-note-on-aging-and-migration.pdf]
3. OECD treats migrants as one of several labor-supply margins in aging economies rather than a standalone fix, alongside women, youth, and older workers. [SOURCE: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-employment-outlook-2025_194a947b-en/full-report/editorial-from-job-shortage-to-labour-shortage-the-new-challenge-of-population-ageing_77cf5ed3.html]

What supports a risk view:
1. Automation and immigration shocks can jointly amplify adverse earnings effects for exposed non-college workers. [SOURCE: https://docs.iza.org/dp18218.pdf]
2. If labor inflows delay automation adoption in some sectors, the long-run productivity and transition story changes. [INFERENCE]
3. ILO's 2025 generative-AI update suggests most exposed jobs are more likely to be transformed than eliminated, which weakens the case for importing generic routine labor while leaving embodied and place-bound work less affected. [SOURCE: https://www.ilo.org/publications/generative-ai-and-jobs-2025-update] [SOURCE: https://www.ilo.org/sites/default/files/2025-05/Research%20brief_GenAI%202025%20Update.pdf]

Current read:
1. Under AGI-soon assumptions, the right target is short-run transition robustness, not 75-year average immigrant NPV.
2. The most relevant questions become sector-specific: care, construction, logistics, food systems, and local service delivery.

## Scenario 7: Aging, geopolitics, and state-capacity view

Question:
How does migration interact with aging, global demographic imbalance, and strategic competition for workers?

What supports it:
1. The IMF G20 note argues migration has helped offset demographic pressure in advanced economies, while aging, fertility decline, and weaker working-age growth threaten growth and living standards. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/research/imf-and-g20/2025/g20-background-note-on-aging-and-migration.pdf]
2. The same note says immigration policy should be paired with immigrant integration and congestion relief. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/research/imf-and-g20/2025/g20-background-note-on-aging-and-migration.pdf]
3. Brookings 2026 argues weaker immigration flows could materially lower labor-force growth, GDP growth, and consumer spending. [SOURCE: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/macroeconomic-implications-of-immigration-flows-in-2025-and-2026-january-2026-update/]
4. UN demographic work and OECD's 2025 employment outlook both reinforce that shrinking working-age populations and rising dependency ratios are structural rather than temporary, which makes migration policy part of a broader state-capacity adjustment problem. [SOURCE: https://population.un.org/wpp/assets/Files/WPP2024_Summary-of-Results.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.un.org/development/desa/pd/sites/www.un.org.development.desa.pd/files/undesa_pd_2024_pb_population-ageing.pdf] [SOURCE: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-employment-outlook-2025_194a947b-en/full-report/editorial-from-job-shortage-to-labour-shortage-the-new-challenge-of-population-ageing_77cf5ed3.html]

What cuts against a simple pro-immigration geopolitical story:
1. State capacity can fail locally before national demographic relief arrives.
2. If politics turns against migration, the country may lose both migration and legitimacy.

Current read:
1. Geopolitically, migration can be a national resilience asset.
2. Domestically, the same flows can still be locally destabilizing if integration and capacity are weak.
3. Strategic competition for migrants means destination-country legitimacy and absorptive capacity become part of migration policy itself, not just side constraints. [SOURCE: https://www.oecd.org/en/topics/sub-issues/migration-policies-returns-and-attracting-talent.html]

## Unified verdict by ledger

1. `National output / macro`: probably positive in most mainstream recent estimates. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-07/60165-Immigration.pdf]
2. `Federal budget`: plausibly positive or less negative than local ledgers for many flows, but our own warehouse still uses proxies rather than a federal microsim. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-07/60165-Immigration.pdf] [SOURCE: /Users/alien/Projects/research/research/immigration-low-skill-origin-incidence-memo.md]
3. `State/local budgets and capacity`: negative or capacity-straining for important recent and low-skill-heavy cases. [SOURCE: https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-06/61256-immigration-state-local.pdf]
4. `Renters and high-cost service users`: often negative in constrained markets. [SOURCE: https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2025/01/10/Immigration-and-Local-Inflation-560285]
5. `Employers and some consumers`: positive.
6. `Descendants and long-run mobility`: mixed but often materially positive on long horizons. [SOURCE: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w33961/w33961.pdf]
7. `Political legitimacy`: can turn negative fast under backlash conditions. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.20221643]

## What we can say now

1. Low-skill immigration is not one object.
2. The strongest current result is an incidence split, not a scalar verdict.
3. The economist-survey consensus style claim `average citizen better off` is too coarse.
4. The project is currently strongest on `which cells look locally burden-heavy` and weakest on `a fully specified federal ledger` and `precise local school-cost magnitude`.
