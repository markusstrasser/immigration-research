## Gap 1 — OECD DIOC (harmonized immigrant stocks + outcomes across destination countries)

### OECD-DIOC — Database on Immigrants in OECD (and non-OECD) Countries
**Source:** OECD International Migration Division (with World Bank for the DIOC-E extension) · **Access:** PUBLIC — direct `.xlsx` download from the OECD file server (no registration). Bulk files are scriptable via stable `webfs.oecd.org` paths. NOTE: the legacy interactive API (`stats.oecd.org`, `DataSetCode=DIOC_*`) **switched off June 19/30 2026** → the supported live-query path is now the **OECD Data Explorer** (`data-explorer.oecd.org`); for reproducible bulk use, pull the per-round `.xlsx` files directly.
**Official:** dataset hub https://www.oecd.org/en/data/datasets/database-on-immigrants-in-oecd-and-non-oecd-countries.html · all-databases overview https://www.oecd.org/en/data/datasets/oecd-databases-on-migration.html · direct bulk file server (DIOC) https://webfs.oecd.org/els-com/Migration/Databases/Database%20on%20Immigrants%20in%20OECD%20and%20non-OECD%20Countries%20DIOC/DIOC%20database%20-%20click%20here/ · 2020/21 round files (e.g. `t1_TOP100.xlsx` … `t6_TOP100.xlsx`, posted Oct 2024) under the `Reference years 2020-2021/` subfolder · Data Explorer migration-dataset correspondence sheet https://gitlab.com/sis-cc/topologies/oecd-migration/-/raw/main/OECDDatasetsCorrespondence.xlsx
**Format / size:** five census rounds — **2000/01, 2005/06, 2010/11, 2015/16, 2020/21** — as Excel tables (a few hundred KB to ~12 MB each). DIOC core = **34 destination countries × 200+ origin countries**; DIOC-E (extended, with World Bank) = **100 destination countries × 200+ origin** for the 2000/01 and 2010/11 rounds.
**Key variables:** by destination × origin country × sex × (sometimes) age and education — immigrant **stocks**; demographic characteristics (age, gender), **duration of stay**, **labour-market outcomes** (labour-force status, occupation, sector of activity), **field of study**, **educational attainment**, and **place of birth**. The 2000/01 and 2010/11 rounds support **emigration rates by skill level** (the brain-drain measure). Sibling OECD products on the same hub: the **International Migration Database (MIG)** — annual flows + stocks + acquisitions of nationality — and the **Indicators of Immigrant Integration** (employment/education/social-inclusion indicators).
**Why it fits:** the canonical **harmonized cross-country immigrant-stock-and-outcomes** database — it places the US foreign-born composition (origin mix, education distribution, labour-market position) on the *same* measurement basis as 33 other OECD destinations, so the US "low-skill-heavy" claim can be benchmarked against peer receivers rather than asserted in isolation. The education × origin breakdown is exactly the composition axis the project's fiscal ledger is driven by; the US destination rows cross-check our ACS B05006 origin-stock layer against an externally harmonized count. Distinct from the already-listed OECD IMO 2021 fiscal *chapter* (which is a net-fiscal *estimate*); DIOC is the underlying *stock-and-characteristics microdata-derived tabulation*.
**Limit / honesty:** TABULATED aggregates (cells by destination×origin×sex×education), **not microdata** — no person-level records, no within-US geography (country-level only). Built from each country's own census/register round, so cross-country comparability is "harmonized" but not identical (definitions of "immigrant" = foreign-born here, vs foreigner elsewhere; education coding bridged via ISCED). Rounds are ~5 years apart and lag (2020/21 is the newest). No legal-status / unauthorized split. The US row reflects the decennial/ACS source, so it is **not independent** of data we already hold — its value is the *comparative* frame, not a new US number.
**Graph fit:** international benchmark node; `destination_country` × `origin_country` × education × sex; the US destination rows join the origin layer by country and cross-check ACS B05006; peer-country rows are comparison-only (no US row-level join).

## Gap 2 — Remittances (the fiscal-leakage channel: money earned in the US sent abroad)

### WB-KNOMAD-REMIT — World Bank / KNOMAD Migration & Remittances Data
**Source:** World Bank Global Knowledge Partnership on Migration and Development (KNOMAD); lead Dilip Ratha · **Access:** PUBLIC — direct `.xlsx` downloads (no registration). Scriptable from the KNOMAD data portal; also mirrored in the World Bank **Data360** catalog (`WB_KNOMAD`) for API-style access.
**Official:** data hub https://knomad-ois.assyst.in/data · remittances page (inflows / outward / bilateral matrix download buttons) https://knomad-ois.assyst.in/data/remittances · World Bank remittances brief (annual Migration & Development Brief, headline numbers) https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/migration/brief/remittances-knomad · Data360 catalog entry https://data360.worldbank.org/en/dataset/WB_KNOMAD · bilateral-matrix methodology https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/peoplemove/bilateral-remittance-matrix-new
**Format / size:** three core annual Excel matrices — **Inward (received) remittances**, **Outward (sent) remittances**, and the **Bilateral Remittance Matrix** (origin×destination corridor estimates) — plus the companion **Bilateral Migration Matrix**. Country × year, global coverage (200+ economies), updated ~annually (recent files cover through 2024 forecasts; matrices re-estimated periodically, latest bilateral round = 2021 flows). Headline magnitudes: global remittances **$836B (2022) / $860B (2023e) / $887B (2024f)**.
**Key variables:** annual remittance **inflows** and **outflows** by country (US$ and % of GDP); the **bilateral matrix** allocates each corridor (e.g. **United States → Mexico ≈ $52B**, US → India ≈ $6–11B). The US is **by far the largest single *source* of outward remittances (~$200B/yr, 2021)**.
**Why it fits:** the **fiscal-leakage / consumption-leakage channel** the project's domestic ledger omits — earnings paid to immigrant workers in the US that are remitted abroad rather than spent/taxed domestically, which bears on the local multiplier and the net-domestic-demand side of the fiscal-impact argument. The US→origin-country corridors (Mexico, Central America, India, Philippines) map directly onto the origin composition of the US foreign-born stock we already model, letting the analysis size the outflow against each origin group. A genuinely new economic channel, not just another stock count.
**Limit / honesty:** remittance figures are **modeled estimates**, not measured transfers — inflows come from balance-of-payments reporting (incomplete, informal channels under-captured) and the **bilateral matrix is an allocation model** (Ratha-Shaw 2007: distributes a country's total inflow across sources by migrant stock × per-capita-income ratios), so corridor numbers are derived, not observed. Remittances are a private transfer, **not a government fiscal flow** — treating them as a "fiscal cost" is a framing choice (`[FRAMING-SENSITIVE]`: leakage to local demand ≠ a budget line item; steel-man the counter-view that remittances are earned wages freely disposed of and that senders also pay US taxes on that income). Country-level only; no legal-status split. Forecast years are projections.
**Graph fit:** `remittance_outflow` channel (new); US-source corridor × origin-country × year; origin-country join to the origin layer (`POBP`); national/corridor aggregate, no within-US geography.

## Gap 3 — USCIS administrative flows (naturalization, DACA, employment authorization, benefits)

### USCIS-DATA — USCIS Immigration & Citizenship Data (form-level adjudication + DACA + naturalization)
**Source:** US Citizenship and Immigration Services (DHS component) · **Access:** PUBLIC — per-FY/quarter `.xlsx` releases on uscis.gov (no registration); fully scriptable from stable `uscis.gov/sites/default/files/document/data/…` paths. Employer Data Hubs are downloadable CSV; several interactive dashboards (Eligible-to-Naturalize, H-1B/H-2A/H-2B hubs).
**Official:** data hub https://www.uscis.gov/tools/reports-and-studies/immigration-and-citizenship-data · naturalization statistics https://www.uscis.gov/citizenship-resource-center/naturalization-statistics · Annual Statistical Report FY2024 (PDF) https://www.uscis.gov/sites/default/files/document/reports/fy24_annual_statistical_report_v1.0.pdf · quarterly all-forms file (example) https://www.uscis.gov/sites/default/files/document/data/quarterly_all_forms_fy2025_q3.xlsx · H-1B Employer Data Hub https://www.uscis.gov/tools/reports-and-studies/h-1b-employer-data-hub · Eligible-to-Naturalize Dashboard https://www.uscis.gov/tools/reports-and-studies/immigration-and-citizenship-data/eligible-to-naturalize-dashboard
**Format / size:** quarterly + annual Excel workbooks per form type, FY2020-present for most series (some back further). Examples of discrete downloadable series: **Active DACA Recipients** (quarterly XLSX), **DACA Quarterly Report**, **Form I-765 Employment Authorization** (by eligibility category, incl. the `(c)(8)` pending-asylum category), **Form I-485** adjustment of status, **Form I-589** asylum, **Form N-400** naturalization, **I-821 / I-821D** (TPS / DACA), **I-918 / I-914** (U / T humanitarian). Naturalization stats give country of birth, **state/city/CBSA of residence**, age, sex.
**Key variables:** counts received/completed/pending by form, eligibility category, case status, processing time, RFE rates, and (for naturalization) **country of birth × state of residence × age/sex**; **active DACA population** by state/country; **H-1B/H-2A/H-2B** petitions by employer, wage level, worksite. The **Eligible-to-Naturalize Dashboard** estimates the LPR-eligible-to-naturalize population.
**Why it fits:** the **administrative-flow ledger of the legal/quasi-legal channels** the fiscal model needs to separate from the unauthorized stock — naturalization volume (who exits "immigrant" status into citizenship), the **active DACA population** (a specifically-litigated fiscal cohort: work-authorized, tax-paying, benefit-restricted — directly relevant to DACA cost/benefit claims), and EAD issuance (who is work-authorized). Complements the held DHS-OHSS state file and the proposed OHSS Yearbook with the *adjudication/processing* side and the **DACA-specific** counts that OHSS does not isolate. The naturalization country×state breakdown joins both our origin and context layers.
**Limit / honesty:** **workload/flow data** (applications and adjudications in a period), **not a population panel** and **not a fiscal estimate** — counts of forms, not dollars or outcomes. "Received/completed" reflects agency processing capacity (backlogs distort year-to-year), not underlying demand or population change. No earnings, no benefit *receipt* (only benefit *eligibility category*). DACA active-recipient counts are a real population stock but cover only the DACA cohort. Distinct from DHS-OHSS Yearbook (OHSS = LPR/refugee/enforcement flows; USCIS = benefit-request adjudication + DACA) — minor overlap on naturalization, which both publish.
**Graph fit:** `admin_flow` layer (new); form × eligibility-category × FY/quarter; naturalization rows join origin (`POBP`/country) × context (`state_fips`); active-DACA join by state/country; H-1B/H-2 hubs join `state_fips` × SOC (overlaps the proposed OFLC-DISCLOSURE labor-demand layer).

## Gap 4 — Local fiscal capacity (the state-local cost *denominator*)

### CENSUS-COG-ALFIN — Census of Governments + Annual Survey of State & Local Government Finances
**Source:** US Census Bureau, Economic Programs (Public Sector / Government Finance) · **Access:** PUBLIC — live **Census Data API** (`/data/timeseries/govsstatefin`, API key required, free) + downloadable historical datasets/tables (XLS/CSV) per year. Fully scriptable exactly like the project's existing ACS/QWI API pulls.
**Official:** Census of Governments API hub https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/cog/data/api.html · gov-finances program (ALFIN) https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/gov-finances.html · datasets & tables https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/gov-finances/data/datasets.html · technical documentation + Public-Sector/CoG API variable docs https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/gov-finances/technical-documentation.html · live API examples https://api.census.gov/data/timeseries/govsstatefin/examples.html
**Format / size:** the **Annual Survey of State & Local Government Finances** (annual, 1942-present via the API "Annual Public Sector Statistics" series) plus the quinquennial **Census of Governments** (full universe in years ending 2 and 7 — **2022** is the latest census; 2017 prior). API geography: US, state, and (in census years) local/county-level government units; GOVTYPE codes separate state vs local vs combined. 2024 "First Look" released April 2026; 2023 historical datasets released July 2025.
**Key variables:** by state (and, in CoG years, local government unit) × year — **revenue** (taxes by type, intergovernmental, charges), **expenditure by function** (education, public welfare, health & hospitals, police, corrections, housing, highways), **debt**, and **cash/securities/assets**. Item codes (`ITEM_CODE`), aggregate descriptors (`AGG_DESC`), and category codes structure the function-level detail.
**Why it fits:** the **fiscal-capacity denominator** the entire state-local cost argument implicitly divides by but the stack currently lacks — total state and local **own-source revenue and expenditure by function**, so any immigration-attributed cost (schools, Medicaid/public welfare, corrections, housing) can be expressed as a *share of the relevant budget* rather than a free-floating dollar figure. It is the authoritative source for "how big is the public-welfare / education / corrections budget in state X" that anchors the CBO state-local report and our stage-5 net-negative layer. Public-welfare and corrections expenditure lines pair directly with the proposed CMS Emergency-Medicaid and SCAAP/incarceration cost cards; education expenditure pairs with our held NCES/school-finance layer.
**Limit / honesty:** **government-unit finance data with NO person-level or nativity dimension whatsoever** — it is a pure **denominator/context** layer; immigration attribution is entirely external (you bring the numerator). Functional categories are broad (e.g. "public welfare" bundles many programs); cross-state comparability is good but service-responsibility splits between state and local vary (some states centralize Medicaid, others push to counties), so per-capita lines need care. Annual survey is a sample between census years (small-government estimates noisier); the full universe only every 5 years. Lags ~2 years.
**Graph fit:** `fiscal_capacity` denominator layer (new); `state_fips` (× local unit in CoG years) × function × year; joins the state-local cost ledger and every cost card as the budget-share denominator; **ecological/context only — no nativity join.**

## Gap 5 — Refugee integration (the only national refugee self-sufficiency panel)

### ORR-ASR — Office of Refugee Resettlement, Annual Survey of Refugees (public-use)
**Source:** HHS Administration for Children & Families, Office of Refugee Resettlement (ORR); fielded by the Urban Institute + SSRS · **Access:** PUBLIC — **public-use microdata files available since 2016**; the 2022 wave is on **openICPSR (project 209224, DOI 10.3886/E209224V1)** as Stata/SAS/SPSS files with full data dictionaries (no ICPSR membership needed for openICPSR public deposits). Snapshot briefs (2021-2023) on acf.gov. Scriptable download once the openICPSR data-use click-through is accepted.
**Official:** program page https://acf.gov/orr/programs/refugees/annual-survey-refugees · 2022 ASR openICPSR deposit https://www.openicpsr.org/openicpsr/project/209224/version/V1/view · DOI https://doi.org/10.3886/E209224V1 · HealthData.gov mirror https://healthdata.gov/ACF/Annual-Survey-of-Refugees/bkas-g3nh · 2021 Snapshot Brief (PDF) https://acf.gov/sites/default/files/documents/orr/2021-ASR-Snapshot-Brief.pdf
**Format / size:** annual cross-section, ~**1,500 sampled households representing ~3,500 family members** (person-level + household-level files), of refugees who resettled in the **preceding five fiscal years** (rolling 5-year window). Conducted by CATI in ~20 languages (~75% of adult arrivals). Public-use files 2016-present; 2022 = ORR's 56th ASR. File sizes ~11-19 MB per format.
**Key variables:** **employment & labour-force status, economic self-sufficiency** (earnings, public-assistance receipt — TANF/RCA/SNAP), **English language proficiency** and change since arrival, **education / human capital** (degrees, US recredentialing), **health and medical insurance coverage**, **LPR (green-card) status and naturalization intent**, **secondary migration** (state moved-from vs current — ~75% stay in resettlement state), pre-arrival experiences, children & schools, social connection, country of origin. Year-of-arrival cohort structure supports trajectory analysis across the 5-year window.
**Why it fits:** the **only national, scientifically-sampled source on refugee integration and self-sufficiency** — it measures, with a status field US household surveys lack, exactly the benefit-side trajectory (welfare receipt declining and employment rising over the first five years) that the fiscal debate disputes for the humanitarian-entrant cohort. It is the empirical core for any refugee-specific cost/benefit claim and the US analog to the refugee arm of StatCan IMDB's category-contrast. The welfare-receipt and employment variables feed the program-use side of the ledger; the LPR/naturalization and secondary-migration variables connect to the USCIS and OHSS flow layers.
**Limit / honesty:** **refugees only** — ORR-eligible "refugee" status, explicitly **not other humanitarian entrants** (asylees, SIV, Cuban/Haitian, parolees are excluded from the ASR universe even though ORR serves them), and **not** the unauthorized population at all. Small annual sample (~1,500 households) → limited subgroup precision; **no public geography below region** (state of residence is restricted on the public file — secondary-migration is captured but fine geography is masked). Self-reported. Cross-section with a cohort structure, not a true individual panel (different households each year). Cohort window shifts annually. Distinct from the proposed HHS-ORR-UC card (that = the UC-program *appropriation/cost* PDFs; ASR = the *integration-outcomes microdata*).
**Graph fit:** `refugee_integration` layer (new); person/household × arrival-cohort × origin-country; origin join to origin layer; **region-level only (no state join on public file)**; outcomes feed the benefit-side ledger, pairs with the held OHSS refugee-arrivals flat file for denominators.

## Gap 6 — Comparison panel (longitudinal immigrant integration, a peer receiver)

### IAB-SOEP-MIG — IAB-SOEP Migration Sample (Germany)
**Source:** Institute for Employment Research (IAB, Nuremberg) + German Socio-Economic Panel (SOEP) at DIW Berlin; partly funded by the German Ministry of Labour & Social Affairs · **Access:** RESTRICTED-but-academic — Scientific Use File via the **SOEP Research Data Centre (FDZ)** under a signed data-distribution contract (free to researchers, but a contract + institutional affiliation; not an open download). The admin-linked version **SOEP-CMI-ADIAB** is available only on-site / remote via the **FDZ of the BA at the IAB** (German secure enclave — the EU analog of US FSRDC). NOT directly scriptable; access-gated.
**Official:** IAB FDZ product page https://fdz.iab.de/en/our-data-products/individual-and-household-data/iab-soep-mig/ · current data version (waves 1-12, 2013-2024) DOI 10.5684/soep.iab-soep-mig.2024 (released 2026-05-26) · DIW SOEP study description https://www.diw.de/en/diw_01.c.930514.en/iab-soep_migration_sample.html · 2023 edition (waves 1-11) https://www.diw.de/en/diw_01.c.960697.en/edition/iab-soep_migration_samples_2023.html · methodology intro (Brücker et al. 2014, SOEP Survey Paper 216) linked from the FDZ page
**Format / size:** household panel, **12 annual waves 2013-2024**, ~2,000-6,000 persons/wave; target population = persons who **migrated to Germany since 1995 and their 2nd/3rd-generation descendants**, drawn from IAB administrative employment registers (IEB). Sub-samples M1/M2/M7/M8a-d target successive arrival cohorts (incl. M8a-d evaluating the 2020 skilled-worker immigration law). Harmonized into SOEP-Core (since v30) using the "Living in Germany" instrument. Provided as Stata/SAS/SPSS SUF.
**Key variables:** longitudinal **labour-market integration** (employment, earnings, occupation, unemployment, benefit receipt), migration biography (arrival year, route, legal status on arrival), education and German-language acquisition, household composition, health, attitudes, and (via **SOEP-CMI-ADIAB**) linkage to the full IAB administrative employment-and-benefit history — i.e. survey-measured integration tied to register-grade earnings/benefit records.
**Why it fits:** the **gold-standard *longitudinal* immigrant-integration panel for a major peer receiver** — it does for Germany what StatCan IMDB does for Canada and what the US conspicuously lacks a public equivalent of: a real over-time earnings-and-benefit trajectory by arrival cohort and origin, with an admin-linked variant. As an international **method-and-contrast benchmark** it tests whether the US "low-skill entrants stay a fiscal drag" claim generalizes to a country with a large recent low/mid-skill and humanitarian inflow (post-2015), and the descendant (2nd/3rd-gen) coverage directly benchmarks the second-generation-mobility question the corpus under-weights. Complements StatCan IMDB (tax-register, points-selected) with a survey+register panel covering a more humanitarian/EU-mobility inflow mix.
**Limit / honesty:** **Germany, not the US** — benchmarks METHOD and cohort-contrast, **not US levels**; institutional differences (EU free movement, German welfare state, recognition-of-credentials regime, no large unauthorized analog in the sampling frame, which is drawn from *registered* employment) limit direct transfer. Access is **contract-gated** (SOEP FDZ) and the admin-linked SOEP-CMI-ADIAB is **in-Germany secure-enclave only** — a non-German researcher can realistically use the SUF remotely under contract but **cannot freely download** it, and the ADIAB linkage is effectively out of reach without an on-site arrangement. Sampling frame (IAB employment registers) under-covers the never-employed and the unauthorized. Survey, so subject to panel attrition (mitigated by refreshment samples).
**Graph fit:** international benchmark node; `arrival_cohort` × origin-country × wave; origin join by country for comparison; **no US row-level join** — a longitudinal comparator beside StatCan IMDB, UK Dustmann-Frattini, and OECD IMO 2021.

Pull MIG only if a *flow-level* cross-country comparison is later needed. URL: https://www.oecd.org/en/data/datasets/oecd-databases-on-migration.html (MIG dataset on the same hub).
