# Immigration — Sociology / Social-Integration Frontier (2026-06-25)

**Axis:** SOCIAL integration/cohesion — assimilation theory, social cohesion & trust, ethnic enclaves, residential segregation as social stratification. Explicitly NOT economics/wages/crime-rates (other agents own those).

**Instrument bias (per constitution):** politically charged topic + LLM-assisted corpus + a field that tilts left. Disconfirming and against-interest evidence weighted HIGHER. Steelman before critique. Every claim tagged. Framing-dependent verdicts flagged `[FRAMING-SENSITIVE]`.

**Bottom line up front:** The sociology frontier on immigration & cohesion is *more two-sided than the popular framing on either pole*. The single most decision-relevant finding for this repo is that the Putnam "diversity erodes trust" claim has been meta-analyzed twice (2014, 2020) and the verdict is a **qualified, scope-limited yes**: a real but *modest* negative effect that is (i) concentrated in trust-in-neighbors / locally-measured diversity, (ii) US-stronger than Europe, and (iii) does NOT extend to *interethnic* trust or generalized civic outlook. That nuance is itself the disconfirmation of both the alarmist and the dismissive position.

## (a) Papers — source-grade + what each tests/disconfirms

Grades: A = peer-reviewed meta-analysis / quasi-experiment in a top venue; B = peer-reviewed empirical with identification limits; C = review/theory, no new identification; D = advocacy/opinion. All saved to corpus unless noted.

### Social cohesion & trust (the two-sided core)

1. **Dinesen, Schaeffer & Sønderskov (2020), "Ethnic Diversity and Social Trust: A Narrative and Meta-Analytical Review," *Annual Review of Political Science* 23:441–465.** Grade **A**. `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-polisci-052918-020708]` OA PDF: pure.au.dk/ws/portalfiles/portal/230771020. **The current canonical meta-analysis** — 1,001 estimates from 87 studies, screened from ~4,000. **Findings (quoted):** "a statistically significant negative relationship between ethnic diversity and social trust across all studies," but the pooled partial correlation is **−0.0256 (s.e. 0.0044)** — described by the authors themselves as "rather modest." "The relationship is stronger for trust in neighbors and when ethnic diversity is measured more locally. Covariate conditioning generally changes the relationship only slightly." **What it settles / disconfirms:** kills the strong Putnam claim (diversity does NOT broadly erode generalized trust) AND kills the strong revisionist claim (the effect is real, significant, and survives controls — it is *not* a pure artifact of omitted SES/region). The honest verdict is "small, local, real." Public meta-data at osf.io/aecs2. `[FRAMING-SENSITIVE]`: whether −0.026 is "trivial" or "meaningful at scale" is a values call, not a data call.

2. **van der Meer & Tolsma (2014), "Ethnic Diversity and Its Effects on Social Cohesion," *Annual Review of Sociology* 40:459–478.** Grade **A**. `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-soc-071913-043309]` OA PDF: repository.ubn.ru.nl/bitstream/handle/2066/133583/1/133583.pdf. The earlier, narrower structured review (90 studies, core cohesion indicators). **Findings (quoted):** "(a) there is consistent support for the constrict claim for aspects of social cohesion that are spatially bounded to neighborhoods, (b) support for the constrict claim is more common in the United States than in other countries, and (c) ethnic diversity is not related to less interethnic social cohesion." Intra-neighborhood indicators: **50% support, only 9% reject**; US support 50% vs **17% other Anglo-Saxon, 26% Europe** ("American exceptionalism"). **What it disconfirms:** Putnam's spillover claim — the harm "does not spill over to more general forms of social cohesion such as generalized trust, volunteering and informal help." Also flags that the *cross-national* studies finding consistent support "fail to meet our criteria for methodological rigor" — an against-interest admission cutting toward the skeptics on the strongest pro-constrict cross-national results.

   - *Steelman of the constrict claim (Putnam's original "E Pluribus Unum," 2007, not re-saved — landmark, training-data + widely cited):* `[TRAINING-DATA]` Putnam's own data showed diversity correlating with lower trust in neighbors AND lower trust in co-ethnics ("hunkering down"), net of SES, in US communities. The two meta-analyses *confirm the neighbor-trust core of this* — Putnam was not simply wrong; he over-generalized from a real local effect to society-wide cohesion.

3. **Lancee & Dronkers (2011), "Ethnic diversity in neighborhoods and individual trust of immigrants and natives: a replication of Putnam (2007) in a West-European country."** Grade **B** (single-country, cross-sectional). Saved to corpus. A direct Netherlands replication — the kind of outside-US test the meta-analyses aggregate. Useful as a worked example of the "modest, local" pattern in a European setting (where the meta-analytic effect is weaker than the US).

### Assimilation theory (segmented vs neo-assimilation)

4. **Drouhot (2024), "Assimilation Theories in the 21st Century: Appraising Accomplishments and Future Challenges," *International Migration Review*.** Grade **C** (review/theory, but the best current synthesis). `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.1177/01979183241274747]` Saved to corpus. A three-pronged appraisal of where the segmented-assimilation (Portes-Zhou) vs new/neo-assimilation (Alba-Nee) debate stands a quarter-century on. **What it does:** surveys whether the "downward assimilation" prediction of segmented theory actually materialized. The emerging frontier consensus (also in #5/#6) is that *catastrophic second-generation decline is the exception, not the rule* — most second-gen groups show net upward mobility, which cuts against the strong segmented-pessimism reading. **This is the disconfirmation-relevant synthesis to read in full.**

5. **Kasinitz (2025), "Segmented assimilation, then and now," *Ethnic and Racial Studies*.** Grade **C**. `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.1080/01419870.2024.2445085]` Saved to corpus. A reflective re-appraisal by an author of the major NYC second-generation study (ISGMNY). Re-examines whether the 1992 segmented framework held up. Pairs with Drouhot as the "what survived" frontier read.

6. **Haller, Portes & Lynch (2011), "Dreams Fulfilled and Shattered: Determinants of Segmented Assimilation in the Second Generation," *Social Forces*.** Grade **B** (CILS-based, identification limits). OA via EuropePMC PMC3819936. Saved to corpus. The flagship *empirical* test of segmented assimilation using CILS — models how family capital, school context, and academic outcomes drive divergent second-gen paths. **What it tests:** the *predictors* of downward vs upward assimilation (it does find a downwardly-assimilating minority concentrated by specific family/context disadvantage). Note: this is Portes's own team — i.e., the pro-segmented camp; read against Waldinger & Feliciano (below) for the skeptical counter.

   - *Skeptical counter (against-interest pairing):* **Waldinger & Feliciano (2003), "Will the new second generation experience 'downward assimilation'? Segmented assimilation re-assessed," *Ethnic and Racial Studies*** (250 citations, not re-saved — found in search). `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.1080/01491987042000189196]` Argues the downward-assimilation prediction is overstated once you correct for the *immigrant generation's* low starting point and selective comparison groups. This is the key disconfirming paper inside the assimilation debate.

### Ethnic enclaves & networks (help vs trap — genuinely two-sided)

7. **Martén, Hainmueller & Hangartner (2019), "Ethnic networks can foster the economic integration of refugees," *PNAS* 116(33):16280.** Grade **A** (quasi-experimental — refugees plausibly-exogenously assigned to Swiss locations). `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1820345116]` OA. Saved to corpus. **The strongest causal evidence on the "enclave helps" side.** Refugees placed where co-ethnic networks were larger had **better** labor-market integration, directly contradicting the policymaker prior that co-ethnic concentration traps. **What it settles:** the enclave-as-trap hypothesis is not generally true; high-quality co-ethnic networks can *accelerate* integration. (Touches labor outcomes, but the mechanism is the *social network* — squarely sociology; the wage-level magnitude belongs to the econ agent.)
   - *Counterweight (the "trap" side):* the segmented-assimilation literature (#4–6) holds that enclaves can entrench *when* the surrounding context is a disadvantaged, racialized reception — i.e., enclave quality and host context decide the sign. Schüller (2016, IZA World of Labor, `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.15185/izawol.287]`, OA, not corpus-saved — review) summarizes that enclave effects are **conditional on enclave quality**, sometimes positive, sometimes negative. Net: the honest reading is *conditional*, not "enclaves good" or "enclaves bad."

### Intermarriage & ethnic attrition (the integration index — and a measurement trap)

8. **Duncan & Trejo (2025), "Immigrant Age at Arrival and the Intergenerational Transmission of Ethnic Identification Among Mexican Americans," SSRN.** Grade **B**. `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.5077491]` Saved to corpus. **Critical methodological disconfirmation that this repo should internalize:** "ethnic attrition" — as Mexican Americans assimilate and intermarry, their US-born descendants increasingly **stop identifying as Mexican/Hispanic**. Because most assimilation studies identify later generations by *self-reported ethnicity*, the most-assimilated descendants drop out of the sample, **biasing measured 3rd-gen+ progress DOWNWARD**. `[INFERENCE]` This directly threatens any CPS/ACS-based "2nd-gen-by-origin" test the repo runs (noted as pending in the brief): origin-coded generational comparisons that rely on self-ID will *understate* assimilation for groups with high intermarriage/attrition (Mexicans especially). Must be controlled via *parental* birthplace (objective) rather than self-reported ancestry.
   - *Supporting:* **Lichter, Qian & Tumin (2015), "Whom Do Immigrants Marry?," *ANNALS***. `[SOURCE: https://doi.org/10.1177/0002716215594614]` (81 cites, not corpus-saved). Documents rising immigrant-native intermarriage as an integration signal but with strong variation by national origin and skin-tone/racialization — intermarriage-as-assimilation-index is real but *stratified by race*. `[FRAMING-SENSITIVE]`

### Residential segregation / spatial assimilation (the social-stratification reading)

9. **Denton & Massey (1988), "Residential Segregation of Blacks, Hispanics, and Asians by Socioeconomic Status and Generation," *Social Science Quarterly*** (247 cites — landmark anchor, not re-saved). `[SOURCE: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/039cc40b9f96ef6f347b85846ff0eac2e7661320]` The foundational spatial-assimilation finding: rising SES and later generation reduce segregation for Hispanics and Asians, but **much less so for Black Hispanics** — segregation is racialized, not just immigrant-transient. **What it settles:** spatial assimilation (residential integration with rising SES/generation) is real for most groups but *blocked by racialization* for Afro-descended immigrants — the empirical seed of segmented theory.

10. **Xiong & Pfeifer (2023), "Complicating 'Suburbanization' and Spatial Assimilation… Southeast Asian Americans, Minneapolis-St. Paul 1990–2010," *Urban Science*.** Grade **B**, OA. Saved to corpus. A recent disconfirmation of the *simple* spatial-assimilation model: Southeast Asian groups suburbanized **without** achieving racial integration — moving to the suburbs did not equal joining majority-white neighborhoods. **What it disconfirms:** the assumption that residential mobility == assimilation; place-stratification can persist through suburbanization.

## (b) Dataset cards (NEW datasets — not in this repo's register)

Format: Source / Acquired-or-gated / Official URL / Key variables / Known quirks / what-it-settles.

### CILS — Children of Immigrants Longitudinal Study (ICPSR 20520)
- **Source:** Portes & Rumbaut; distributed via ICPSR/DSDR.
- **Acquired-or-gated:** **PUBLIC-USE, pullable now.** `[SOURCE: https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/20520]` — "public-use data files in this collection are available for access by the general public. Access does not require affiliation with an ICPSR member institution." (Free ICPSR account, click-through.)
- **Official URL:** https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/20520 ; project + codebooks: https://cmd.princeton.edu/publications/data-archives/cils
- **Key variables:** 3 waves (1992 baseline 8th/9th grade N=5,262 from 77 nationalities; T2 ~1995; T3 ~2001-03 at mean age 24, N=3,613, 69% retention). Variables: parental nativity & year of arrival, language use & loss, **self-identity / ethnic self-identification over time**, educational attainment, employment, civil status, civic participation. Sites: Miami/Ft. Lauderdale + San Diego (Cubans, Haitians, Nicaraguans, West Indians, Mexicans, Filipinos, Vietnamese, Laotians, Cambodians).
- **Known quirks:** Two metro areas only (not nationally representative). Documented sampling bias in the 2nd follow-up requiring statistical correction (see Portes & Rumbaut 2005). Cohort is now ~30 yrs old — speaks to *1990s* second-gen, not post-2010 arrivals.
- **What it settles:** **The flagship test of segmented assimilation** — directly measures who assimilates downward vs upward and the predictors (this IS the data behind Haller-Portes-Lynch #6). Settles "does downward assimilation happen, for whom" within the 1990s second gen.

### NIS — New Immigrant Survey (ICPSR 38031 R1, 38061 R2)
- **Source:** Jasso (NYU), Massey (Princeton OPR), Rosenzweig (Yale), Smith (RAND).
- **Acquired-or-gated:** **TIERED.** Public-use files (demographics, most individual characteristics after top-coding/collapsing) are **pullable now** (added 2024-25 to ICPSR, no member affiliation needed). **Restricted Version 1** (unmodified country-of-origin, education, health, occupation) = Restricted Data Use Agreement. **Restricted Version 2** (state/city/zip geo-identifiers + INS class-of-admission / visa category / country of chargeability) = **secure enclave only.** `[SOURCE: https://nis.princeton.edu/data_restricted.html]`
- **Official URL:** R1 https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/38031 · R2 https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/38061 · project https://nis.princeton.edu/
- **Key variables:** Nationally representative sample of *new legal permanent residents* (INS administrative-record frame) + their children, longitudinal. Visa/admission class, country of origin, religion, health, employment, language, **and a child sample** — the only nationally representative new-LPR panel.
- **Known quirks:** Frame is **legal** immigrants admitted to permanent residence — excludes the undocumented entirely (a major scope limit for any analysis where unauthorized status matters). The variables that best settle origin/selection questions (COA, visa, geo) are the *restricted* ones.
- **What it settles:** Selection-on-visa-class and origin effects on integration, *for the legal-immigrant population*, nationally — the representative complement to CILS's two-metro design. Public tier answers demographics; the integration-by-admission-class question needs the restricted tier.

### Add Health (Wave I–VI; UNC CPC)
- **Source:** Carolina Population Center (Harris et al.).
- **Acquired-or-gated:** **TIERED.** Public-use (subset: Wave I N=6,504 … Wave VI N=3,937) **pullable now** with a confidentiality pledge. Restricted-use (full sample + **neighborhood/contextual data, in-school friendship-nomination NETWORK data, spatial-analysis, geocodes**) = **contractual agreement, IRB, security plan, 3-yr contract, fee; no external data linkage permitted.** `[SOURCE: https://addhealth.cpc.unc.edu/restricted-use-vs-public-use-data-files/]`
- **Official URL:** https://addhealth.cpc.unc.edu/data/
- **Key variables / immigrant content:** Generation breakdown in the full sample: **1st gen 8.34% (N=1,707), 2nd gen 14.59% (N=2,987), 3rd+ 77.07% (N=15,774)** `[SOURCE: addhealth.cpc.unc.edu/.../Overview_of_Add_Health_Harris.pdf]`. Adds longitudinal *network* structure (friendship nominations) + health + later-life SES across Waves I–VI (adolescence→~40s).
- **Known quirks:** The enclave/segregation-relevant variables (network nominations, neighborhood/contextual, geocodes) are **all restricted-use** — public-use cannot settle enclave or peer-network questions. School-based sampling frame (started as adolescents in 1994-95).
- **What it settles:** Whether co-ethnic *peer networks* (not just residential enclaves) help or hinder second-gen attainment and health — but only via the restricted network files. Generation comparisons feasible on public-use.

### GSS trust items by nativity (NORC)
- **Source:** General Social Survey, NORC/U.Chicago.
- **Acquired-or-gated:** **FULLY PUBLIC, pullable now** (GSS Data Explorer / direct download; no agreement).
- **Official URL:** https://gss.norc.org/ (Data Explorer: gssdataexplorer.norc.org)
- **Key variables:** Core trust battery — **TRUST** ("most people can be trusted"), **FAIR**, **HELPFUL** — crossed with **BORN** (respondent born in US?) and **PARBORN** (parents foreign-born — enables 1st/2nd/3rd-gen split), plus **ETHNIC**/region. Repeated cross-sections 1972→present.
- **Known quirks:** Trust items not on every wave; immigrant subsample small in any single year (pool waves). `BORN`/`PARBORN` give nativity/generation but **not country of origin** at public-use granularity in most years.
- **What it settles:** **Generalized trust BY nativity/generation within the US** — i.e., do immigrants themselves arrive lower-trust and converge? This is the *micro* complement to the contextual-diversity meta-analyses (which measure native trust as a function of neighborhood diversity). The repo can run this **today** with no gating. **[Strong build candidate — cheap, public, directly tests a convergence claim.]**

### Census/ACS segregation tabulations (dissimilarity / isolation indices)
- **Source:** US Census Bureau / derived (e.g., Brown University Diversity & Disparities, or self-computed from tract tables).
- **Acquired-or-gated:** **PUBLIC** (ACS tract-level via API; precomputed indices via Brown's "Diversity and Disparities" project).
- **Official URL:** ACS API https://www.census.gov/data/developers/data-sets/acs-5year.html ; precomputed: s4.ad.brown.edu/projects/diversity
- **Key variables:** Tract-level race/ethnicity/nativity counts → **Index of Dissimilarity (D)**, **Isolation Index**, exposure indices, by group and metro, over time.
- **Known quirks:** Dissimilarity is sensitive to tract definitions and the reference group; "foreign-born" tabulations differ from race tabulations. ACS margins of error at tract level are large.
- **What it settles:** **Trends** in immigrant residential segregation and whether spatial assimilation (declining D with generation/SES) is actually happening — the macro reading behind Denton-Massey #9 and Xiong-Pfeifer #10. Repo already touches ACS for fiscal/supply-shock; the segregation-index layer is an additive computation, not new acquisition.

## (c) Genuinely NEW vs already-covered

**Already covered (do NOT re-do):** Abramitzky-Boustan intergenerational *economic* mobility (AER 2021); cluster-V cultural-transmission/deep-roots; the built first-gen assimilation income/emp-gap-closing profile; the pending CPS 2nd-gen-by-origin test. The repo's INDEX shows CILS / Add Health / NLSY97 already listed as *dataset-roadmap rows* (batch-4 US) — i.e., named but not yet researched or pulled.

**Genuinely NEW from this scout:**
1. **The trust/cohesion literature is essentially absent from the repo and is the biggest gap.** Two top-venue meta-analyses (Dinesen 2020; van der Meer-Tolsma 2014) deliver a *settled, quantified, two-sided* verdict (modest, local, US-stronger, no spillover to generalized trust). This is a distinct mechanism from economic assimilation — it's about *native and immigrant trust as a function of diversity*, which the repo has not addressed.
2. **Ethnic attrition (Duncan-Trejo 2025) as a measurement threat to the repo's own pending 2nd-gen-by-origin test.** This is *directly actionable*: it predicts the repo's planned CPS/ACS origin-coded generational comparison will *understate* assimilation unless it uses parental birthplace rather than self-ID. Not a finding *about* the world so much as a correction to the repo's method — high value.
3. **Causal enclave evidence both ways** — Martén-Hainmueller-Hangartner (PNAS 2019, enclaves *help* refugees, quasi-experimental) is a strong against-the-popular-prior result the repo lacks. The "trap vs help" question resolves to *conditional on enclave quality + host context*.
4. **The segmented-vs-neo-assimilation debate's current state** (Drouhot 2024, Kasinitz 2025): frontier consensus is that *catastrophic downward assimilation is the exception*, which moderates the pessimistic reading — complements (does not duplicate) the repo's economic-convergence profile by addressing the *non-economic* (identity, language, residence) dimensions.
5. **The social-stratification reading of segregation** (Denton-Massey; Xiong-Pfeifer): spatial assimilation works *except where racialization blocks it* — a mechanism the repo's housing-market/economics coverage does not capture.
6. **Five named, characterized datasets with exact gating + the specific variable that settles each claim** (CILS, NIS, Add Health, GSS trust-by-nativity, ACS segregation indices). **GSS trust-by-nativity is the cheapest immediate build** — fully public, directly tests immigrant trust convergence.

## (d) Disconfirmation section

Per constitution, evidence *against* the directions a restrictionist or an assimilationist prior would predict, weighted as higher-information:

- **Against the strong "diversity destroys cohesion" claim:** Both meta-analyses find the effect is **modest (r ≈ −0.026), confined to neighbor-trust and local measurement, and does NOT spill over to generalized trust, volunteering, or informal help.** van der Meer-Tolsma: "ethnic diversity is not related to less interethnic social cohesion" — if anything, contact opportunities *raise* interethnic trust. The cross-national studies most supportive of the strong constrict claim "fail to meet our criteria for methodological rigor."
- **Against the strong "enclaves trap immigrants" claim:** Martén et al. (PNAS, quasi-experimental) find co-ethnic networks **improve** refugee integration. The trap story is conditional, not general.
- **Against the strong "second-generation downward assimilation" pessimism:** The frontier reviews (Drouhot, Kasinitz) and Waldinger-Feliciano hold that catastrophic downward assimilation is **the documented exception, not the norm**, and that measured 2nd-gen "decline" is partly an artifact of the immigrant generation's low baseline and of selective comparison groups.
- **Against the dismissive "diversity has no effect / Putnam debunked" claim (the other pole):** The 2020 meta-analysis is unambiguous that the negative trust effect is **real, statistically significant, and survives covariate conditioning** — it is small, not zero, and Putnam's *neighbor-trust* core held up. Anyone citing "Putnam was debunked" is overstating.
- **Against the repo's own pending method:** Ethnic attrition (Duncan-Trejo) means a self-ID-based origin-coded generational test will be **biased toward finding LESS assimilation than reality** for high-intermarriage groups. The repo should expect (and correct for) this before reading any such result as evidence of stalled assimilation.
- **`[FRAMING-SENSITIVE]` residue:** whether r ≈ −0.026 is "negligible" or "consequential at population scale" is not resolvable by the data — it depends on what magnitude of trust-erosion one judges socially material. State the number; let the reader weight it. Likewise "enclaves help" (faster labor entry) vs "enclaves slow full integration" (language, intermarriage) can both be true on different outcomes — the sign depends on which outcome you privilege.
