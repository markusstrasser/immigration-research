## Core claims (Clemens's canon, as deployed)

1. **The Place Premium is real and huge** — observably-identical workers earn multiples more in the US than at home; selection on unobservables is modest, so most of the gap is *location*, not *person*. [Clemens-Montenegro-Pritchett 2008/2009]
2. **"Trillion-dollar bills on the sidewalk"** — removing migration barriers offers the largest unrealized global efficiency gains, plausibly on the order of *doubling world GDP* (50-150% range in the model literature). [Clemens JEP 2011]
3. **Policy advocacy** — therefore freer labor mobility is the highest-leverage development/welfare intervention available; the gains dwarf those from trade or aid liberalization. [Clemens policy writing]

## The single most decisive — and fairest — kill: Clemens's OWN 2019 model bounds his OWN headline

The strongest move in this whole dismantling is not a counterweight from a rival — it is **Clemens's own later formal model.** In **Clemens & Pritchett, "The New Economic Case for Migration Restrictions: An Assessment" (2019, *Journal of Development Economics* 138:153-164)**, the same author builds an *epidemiological* model in which migrants partly transmit origin-country (lower) productivity to the destination, governed by three parameters — **transmission (τ)** of origin TFP, **assimilation (a)**, and **congestion (c)**. The result is an **interior optimum** migration rate m\*, **not** open borders. Verbatim (corpus full-text, Clemens-Pritchett 2019):

> "it is possible that at some sufficiently high level of migration the underlying conditions that maintain high and rising TFP … might be nonlinearly undermined. In such a case, past some threshold, additional migration would begin to deteriorate host country TFP … or at the extreme, migration restrictions could be potential-Pareto-improving (Kaldor-Hicks optimal)."

> "On current evidence about the magnitudes of these parameters, dynamically efficient policy would **NOT** imply open borders but would imply **relaxations on current restrictions**."

> "One can accept a theoretical case against open borders but still make the economic case that substantial reductions in existing barriers … are potential Pareto-improving, and greatly so."

**This is the master fairness anchor — devastating precisely because it is fair.** The repo's own stance is *exactly* Clemens's published 2019 position: **direction yes (mobility is high-leverage), mass-absorption no, optimum = substantial relaxation, not open borders.** So the "double world GDP / open borders" slogan does not merely fail against the friction-heavy rival literature (Docquier, Claim 2) — **it fails against Clemens's own model.** The overclaim belongs to the *popularizers* (Caplan, the openborders.info deployment), not to Clemens, who formalized the ceiling himself. [SOURCE: Clemens-Pritchett 2019, JDE 138:153-164 / CGD WP-423 / IZA DP9730; conclusion independently re-verified at primary 2026-06-25 — Exa /answer verdict "supported", conf 1.0]

## Per-claim dismantling

### Claim 1 — The Place Premium is real and huge (Clemens-Montenegro-Pritchett 2008)

**(a) Steelman.** For observably-identical workers — same age, education, sex, even same country of birth and rural/urban origin — the wage gap from *being in the US versus at home* is a multiple, not a margin. CMP's decisive move is to confront the obvious objection head-on: maybe the gap is just positive selection on unobservables (drive, ability). They bound it, and find selection is **modest** — typically a correction factor of **1.0-1.3**, with the strongest observed case ~**1.45**; in **38 of 42 countries** the estimated place premium remains **above 2** even after a strong-selection adjustment. So most of the gap is *location*, not *person*. [SOURCE: cgdev.org Place Premium WP-148; repo audit §2]

**(b) Verdict: GRANTED in full — and the repo's audit VERIFIED it.** This is the strongest single fact in the open-borders corpus, and it kills the cheap restrictionist objection ("it's just that ambitious people migrate"). The repo's claims table marks "place-premium evidence supports very large marginal gains to movement" as **VERIFIED** (CMP 2008). Do not touch it.

**(c) Where the canon OVERCLAIMS — Move 3 (marginal-to-mass) + Move 4 (capacity erasure).** The premium is a fact about **a marginal or moderate-skill mover under *current* institutions** — i.e. moving the *next* person into an absorption system built for today's flows. It does **not** show the premium *per mover* survives when destination housing, schooling, labor absorption, and political equilibrium are stressed by **orders of magnitude** larger flows. The selection correction CMP estimate (modest) is for the population that *actually migrates today*; a mass inflow is a *different, less-selected* draw. The repo's audit is explicit: the place premium is "a **micro-to-meso fact**, not a warrant for the strongest macro slogan." [SOURCE: repo audit §2, claims-table row 5 REJECTED]

**CMP fenced the scale-warrant themselves, on page 1 — this is the fairest form of the kill.** The selection bound, verbatim: *"the degree of positive selection of existing movers on unobserved wage determinants is modest at best, typically inducing a bias of 1.0 to 1.3, with the greatest observed bias (for Peru) at 1.45"* (§3.9); and even under worst-case selection applied to all 42 countries, *"R_e would still exceed 3 in 20 out of 42 countries and would still exceed 2 … in all but four"* (premium >2 in 38 of 42). Crucially, CMP label these estimates **"'marginal' in two distinct senses: (i) it is the effect on the wage of the next person who would arrive after a small relaxation … not … the average person chosen at random; and (ii) … not the general equilibrium wages under fully open borders which involves … how mobile labor would affect capital accumulation and Total Factor Productivity."** The **doubly-marginal self-fence is the authors' own** — the place premium was *never* offered as an open-borders warrant; the popularizers removed the fence. [SOURCE: CMP 2008 §1, §3.9, verbatim corpus full-text]

**Sharp question:** *"Your selection bound is for the marginal mover under today's institutions — and CMP say so on page 1, in two senses. Why would the same per-mover premium hold for the billionth mover, into housing and labor markets you've expanded by an order of magnitude and a political equilibrium you've inverted?"*

### Claim 2 — "Trillion-dollar bills on the sidewalk" / open borders ≈ doubling world GDP (Clemens JEP 2011) — **THE central kill**

**(a) Steelman.** Clemens's *Journal of Economic Perspectives* survey is the most-cited articulation that migration-barrier removal is the single largest unrealized global efficiency gain — the model literature he reviews clusters in the **50-150% of world GDP** range, dwarfing the estimated gains from removing all remaining barriers to trade (a few percent) or capital flows. The framing ("trillion-dollar bills on the sidewalk") is a real and serious claim that the *direction* and *order of magnitude* of mobility gains are underappreciated.

**(b) Verdict: "gains are probably huge" SURVIVES; "realistically double world GDP" is REJECTED as a central forecast — and the kill is sourced to the papers themselves, several of them *against interest*.** The four-move structure:

1. **Move 2 — upper-bound laundering.** The 50-150% range is the **upper-bound model literature**, not a realistic baseline. The single most important omitted counterweight is **Docquier, Machado & Sekkat (2012)**: once you add the frictions the slogan abstracts away — incompressible moving costs, endogenous productivity, congestion, heterogeneous education quality, imperfect immigrant-native substitution, network effects — the long-run efficiency gain collapses to about **4% of world GDP**. The slogan picks the top of the range and drops the paper that prices the frictions. [SOURCE: uclouvain DP IRES 2012023; repo audit §6]
2. **Move 1 — ledger-switching.** Clemens's own Table 1 mixes **GDP**, **welfare/efficiency**, and **consumption-equivalent** estimates; the "double world GDP" slogan compresses three different ledgers into one literal-GDP object. Iregui's 15-67% figure, for instance, is **equivalent-variation welfare**, not a GDP accounting identity. [SOURCE: repo audit §1, §5]
3. **Move 3 — marginal-to-mass / "epic movements."** The doubling numbers require population movements the modelers themselves call unrealistic. Clemens says the complete-elimination figures need **at least half the population of poor countries to move**. Bradford (2012) gets +75% world output — but only if **94-97% of the poor-region workforce migrates**, which he **explicitly calls "unrealistic."** Iregui calls broad liberalization **"far from realistic."** The magnitude and the realism are inversely coupled, and the slogan keeps the magnitude while dropping the asterisk. [SOURCE: repo audit §3, §4, §5 — all against-interest author concessions]
4. **Move 4 — capacity erasure (housing binds in year 1).** The repo's own conservative calibration ($7,500/migrant/yr): **+200M → 1.4%** of world GDP, **+1B → 6.8%**, and you need **+3B additional migrants** to approach doubling territory — already a realism red flag. And the binding constraint is physical: +200M implies ~**80M housing units** (~55% of current US stock), **~1.9× current US annual housing starts sustained for 30 years**; in the 5-year absorption frame, **10M arrivals/year** hit housing capacity for only **~3.5M people/year** — housing **binds in year 1** of even the smallest large-flow scenario. Immigrant inflows already concentrate in the **least* elastic, most-regulated metros (repo corr fb-share × Saiz elasticity = **−0.41**), so the "destination housing adjusts smoothly" assumption fails exactly where the people go. [SOURCE: repo audit §repo-infra 1-3; break-even-bounds memo]

The modern bounded benchmark makes the point cleanly: **World Bank 2018** (*Moving for Prosperity*) — moving **100M** young people to high-income countries → **$1.4T/year**. Enormous, high-leverage, real — and **nowhere near doubling.** [SOURCE: repo audit §7]

**Verdict on Claim 2: "migration gains are probably very large" GRANTED; "open borders realistically doubles world GDP" REJECTED — Moves 2+1+3+4.** The honest restatement: it is an *upper-bound thought experiment*, not a central estimate.

**Sharp question:** *"Every model that gets near-doubling also says it needs 90%+ of poor-region workers to move; the one model that prices congestion, moving costs, and imperfect substitution gives 4%. Why is the upper bound the headline and the friction-priced estimate the footnote — and where do 200M people live in year 1?"*

### Claim 3 — Therefore freer mobility is the highest-leverage intervention, dwarfing trade/aid — **GRANT the direction, kill the cost-erasure (Move 4 + Move 6)**

**(a) Steelman.** Even *bounded* liberalization carries gains that compare favorably with the remaining gains from trade or capital liberalization — the World Bank's $1.4T/100M-movers benchmark and the place premium both support "mobility is unusually high-leverage." This is directionally right and under-weighted in policy debate.

**(b) Verdict: the high-leverage DIRECTION is GRANTED; the "dwarfs everything / implementation is obvious" deployment is killed by the break-even and political-economy ledgers the slogan omits.** The repo's break-even bound: the +200M gain ($1.5T) is **wiped out by a destination-incumbent loss of only ~$1,071 per existing rich-country resident per year** — a *moderate*, not catastrophic, threshold. So "the gains are so vast that incumbent costs are rounding error" is false on the repo's own conservative math: the gains are real but the break-even is *reachable*, which means destination-capacity degradation and distribution cannot be waved away (Move 4). And the slogan abstracts away the **political-economy** question entirely — who bears the transition, whether the destination polity consents, how the absorption machine is financed and built (Move 6, political-economy underspecification). [SOURCE: break-even-bounds memo §1-3; repo audit §causal-check]

**(c) The survivable version is Clemens's OWN partial-relaxation claim — and it needs no doubling.** Verbatim from the JEP: *"the emigration of **less than 5 percent** of the population of poor regions would bring global gains exceeding the gains from total elimination of all policy barriers to [goods and capital flows]."* That is the defensible, genuinely high-leverage claim — and it is *far* more modest than the doubling. Clemens flags the doubling figure as his **own explicit upper bound** and declines to claim the incumbent effect is positive: his back-of-envelope (half the poor region emigrates → migrants gain ~$23T ≈ 38% of global GDP) carries his own caveat that for nonmigrants *"the net effect of these other changes could theoretically be negative, zero, or positive,"* and the abstract warns the estimates *"are sensitive to assumptions."* **The <5% claim is what survives; the doubling is the flagged ceiling** — and the gap between them is the entire dismantling. [SOURCE: Clemens JEP 2011, verbatim — cgdev.org PDF / aeaweb]

**Sharp question:** *"Your own conservative calibration says a ~$1,071-per-incumbent annual loss erases the +200M gain. That's not catastrophe — it's a bad decade of local housing and service strain. Why is that a rounding error rather than the whole ballgame?"*

## What survives (generous — and the fairness anchor)

Clemens is the **most empirically careful** figure in this whole memo, and the dismantling is surgical precisely because so much survives intact:

1. **The Place Premium is real, large, and not a selection artifact.** Location explains most of the wage gap for identical workers; selection on unobservables is modest (1.0-1.3). VERIFIED in the repo audit. The cheap "ambitious people self-select" objection is **dead**. [SURVIVES]
2. **Migration gains are probably among the largest remaining global efficiency gains.** Direction and order of magnitude survive — the World Bank's $1.4T/100M-movers benchmark alone is a serious number. The case for *more* mobility is strong. [SURVIVES]
3. **Clemens's Mariel rigor — which the repo ADOPTS.** This is the fairness anchor: Clemens-Hunt (2019) is the work that established Borjas's Mariel "10-30% harm" as a ~17-observations-per-year compositional artifact — and the repo **agrees** (confidence-ladder entry 44). The same author whose careful re-analysis the repo *relies on* is being dismantled only at the magnitude/absorption margin, never at the rigor. [SURVIVES — repo-adopted]
4. **Clemens's OWN hedges are more honest than the slogan that quotes him.** He distinguishes **complete vs partial** liberalization, states plainly that the big numbers need **"epic movements of people"**, and presents the 50-150% range as *model literature*, not a forecast. The kill in Claim 2 lands on the **popularized deployment** (openborders.info's literal "double world GDP," the slogan as wielded), **not** on Clemens's hedged prose. A fair reading is that Clemens is closer to "mobility is unusually high-leverage" than to "expect a doubling" — which is the survivable position. [SURVIVES]
5. **Clemens's OWN 2019 model is the repo's position.** Clemens-Pritchett (2019) formalizes an **interior optimum** — "dynamically efficient policy would NOT imply open borders but would imply relaxations on current restrictions." The repo agrees with Clemens-the-modeler against the open-borders-slogan that quotes Clemens-the-surveyor. The author already conceded the ceiling. [SURVIVES — and it is the strongest fairness anchor in the memo]

## One-line honest verdict

**Clemens is right that labor mobility is plausibly the single largest underused source of global welfare gain, the Place Premium is real and not selection, and his Mariel rigor is good enough that the repo adopts it. The canon fails only where the upper-bound *model-literature* range is laundered into a realistic "double world GDP" central forecast — a claim that needs 90%+ of poor-region workers to move (its own authors call that unrealistic), compresses GDP/welfare/consumption ledgers, drops the friction-priced ~4% estimate, ignores a housing-absorption frontier that binds in year 1, and — most decisively — contradicts Clemens's OWN 2019 model, which puts the optimum at a substantial *relaxation*, not open borders.** The gains are huge in principle and high-leverage in practice; the doubling is a thought experiment Clemens himself fenced — Moves 2+1+3+4 (+6 on the politics).
