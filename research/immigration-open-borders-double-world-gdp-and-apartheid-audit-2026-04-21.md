# Open Borders “double world GDP” and “apartheid” audit

**Question:** Is the Open Borders claim that open borders could "double world GDP" realistic, and is the site's "apartheid" framing analytically sound?  
**Tier:** Deep | **Date:** 2026-04-21  
**Ground truth:** The repo already held a bounded open-borders calibration and local-capacity critique in `sources/immigration-causal/` and `research/immigration-causal-*`. This memo extends that work by reading the cited papers directly and auditing the rhetoric against the paper claims. [DATA]

## Bottom line

1. **`Freer global labor mobility likely creates very large gains`** survives. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.83] [SOURCE: https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/213751530086130618/pdf/Moving-for-prosperity-global-migration-and-labor-markets-policy-research-report.pdf]
2. **`Open borders would realistically double world GDP`** does **not** survive as a central forecast. The strongest papers support this only in highly stylized long-run models with implausibly large migration volumes and weak real-world constraints. [SOURCE: https://www.ssc.wisc.edu/~jkennan/research/OpenBorders.pdf] [SOURCE: https://freit.org/WorkingPapers/Papers/Immigration/FREIT432.pdf] [SOURCE: https://digitallibrary.un.org/record/536477/files/dp2003-27.pdf] [SOURCE: https://sites.uclouvain.be/econ/DP/IRES/2012023.pdf]
3. The Open Borders page is directionally right that migration barriers may be one of the largest remaining global inefficiencies, but it presents an **upper-bound model-literature slogan as if it were a realistic baseline**. [INFERENCE]
4. The site's **apartheid analogy** is best read as a **moral analogy about coercive exclusion by birth/origin**, not as a precise institutional or empirical analogy. As rhetoric it has force; as analysis it is too blunt. [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/global-apartheid/] [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/blog/2015/01/]

## Full-paper audit

### 1) Clemens 2011 — literature summary, not direct proof

Michael Clemens' JEP article is the right starting point because it is what the Open Borders page is effectively leaning on. It does **not** itself estimate "doubling world GDP" from observed policy reform. It surveys then-existing model estimates and notes that labor-mobility-barrier removal often yields gains in the **50-150% of world GDP** range. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.83]

Important caveats from the paper itself:

1. The largest gains require **"epic movements of people"**; Clemens explicitly says at least half the population of poor countries would need to move for the complete-elimination numbers. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.83]
2. Clemens distinguishes **complete** vs **partial** relaxation. His partial-elimination point is much more defensible than the "double world GDP" slogan. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.83]
3. Table 1 mixes **GDP**, **welfare/efficiency**, and **consumption-equivalent** style estimates. The Open Borders slogan compresses these into one rhetorical object. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.83]

Verdict: Clemens supports **"migration gains are probably huge"**, not **"realistically expect a doubling."**

### 2) Place Premium (Clemens, Montenegro, Pritchett 2008) — real mechanism, weak scale warrant

The Place Premium paper is the strongest evidence that **location itself** explains an enormous share of wage differences for observably similar workers. It also directly addresses migrant selection. The paper concludes that positive selection on unobservables is **typically modest**, often around **1.0 to 1.3**, with the strongest observed case around **1.45**; in 38 of 42 countries, even after strong-selection adjustments, the estimated place premium remains above 2. [SOURCE: https://www.cgdev.org/publication/place-premium-wage-differences-identical-workers-across-us-border-working-paper-148]

This matters because it kills the cheap objection that the entire place premium is just "ambitious people move." It does **not** solve the external-validity problem:

1. The paper is still about the gain to a **marginal or moderate-skill mover under current institutions**. [SOURCE: https://www.cgdev.org/publication/place-premium-wage-differences-identical-workers-across-us-border-working-paper-148]
2. It does not show that the same premium survives when destination housing, schools, labor absorption, and political equilibrium are stressed by **orders of magnitude** larger flows. [INFERENCE]

Verdict: the place premium is real and large, but it is a **micro-to-meso fact**, not a warrant for the strongest macro slogan.

### 3) Kennan 2013 — strongest upper-bound case, still heavily stylized

John Kennan's paper is one of the more favorable formal models for the open-borders side. It estimates that effective world labor supply would roughly **double** and that the average net gain for a worker in non-frontier countries would exceed **$10,000 per year**, more than doubling average income in that sample. [SOURCE: https://www.ssc.wisc.edu/~jkennan/research/OpenBorders.pdf]

But the model's strong results ride on strong assumptions:

1. Factor-price equalization logic and a setting where country-specific productivity gaps dominate, while physical capital endowments do not explain low wages. [SOURCE: https://www.ssc.wisc.edu/~jkennan/research/OpenBorders.pdf]
2. A simple migration-cost specification. [SOURCE: https://www.ssc.wisc.edu/~jkennan/research/OpenBorders.pdf]
3. Gradual capital adjustment that largely removes rich-country wage losses over time. [SOURCE: https://www.ssc.wisc.edu/~jkennan/research/OpenBorders.pdf]

Verdict: this is a serious theoretical upper-bound paper, but still not a realistic medium-run policy forecast.

### 4) Bradford 2012 — dynamic, explicit, and still unrealistic at scale

Bradford's paper is useful because it is more explicit about dynamics, poverty, and capital adjustment. It finds that removing rich-country migration barriers could raise world output by about **75%** in the model and greatly reduce poverty. [SOURCE: https://freit.org/WorkingPapers/Papers/Immigration/FREIT432.pdf]

The critical passage is the one the slogan tends to glide past: Bradford says his result implies that roughly **94-97% of the poor-region workforce** would move under free migration, which he calls **unrealistic**. [SOURCE: https://freit.org/WorkingPapers/Papers/Immigration/FREIT432.pdf]

Verdict: Bradford strengthens the "gains could be huge in principle" claim while simultaneously weakening the "realistic doubling" claim.

### 5) Iregui 2003/2005 — large gains, but welfare-equivalent and explicitly unrealistic

Iregui uses a multiregion CGE framework and gets **15-67% of world GDP** in equivalent-variation welfare gains when both skilled and unskilled labor migrate. [SOURCE: https://digitallibrary.un.org/record/536477/files/dp2003-27.pdf]

Two important points:

1. These are **welfare-equivalent gains**, not cleanly observed GDP accounting identities. [SOURCE: https://digitallibrary.un.org/record/536477/files/dp2003-27.pdf]
2. The paper says broad liberalization is **"far from realistic"** because of social and political resistance. [SOURCE: https://digitallibrary.un.org/record/536477/files/dp2003-27.pdf]

Verdict: supportive of large theoretical gains, not of realism.

### 6) Docquier, Machado, Sekkat 2012/2015 — the main omitted counterweight

This is the most important paper the Open Borders page does not foreground. The authors explicitly argue that once one adds **incompressible moving costs, endogenous productivity, congestion, heterogeneous education quality, imperfect substitution between immigrants and natives, and network effects**, the efficiency gains are only around **4% of world GDP** in the long run. [SOURCE: https://sites.uclouvain.be/econ/DP/IRES/2012023.pdf]

Verdict: this does not kill the open-borders economic case. It does kill the idea that the old near-doubling estimates are a robust central baseline.

### 7) World Bank 2018 — large but bounded modern benchmark

The World Bank's *Moving for Prosperity* gives a much more policy-scaled benchmark: moving **100 million young people** from developing to high-income countries would produce about **$1.4 trillion** in annual income gains. [SOURCE: https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/213751530086130618/pdf/Moving-for-prosperity-global-migration-and-labor-markets-policy-research-report.pdf]

That is an enormous number. It is also nowhere near "double world GDP." The World Bank is much closer to **"mobility is unusually high leverage"** than to **"mass opening would literally double output."**

### 8) Khanna et al. 2026 — migration can raise origin-country domestic income too

Khanna et al. find that international migrant-income shocks in the Philippines raise not just migrant income but also **domestic income** over time, with much of the long-run gain coming from domestic channels. [SOURCE: https://deanyang-econ.github.io/deanyang/files/khanna-et-al-2026-abundance-from-abroad-migrant-income-and-long-run-economic-development.pdf]

This is important because it strengthens the case that migration gains are not limited to migrants and destination employers. But it still does not validate the "double world GDP" claim.

## Repo-infra cross-check

### 1) The repo's own Clemens calibration does not imply realistic doubling

The local script `sources/immigration-causal/scripts/open_borders_baseline.py` is deliberately conservative. Using **$7,500** annual gain per migrant, it yields:

1. **+200M migrants** -> **$1.5T** annual gain -> **1.4% of world GDP** [DATA: `sources/immigration-causal/data/clemens/open_borders_calibration_baseline.json`]
2. **+500M migrants** -> **$3.75T** -> **3.4% of world GDP** [DATA]
3. **+1B migrants** -> **$7.5T** -> **6.8% of world GDP** [DATA]
4. **+3B migrants** -> **$22.5T** -> **20.5% of world GDP** [DATA]

So the repo's own bounded calibration says: under a **policy-sized** range from +200M to +1B, gains are large but nowhere near doubling; to get into Clemens-style extreme territory, you need **+3B** additional migrants, which is already a red flag for realism. [INFERENCE]

### 2) Housing is the first binding constraint in every large-flow scenario

The local review `sources/immigration-causal/data/clemens/gpt54_calibration_review.md` uses a 5-year absorption horizon and current U.S. housing starts. It concludes that **housing/construction binds in year 1** even in the smallest large-flow scenario:

1. **+50M over 5 years** -> **10M arrivals/year** against housing capacity for only about **3.5M people/year** [DATA: `sources/immigration-causal/data/clemens/gpt54_calibration_review.md`]
2. **+200M over 5 years** -> **40M arrivals/year**, requiring about **16M housing units/year** against current starts of **1.4M/year** [DATA]

This does not prove long-run impossibility. It does prove that the "just move the labor and collect the gains" story is not a realistic short-run implementation story. [INFERENCE]

### 3) The repo's local data confirm immigrant concentration in inelastic, regulated metros

From `sources/immigration-causal/data/analysis/saiz_msa_rent_immigrant_2022.parquet`, metros in the **lowest elasticity quartile** have:

1. median foreign-born share **11.6%** vs **4.4%** in the highest-elasticity quartile [DATA]
2. median rent **$1,343** vs **$929** [DATA]
3. median WRLURI **0.386** vs **-0.913**, consistent with tighter regulation where immigrant concentration is higher [DATA]

The same file shows a correlation of **-0.41** between foreign-born share and housing elasticity, and **+0.44** between foreign-born share and WRLURI. [DATA]

This materially weakens the naive model assumption that destination housing adjusts smoothly.

### 4) The repo's labor-market evidence removes one easy offset

The repo's own E-Verify and sanctuary analyses do **not** find the large native wage gains that would make the native-cost ledger easier to absorb:

1. E-Verify exposed-sector estimate for low-education natives: about **+0.5%**, statistically null [DATA: `sources/immigration-causal/data/analysis/everify_twfe_results.csv`]
2. Sanctuary treated estimate for low-education exposed sectors: about **+0.5%**, statistically null [DATA: `sources/immigration-causal/data/analysis/sanctuary_twfe_results.csv`]

So the strongest repo-owned support for realism is **not** "natives get large labor-market compensation." The gains, if real, are mainly migrant/global gains rather than clear low-skill-native wage gains. [INFERENCE]

### 5) Local burden is not simply "immigrants arrive -> town fills"

The repo's internal-vs-immigrant newcomer memo finds that at the median county, native internal migration is roughly **33×** recent-immigrant annualized inflow. [SOURCE: research/immigration-causal-internal-vs-immigrant-newcomers.md]

That matters because it means:

1. housing stress in the U.S. is already not a pure immigration object [INFERENCE]
2. using observed local stress to dismiss global migration gains is too crude [INFERENCE]
3. but using existing local stress as proof of painless absorption is also too crude [INFERENCE]

## Causal check

### Observation

The Open Borders page and adjacent rhetoric imply that open borders are the efficient and near-obvious way to **roughly double world GDP**, and that moral objections to the border regime can be analogized to apartheid. [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/double-world-gdp/] [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/blog/tag/apartheid/]

### Null generative process

Workers move from low-productivity places to high-productivity places -> migrant earnings rise sharply -> employer/consumer surplus rises -> global output rises -> over time capital deepens and rich-country wage losses attenuate. [SOURCE: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.25.3.83] [SOURCE: https://www.ssc.wisc.edu/~jkennan/research/OpenBorders.pdf]

### Residual question

Does this mechanism still yield **near-doubling** once one adds:

1. realistic migration volumes
2. housing/construction bottlenecks
3. congestion and school/service burden
4. imperfect substitution and political backlash
5. institution/productivity spillovers and selection dilution?

### Geometry

This is not a sharp-break question. It is a **gradual-erosion / parameter-sensitivity** question: the core mechanism is real, but the estimated size erodes as realistic frictions are added. [INFERENCE]

### Competing hypotheses

| Hypothesis | Prior | Why |
|---|---:|---|
| H1: near-doubling is realistic under long-run open borders | 0.20 | Supported by classic upper-bound models |
| H2: gains are real but strongly attenuated; realistic effect is probably single-digit to low-double-digit percent of world GDP | 0.65 | Supported by later friction-heavy models + repo capacity evidence |
| H3: the big-gains literature is mostly artifact and gains are small or trivial | 0.15 | Inconsistent with place-premium and World Bank evidence |

### Specificity ranking

| Hypothesis | Temporal | Magnitude | Scope | Mechanism | New prediction | Total |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| H1 | 2 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 1 | 9 |
| H2 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 15 |
| H3 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 5 |

**New prediction checked:** if H2 is right, repo housing data should show immigrant concentration in low-elasticity/high-regulation metros. It does. [DATA: `sources/immigration-causal/data/analysis/saiz_msa_rent_immigrant_2022.parquet`]

### Commit

**Most likely cause (65%):** the Open Borders slogan is an **upper-bound extrapolation error**. It takes a real mechanism with large marginal gains and extends it across implausibly large flows while underpricing housing, congestion, composition, and political constraints. [INFERENCE]

**Top alternative (20%):** the classic literature is basically right in the long run, and current skepticism is mostly short-run state-capacity myopia. This survives because the place premium is large, and later models may understate dynamic adaptation. [INFERENCE]

**Falsifier:** a modern, transparent GE model with realistic migration volumes, housing/build constraints, endogenous capital, imperfect substitution, and institutional feedbacks still producing robust gains near **50%+ of world GDP** under politically plausible transition paths would materially raise confidence in H1. [INFERENCE]

**Decision impact:** do **not** treat "double world GDP" as a realistic central estimate. Treat it as a provocative upper-bound slogan. A serious position should say: labor mobility likely offers some of the largest remaining global gains, but realistic implementation is bottlenecked by housing, local capacity, politics, and destination composition. [INFERENCE]

## The apartheid framing

### What survives

1. Border regimes and apartheid both involve **coercive exclusion tied to a birth trait** rather than an individual act. [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/global-apartheid/] [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/blog/2015/01/]
2. The analogy correctly highlights that nationality is morally arbitrary in a way that many people fail to notice because borders are normalized. [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/blog/2015/01/]
3. When restrictionists describe voluntary immigrant clustering as "apartheid communities," Open Borders is right to call that confused; voluntary ethnic clustering is not the same thing as state-enforced segregation. [SOURCE: https://openborders.info/blog/victor-davis-hanson/]

### What fails

1. Apartheid was a specific regime of **internal racial domination** with a distinct legal architecture, violence pattern, and political purpose. Modern border regimes are not literally the same institutional object. [INFERENCE]
2. The analogy does little causal work on the key empirical question here: whether mass labor mobility under modern state-capacity, housing, and political constraints can be absorbed without large transitional costs. [INFERENCE]
3. As rhetoric it tends to **moralize away the strongest realist objections** rather than answer them. [INFERENCE]

### Verdict on the analogy

The best formulation is:

1. **Moral analogy:** partly valid
2. **Institutional analogy:** overstated
3. **Economic evidence:** not evidence at all

## Final verdict

The Open Borders page is **not nonsense**, but it is **not realistic in the way it presents itself**.

The defensible version is:

1. labor mobility is probably one of the largest underused sources of global welfare gain [SOURCE]
2. even moderate increases in migration could generate gains that compare favorably with the remaining gains from trade/capital liberalization [SOURCE]
3. the classic "double world GDP" line is an **upper-bound thought experiment**, not a realistic central forecast [SOURCE] [DATA]
4. the apartheid framing is a **moral provocation**, not a substitute for empirical or causal analysis [SOURCE] [INFERENCE]
