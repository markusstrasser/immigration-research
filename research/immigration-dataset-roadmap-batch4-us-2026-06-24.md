# Immigration — Dataset Roadmap, Batch 4 (US micro, 2026-06-24)

Fourth batch. Supplements the 32 datasets already proposed across
`immigration-dataset-roadmap.md` (12), `immigration-dataset-roadmap-additions-2026-06-24.md` (5),
`immigration-dataset-roadmap-batch3-2026-06-24.md` (12+3 honorable mentions), and the held stack in
`immigration-dataset-register.md`. **None of these repeats the 32 already on the books.**

**Axis = US micro.** Gaps targeted, in priority order requested by parent:
(1) crime **VICTIMIZATION** — the victim side, which the corpus entirely lacks (NCVS);
(2) **second-generation longitudinal** — CILS, the canonical 2nd-gen panel;
(3) **health micro** — NHANES (nativity + biomarkers + utilization), CDC WONDER mortality (Hispanic paradox);
(4) **longitudinal labor** — NLSY97 immigrant sample;
(5) **program / food security** — CPS Food Security Supplement by nativity.

All URLs verified live via search 2026-06-24 unless tagged `[UNVERIFIED]`.

Join-key legend for the warehouse: `state_fips`, `county_fips`, `POBP`/origin-country, `education_bucket`,
`citizenship`/`nativity`, `topic`, `FY/year`. A dataset with no nativity/status field is flagged
ecological/calibration, not a status dataset.

## Axis 1 — Crime VICTIMIZATION (the victim side; highest priority, corpus has zero)

### NCVS-CITIZENSHIP — National Crime Victimization Survey, with citizenship/nativity (2017+)
**Source:** Bureau of Justice Statistics (BJS); fielded by the Census Bureau · **Access:** PUBLIC (multiple tiers, all free)
- Public-Use Files at NACJD/ICPSR — annual + concatenated, broad variable set, free with ICPSR account, SDA online analysis.
- BJS "Select Datasets" via **API** (commonly-used variables, concatenated 1993→) — directly scriptable.
- N-DASH dashboard (interactive). **Restricted** geo/detail via Census FSRDC only.
**Official:** BJS data-user hub https://bjs.ojp.gov/ncvs-data-user-page · concatenated 1992-2023 https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/NACJD/studies/38963 (DOI 10.3886/ICPSR38963.v1) · 2023 annual https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/NACJD/studies/38962 (DOI 10.3886/ICPSR38962.v1) · concatenated legacy 1992-2024 https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/NACJD/studies/39273 [SOURCE: icpsr.umich.edu/web/NACJD/studies/38963]
**Format / size:** SAS/SPSS/Stata/ASCII/R; hierarchical (household / person / incident record-types); ~240k persons/yr in recent samples. Concatenated files for trend.
**Key variables — DOES IT HAVE NATIVITY? YES, since 2017.** Victimization type (rape/sexual assault, robbery, aggravated + simple assault, personal larceny; household burglary, theft, MV theft), reporting-to-police, victim-offender relationship, injury, weapon, self-protection; victim age/race/sex/income. **Citizenship + foreign-born status added to the instrument in 2017** — the variable that powers Xie & Baumer (*Criminology* 2021, 59(4):610-644, PMC8849556): foreign-born status confers PROTECTION against victimization; the protection does not extend to "ambiguous citizenship" respondents resembling the undocumented. [SOURCE: pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8849556]
**Why it fits:** THE victim-side dataset the entire corpus lacks. Every held crime source (TX-DPS, USSC, BJS-SPI, NIBRS) is offender/arrest/incarceration — the *offending* side. NCVS is the nationally-representative measure of who gets victimized, and since 2017 it splits by citizenship/nativity. Directly answers "are immigrants disproportionately victims?" — a frame entirely absent from the stack, and one that reframes the immigration-crime debate (immigrants as under-protected victims, not just as a putative offender population).
**Limit / honesty:** citizenship/nativity only from the **2017 redesign onward** (pre-2017 concatenated years have no nativity) — so the nativity panel is short (2017→), not the full 1992+ series. "Citizenship status" is self-reported and the undocumented are identifiable only by inference (the "ambiguous citizenship" proxy Xie & Baumer use). NCVS undercounts the most marginalized; public file has limited geography (no clean state below the State-Level Analysis tool). The instrument was redesigned again in 2024 (legacy vs new files — confirm nativity-variable continuity across the break before building a 2017-2024 panel). [SOURCE: bjs.ojp.gov/ncvs-data-user-page]
**Graph fit:** new `crime_victimization` domain; `citizenship`/`nativity` × victimization-type × year (2017+); the victim-side complement to the offender-side crime layer. National; no clean state join on the public file.

## Axis 2 — Second-generation longitudinal (canonical 2nd-gen panel)

### CILS — Children of Immigrants Longitudinal Study (Portes & Rumbaut)
**Source:** Alejandro Portes (Princeton) & Rubén G. Rumbaut (UC-Irvine); archived at DSDR/ICPSR · **Access:** PUBLIC — ICPSR download (free account) + online SDA analysis (crosstabs/frequencies without download). Princeton CMD hosts a project mirror.
**Official:** ICPSR 20520 https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/20520 (DOI 10.3886/ICPSR20520.v3) · variables https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/20520/variables · Princeton CMD archive https://cmd.princeton.edu/publications/data-archives/cils [SOURCE: icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/20520]
**Format / size:** three waves (1992 baseline 8th/9th graders; 1995-96 follow-up; 2001-03 early-adult follow-up) + parental survey; SAS/SPSS/Stata. **N=5,262** baseline, 4,288 retained at wave 2 (81.5%); **77 nationalities**, two metros (Miami/Ft. Lauderdale FL + San Diego CA); evenly split foreign-born / US-born.
**Key variables — DOES IT HAVE NATIVITY? YES, it is BUILT on it.** Second generation defined as US-born with ≥1 foreign-born parent OR foreign-born brought young. Captures: national origin, generational status (1.5 vs 2nd), language use/preference, ethnic self-identity, self-esteem, academic attainment + dropout, aspirations, family SES, discrimination perception; parental survey gives direct immigrant-parent characteristics. Later-wave outcomes: educational attainment, employment, **incarceration/arrest self-report**, downward-assimilation indicators.
**Why it fits:** THE canonical second-generation dataset and the empirical basis of *segmented assimilation* theory (Portes-Rumbaut). The corpus's mobility evidence is aggregate/admin (ABJP rank-rank, Opportunity Atlas); CILS is the person-level *longitudinal* second-gen panel that traces the SAME children from adolescence into early adulthood — directly testing whether children of low-skill/humanitarian immigrants assimilate upward or downward, the structural counter to the "low-skill parent ⇒ permanent fiscal drag" frame. Adds a *mechanism* layer (identity, language, school) the aggregate sources cannot.
**Limit / honesty:** two metros only (Miami + San Diego) — NOT nationally representative; over-weights the largest origin groups in those cities (Cuban, Haitian, Nicaraguan, Mexican, Filipino, Vietnamese). Cohort is dated (baseline 1992, born ~1977-80) — pre-frontier for *recent* flows. Attrition by wave 3, with mild higher-SES retention bias the PIs document. Outcomes self-reported. Not a fiscal dataset — a mobility/assimilation panel that feeds the second-gen node, not a ledger line.
**Graph fit:** `second_generation` longitudinal node; origin-country × generation × wave; origin-country joins the origin layer; pairs with ABJP-MOBILITY (aggregate) and Opportunity-Atlas-2ndgen as the person-level mechanism panel.

## Axis 3 — Health micro (nativity + biomarkers + utilization)

### NHANES — National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (nativity + exam biomarkers)
**Source:** CDC / National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) · **Access:** PUBLIC — direct `.xpt` (SAS transport) download from wwwn.cdc.gov, no registration, fully scriptable. (Detailed country-of-birth beyond a few categories + small-cell geography are RESTRICTED via the NCHS RDC.)
**Official:** demographics codebook 2021-2023 (DEMO_L) https://wwwn.cdc.gov/Nchs/Data/Nhanes/Public/2021/DataFiles/DEMO_L.htm · 2017-2018 (DEMO_J) https://wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/data/nhanes/public/2017/datafiles/DEMO_J.htm · variable-list search https://wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/search/variablelist.aspx?Component=Demographics [SOURCE: wwwn.cdc.gov/Nchs/Data/Nhanes/Public/2021/DataFiles/DEMO_L.htm]
**Format / size:** continuous 2-year cycles 1999-2000 → Aug2021-Aug2023 (DEMO_L); ~9,000-10,000 examined/cycle; interview + **physical exam + lab biomarkers** (the distinguishing feature vs every other survey here). Demographics, exam, lab, questionnaire components as separate linkable files (join on SEQN).
**Key variables — DOES IT HAVE NATIVITY? YES (confirmed in codebook).** `DMDBORN4` "In what country were you born?" (country-of-birth, collapsed categories on public file); `DMDCITZN` "Are you a citizen of the United States?"; `DMDYRUSZ`/`DMDYRUS` "length of time in the US." Plus measured biomarkers (BP, BMI, HbA1c, lipids, kidney/liver panels, nutritional status), chronic-condition questionnaires, health-insurance + healthcare-access/utilization modules (usual source of care, recent visits, unmet need), Rx use.
**Why it fits:** the ONLY US source linking **nativity → measured clinical biomarkers → insurance/utilization** at the person level. The held health stack (MEPS payer cells, Emergency-Medicaid aggregates, HCUP cost) is self-report cost or admin payer; NHANES adds *objective health status* by nativity — letting us test the "healthy-immigrant effect" / Hispanic-paradox at the biomarker level (foreign-born often healthier on exam despite lower utilization), which directly bears on whether immigrants are a health-cost driver or net-favorable risk pool. Long continuous series, free, scriptable.
**Limit / honesty:** country-of-birth is COLLAPSED on the public file (e.g., Mexico / Other-Hispanic / Other vary by cycle) — fine origin granularity and any sub-national geography are RDC-restricted; no state. Per-cycle sample (~10k) is small for rare conditions or fine nativity×condition cells — pool cycles (NCHS provides combined-cycle weights). No legal status. Cross-sectional (not longitudinal within person). [SOURCE: wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/search/variablelist.aspx?Component=Demographics]
**Graph fit:** `health` layer; `nativity`/`citizenship`/years-in-US × biomarker × insurance/utilization; national (no state); biomarker complement to MEPS cost cells and NHIS incidence.

## Axis 5 — Program / food security (nativity)

### CPS-FSS — CPS Food Security Supplement, by nativity
**Source:** US Census Bureau (fields it) for USDA Economic Research Service (ERS, sponsor/analyst) · **Access:** PUBLIC — direct ASCII + CSV microdata from Census, no registration, fully scriptable; NBER mirror provides codebooks + SAS/Stata/SPSS input statements. Annual since 1995.
**Official:** ERS data-access hub https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-security-in-the-united-states · 2023 Census data page https://www.census.gov/data/datasets/2023/demo/cps/cps-food-security.html · Dec-2023 technical doc (ERS) https://www.ers.usda.gov/media/6313/december-2023-cps-food-security-supplement-data-file-technical-documentation.pdf · foreign-born CPS variable confirmation https://www.census.gov/topics/population/foreign-born/data/tables/cps-tables.html [SOURCE: census.gov/data/datasets/2023/demo/cps/cps-food-security.html]
**Format / size:** December supplement to the monthly CPS; ~127k person records / ~42k households per year (Dec-2023: 126,832 records, 30,863 supplement-completing households, 72,492 person records). Data files [~12 MB gz] + replicate-weight files [~25 MB].
**Key variables — DOES IT HAVE NATIVITY? YES (via the CPS core carried on every record).** USDA 18-item Household Food Security Scale → food-secure / low / very-low food security; child food security; SNAP/WIC/school-meal participation; coping behaviors; spending. The Basic-CPS variables on each record carry **nativity, citizenship, year of entry, world region of birth, and immigrant generation** (the same CPS nativity items behind the Census "Foreign-Born CPS Data Tables"). So food insecurity can be split by nativity/citizenship/years-in-US directly at the person/household level.
**Why it fits:** food insecurity is a benefit-eligibility-and-hardship channel the cost stack touches only via SNAP aggregates (stage5 `snap_state_2023.csv`) — CPS-FSS gives **person-level food-insecurity by nativity**, letting us test whether immigrant households are more food-insecure (a hardship signal) AND whether they under-use food assistance despite eligibility (the "chilling effect" / lower-take-up channel relevant to the net-fiscal question). Annual 30-year series; pairs the program-participation side with the held SIPP/SNAP layers.
**Limit / honesty:** CPS undercounts the unauthorized and has no legal-status field — nativity/citizenship only. Supplement-completing subsample (~72k persons) shrinks fine nativity×food-security×state cells; food-insecurity items are household-level. Year of entry + region of birth are coarse (not specific country). Use ERS-published thresholds for the 18-item scale rather than re-deriving. [SOURCE: ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-security-in-the-united-states]
**Graph fit:** `program_participation` / `food_security` layer; `nativity`/`citizenship`/year-of-entry × food-security-status × SNAP/WIC × year; state available (CPS) but cells thin; complements stage5 SNAP aggregates with person-level take-up.

## Axis 4 — Longitudinal labor (national second-gen earnings panel)

### NLSY97 — National Longitudinal Survey of Youth 1997 (parental nativity / second-gen labor panel)
**Source:** US Bureau of Labor Statistics (NLS program); fielded by NORC · **Access:** PUBLIC — public-use via the online **NLS Investigator** extraction tool (free; account only to save extracts) + full bulk download; SAS/Stata/SPSS/CSV. State/county/tract geocode is RESTRICTED (BLS SAP application, US-only).
**Official:** overview https://www.bls.gov/nls/nlsy97.htm · accessing data https://www.bls.gov/nls/getting-started/accessing-data.htm · race/ethnicity/citizenship topical guide https://nlsinfo.org/content/cohorts/nlsy97/topical-guide/household/race-ethnicity-citizenship · parent-characteristics (nativity items) https://www.nlsinfo.org/content/cohorts/nlsy97/topical-guide/family-background/parent-characteristics [SOURCE: bls.gov/nls/nlsy97.htm]
**Format / size:** **N=8,984** born 1980-84, US-resident at 1997 baseline (ages 12-16); **Rounds 1 (1997-98) → 20 (2021-22)**, annual to 2011 then biennial. Cross-sectional sample (6,748) + supplemental Black/Hispanic oversamples (2,236). Extensive labor, education, income, **government-program participation**, health, family-formation, ASVAB.
**Key variables — DOES IT HAVE NATIVITY? YES, but mostly PARENTAL (this is the honest nuance).** The round-1 Parent Questionnaire records whether the responding parent was US-born (`BornUS_PR`), and for foreign-born parents their **country of birth** and **year first came to the US** (`FirstLiveUS_PR`); created `PAR_ASIA / PAR_EUROPE / PAR_AFRICA / PAR_CARIBBEAN / PAR_CENTRAL_AMERICA / ...` count parents born in each world region; home-language-not-English flag. Respondent-level: `KEY!RACE`, `KEY!ETHNICITY` (Hispanic), citizenship. Outcomes across 20 rounds: wages/employment trajectory, educational attainment, welfare/program receipt, health, **arrests/incarceration**, fertility.
**Why it fits:** a **nationally-representative** longitudinal panel that follows one cohort from adolescence to ~age 40, with **parental nativity + region of birth** — i.e. a *second-generation* labor-and-earnings panel. It is the national complement to CILS (which is two metros, dated, school-focused): NLSY97 gives national labor-market trajectories of children of immigrants vs children of the US-born over the full early-career arc, the exact human-capital/earnings dynamics the lifetime-NPV work approximates from ACS cross-sections. Adds program-participation and crime-self-report on the SAME longitudinal record.
**Limit / honesty — RE-FRAME from "immigrant sample":** the cohort is US-resident born 1980-84, so the great majority are **US-born**; respondent nativity is captured chiefly **through the parents**, and there is **NO dedicated immigrant oversample** (only Black + Hispanic supplemental samples). So NLSY97 is best used as a *second-generation* panel (respondent = child of immigrant via parental country-of-birth), NOT as a first-generation immigrant panel — for a first-gen longitudinal panel use NIS (already on the roadmap). The foreign-parent subsample is modest (Hispanic/Asian-origin concentrated) → thin cells for fine origin × outcome; pool or use region-of-birth. Fine geography is restricted. Cohort born 1980-84 = pre-frontier for the most recent flows. [SOURCE: nlsinfo.org/content/cohorts/nlsy97/topical-guide/family-background/parent-characteristics]
**Graph fit:** `second_generation` / `labor_longitudinal` layer; parental-country-of-birth / region × respondent-outcome × round; region-of-birth joins origin layer (coarse); national; pairs with CILS (metro mechanism panel) and NIS (first-gen panel).

## Axis 3b — Health: nativity-linked MORTALITY (the Hispanic-paradox dataset done right)

### NLMS — National Longitudinal Mortality Study (CPS × death certificates, with country of birth)
**Source:** US Census Bureau + NHLBI / NCI / NIA / NCHS (sponsoring agencies) · **Access:** TWO tiers — (a) **PUBLIC-USE files** (a set of reference + analysis extracts) distributed free via **NHLBI BioLINCC** (register + DUA, no commercial restriction) and historically a Census public-use release; (b) the **full NLMS database** (3.8M records + Medicare/Medicaid/SEER/Census-tract supplements) only via **RODAS** indirect access through a sponsoring-agency PI proposal (Census statistician runs the code). Public files are downloadable + scriptable; full DB is mediated.
**Official:** project hub https://www.census.gov/topics/research/nlms.html · data availability https://www.census.gov/topics/research/nlms/nlms-da.html · Reference Manual v4.0 (PDF; Appendix D = UN country-of-birth codes) https://www.census.gov/content/dam/Census/about/about-the-bureau/adrm/MDAC/NLMS%20Reference%20Manual%20Version.pdf · BioLINCC public files (version 5) https://biolincc.nhlbi.nih.gov/studies/nlms/ · NCT00005159 https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT00005159 [SOURCE: census.gov/topics/research/nlms.html]
**Format / size:** CPS/ASEC samples 1973-2011 (39 cohorts) matched to the National Death Index; full DB ~3.8M records / 550k+ deaths. **BioLINCC version-5 public-use** = five files: full 11-yr follow-up (n=1,835,072), 1983-centered 6-yr (n=1,040,719), 1993-centered 6-yr (n=525,884), 2002-centered 6-yr (n=745,162), tobacco-use (n=493,282).
**Key variables — DOES IT HAVE NATIVITY? YES — country of birth is a core analysis variable.** Baseline (from CPS): age, sex, race, Hispanic origin, **country of birth / national origin** (UN country codes, Reference Manual Appendix D), education, income, employment, industry/occupation, marital status, region/state of residence; outcome: mortality status + **cause of death** (ICD-10, CDC 113-cause list), follow-up time. NCT00005159 lists the design variables explicitly including "country of birth."
**Why it fits — this is the Hispanic-paradox dataset done correctly, and it beats CDC WONDER.** The held/proposed health stack has NO mortality-by-nativity. **CDC WONDER Multiple-Cause-of-Death has race + Hispanic ethnicity + state/county but NO place-of-birth / nativity dimension** (verified: WONDER MCD dimensions are race, Hispanic ethnicity, age, sex, geography, cause — nativity is absent) — so WONDER can only proxy the paradox by Hispanic ethnicity and cannot separate foreign- vs US-born. NLMS *links the actual CPS country-of-birth to death records*, giving true foreign-born vs US-born mortality differentials by cause — the direct test of the immigrant-mortality (healthy-immigrant / Hispanic-paradox) effect that bears on net lifetime fiscal cost (lower mortality = longer benefit-collection but also longer tax-paying). Large enough for cause-specific nativity cells the survey-based NHANES/NHIS cannot support.
**Limit / honesty:** ends 2011 (no recent flows; the public files are not refreshed to the 2020s) — pre-frontier for current immigration. NDI match is imperfect (some deaths unmatched → follow-up censoring). Public files carry a reduced variable set + collapsed country-of-birth (fine origin + the supplements are RODAS-only). Sample is the non-institutionalized population (excludes prison/long-term-care). Country-of-birth ≠ legal status. Confirm the exact nativity-variable granularity on the specific BioLINCC public file before relying (full detail may be RODAS-gated). [SOURCE: clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT00005159] [SOURCE: census.gov/topics/research/nlms/nlms-da.html]
**Graph fit:** `health_mortality` layer; `country-of-birth`/nativity × cause-of-death × age/sex × follow-up; nativity is native to the file; country-of-birth joins origin layer (coarse on public file); the mortality counterpart to NHANES biomarkers and the corrective for CDC WONDER's missing nativity dimension.

## Axis 2b / 3c — Second-gen × crime × health, one national longitudinal panel

### ADD-HEALTH — National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent to Adult Health (immigrant-family oversample + biomarkers + delinquency)
**Source:** Carolina Population Center, UNC-Chapel Hill (Harris/Hummer/Aiello/Udry); congressionally-mandated, NICHD + 23 co-funders · **Access:** TWO tiers — **PUBLIC-USE** (reduced-size random subsample, Waves I-VI) free via ICPSR/DSDR download (requires supervisor name/email + reason; SAS/SPSS/Stata/R/ASCII/Delimited) + online SDA; **RESTRICTED-USE** (full sample + geocodes + genetic + birth/death records) via security contract.
**Official:** ICPSR 21600 https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/21600 (DOI 10.3886/ICPSR21600.v26) · study home https://addhealth.cpc.unc.edu/ · new-user overview (immigrant-status + ethnic-oversample tables) https://addhealth.cpc.unc.edu/wp-content/uploads/docs/user_guides/Overview_of_Add_Health_Harris.pdf · biomarker guide https://addhealth.cpc.unc.edu/wp-content/uploads/docs/user_guides/Add_Health_Wave_V_Biological_Data_and_Vital_Events_Dole_Hussey_Whitsel.pdf [SOURCE: icpsr.umich.edu/web/DSDR/studies/21600]
**Format / size:** **N≈20,745** in grades 7-12 in 1994-95; **six waves** (I 1994-95 → VI 2022-25), cohort now ~ages 40-44. In-school + in-home + parent + school-administrator + contextual; embedded sibling/twin genetic samples.
**Key variables — DOES IT HAVE NATIVITY? YES — explicit immigrant-status + ethnic oversamples designed for it.** The study's documentation reports an **"Immigrant Status"** breakdown and **ethnic oversamples** (Cuban, Puerto Rican, Chinese, plus large Mexican/Central-South-American/Filipino/Other-Asian counts: Mexico 1,767; Cuba 508; Central-South America 647; Puerto Rico 570; China 341; Philippines 643) explicitly to yield "large numbers of youth in immigrant families." Outcomes across waves: **delinquency / violence / criminal-justice-contact self-report**, substance use, education, employment/earnings, family formation, mental health, AND **measured biomarkers** (BP, BMI, HbA1c, glucose, lipid panel, hsCRP, creatinine/cystatin-C, inflammation/immune, plus omic/genetic).
**Why it fits — a single national panel that bridges THREE of this project's gaps at once.** It is simultaneously (a) a **second-generation** longitudinal panel (immigrant-family youth followed to age ~40, national — unlike CILS's two metros), (b) a **crime** source (self-reported delinquency/violence/justice-contact by immigrant generation — the developmental-trajectory complement to the arrest/incarceration stock data), and (c) a **health** source with measured biomarkers by nativity. No other US dataset links generational immigrant status to *both* offending-behavior trajectories and clinical biomarkers over the life course — directly testing whether children of immigrants show the lower-delinquency + healthy-immigrant pattern and whether it erodes across waves (assimilation-as-decline, a core segmented-assimilation prediction).
**Limit / honesty:** the PUBLIC-USE file is a **reduced random subsample** — immigrant-family and ethnic-oversample cells are far smaller than the full sample, so fine generation × origin × outcome analysis usually needs the **restricted** contract (security plan, fee). Cohort born ~1976-82 → pre-frontier for recent flows (same vintage issue as CILS/NLSY97). Self-report for delinquency. Genetic/geocode/vital-records are restricted. (Note: the ICPSR page carries a 2025 NIH "under review for potential modification" banner — confirm continued availability before building on it.) [SOURCE: addhealth.cpc.unc.edu/wp-content/uploads/docs/user_guides/Overview_of_Add_Health_Harris.pdf]
**Graph fit:** `second_generation` × `crime` × `health` longitudinal node; immigrant-generation / ethnic-origin × {delinquency, biomarker, earnings} × wave; origin joins origin layer (coarse, larger groups only on public file); national; the life-course bridge between CILS (mechanism, metros), NLSY97 (labor, national), and NHANES (biomarker, cross-section).
