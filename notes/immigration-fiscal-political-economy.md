## Axis 1: Funding Networks & Institutional Incentives

### The Tanton Network (Restrictionist Side)

**Confirmed facts** [SOURCE: Wikipedia, SPLC archives]:
- **John Tanton** (1934-2019): ophthalmologist, white nationalist, founded or funded 13 anti-immigration organizations
- **FAIR** (Federation for American Immigration Reform): founded 1979 by Tanton. SPLC-designated hate group.
- **CIS** (Center for Immigration Studies): co-founded by Tanton. Billed as "independent think tank" but created within the Tanton network.
- **NumbersUSA**: co-founded by Tanton. Lobbying arm.
- These three are called "The Big Three" or "The Nativist Lobby" (SPLC term)
- Tanton also founded: U.S. English, ProEnglish, The Social Contract Press (published white nationalist writers), Society for Genetic Education (pro-eugenics)

**Funding** [PARTIALLY VERIFIED]:
- **Colcom Foundation** (Cordelia Scaife May): major funder of FAIR and CIS [TRAINING-DATA — agent couldn't verify specific amounts in this session]
- **Pioneer Fund**: historical funder of Tanton orgs, founded 1937 to promote eugenics [TRAINING-DATA]
- FAIR's 990s show millions in annual revenue but specific donor breakdowns require OpenSecrets/ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer verification [UNVERIFIED]

**Key quote from Tanton** (1993 letter to Garrett Hardin): "I've come to the point of view that for European-American society and culture to persist requires a European-American majority, and a clear one at that." [SOURCE: SPLC]

### Pro-Immigration Side

- **Cato Institute**: Koch network funding. David Bier is primary immigration researcher. Produces detailed fiscal analyses.
- **ITEP** (Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy): left-leaning, focuses on tax contributions of undocumented immigrants. Funding sources less publicly documented than Tanton network [UNVERIFIED].
- **CGDev** (Center for Global Development): development economics perspective, generally pro-immigration. Michael Clemens, etc.

### Nominally Independent

- **NAS 2017 panel**: Commissioned by MacArthur Foundation [SOURCE: agent search]. Panel included economists from multiple perspectives. This is the closest thing to a neutral arbiter.
- **CBO**: Congressional mandate, nonpartisan by design. Recent reports (2024) cover federal fiscal impact and state/local costs.
- **Manhattan Institute**: Conservative but not Tanton-network. Daniel Di Martino is primary immigration fiscal researcher. Produces sophisticated models but consistently finds large costs for low-education immigrants.

### Heritage Foundation

- Heritage published the Richwine dissertation controversy (2013) — Jason Richwine's PhD thesis on IQ and immigration led to his resignation from Heritage after media backlash [TRAINING-DATA]. Heritage continues active immigration research with restrictionist lean.
- Heritage's immigration work emphasizes: reject amnesty, merit-based system, end birthright citizenship, "patriotic assimilation" [SOURCE: Heritage website].

## Axis 2: Methodological Fingerprinting

### Which Choices Each Org Consistently Makes

| Methodological Choice | Heritage/FAIR/CIS | Cato | Manhattan Inst. | NAS 2017 | CBO |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Average vs marginal cost** | Average (higher cost) | Marginal | Varies | Both | Marginal |
| **Include US-born children as "cost"** | Yes | No | Varies by scenario | Both scenarios | No (separate) |
| **Time horizon** | Short (annual snapshot) | Long (75-year NPV) | 10-yr and 30-yr | Both (annual + 75-yr) | 10-yr budget window |
| **Federal only vs all levels** | All levels (higher cost) | Federal only (net positive) | Both | Both | Separate reports |
| **Public goods (defense etc.)** | Allocated per capita | Marginal = ~$0 | Allocated per capita | Both | Marginal |
| **Dynamic/fiscal feedback** | No | Yes | Partial | Some scenarios | Yes |

**Key pattern**: The single largest methodological driver is **public goods allocation**. If you allocate defense, interest on debt, and federal overhead per capita to immigrants, costs are enormous. If you treat these as marginal (one more person doesn't change the defense budget), costs shrink or turn positive. [SOURCE: Cato Working Paper 82 critique of Manhattan Institute]

**Cato's specific critique of Manhattan Institute** [SOURCE: Cato Working Paper 82, Nov 2024]:
- MI assumes new arrivals cause "large, immediate increases in defense spending" — Cato calls this unrealistic
- MI assumes no increase in corporate tax payments from immigrant workers — Cato calls this inconsistent
- When Cato adjusts MI's own model with "more realistic assumptions," the result flips from net negative to net positive
- MI uses 10-year and 30-year horizons; Cato argues these systematically disadvantage young immigrants who haven't reached peak earning years

**Who presents ranges vs point estimates**:
- NAS 2017: Presented 8 scenarios with different assumptions → gold standard [SOURCE: NAS report]
- CBO: Presents central estimate with described uncertainty
- Heritage/FAIR: Point estimates, presented as definitive → advocacy signal
- Cato: Point estimates but shows sensitivity to key assumptions
- Manhattan Institute: Shows scenarios but leads with the most negative headline number

[PARTIALLY TRACED]:

- FAIR estimates total cost at ~$150.7B annually (federal + state/local), minus ~$32B in tax revenue

## Axis 4: Academic vs Policy Gap

[PARTIALLY SOURCED, PARTIALLY TRAINING-DATA]:

### What Labor Economists Actually Think

- **George Borjas** (Harvard): Most prominent academic restrictionist. Finds significant negative wage effects for native workers without high school diplomas. His work is cited by Heritage/CIS/FAIR.
- **David Card** (Berkeley, Nobel 2021): Found minimal wage effects from Mariel boatlift. His work is cited by pro-immigration side.
- **Giovanni Peri** (UC Davis): Finds immigration is complementary to native labor, not substitutive. Frequently cited by Cato/pro-immigration side.
- **The conflation problem**: Think tanks cite "economists agree immigration is net positive for the economy" — but this is about *legal* immigration and *GDP growth*. Unauthorized immigration's *fiscal* impact is a separate question with less consensus. Groups on both sides conflate these. [FRAMING-SENSITIVE]

### Academic Fiscal Impact Papers (Non-Think-Tank)

- NAS 2017 is the most authoritative [SOURCE: confirmed]
- CGDev has produced working papers (e.g., "Fiscal Effect of Immigration: Reducing Bias in Influential Estimates") [SOURCE: Exa search found CGDev paper URL]
- Academic work tends to show: fiscal impact depends enormously on assumptions, education level of immigrant, and time horizon. No single number is "correct."

## Axis 5: Historical Pattern-Matching

[SOURCE: New Yorker (Jan 2025), The Atlantic archives, Cambridge academic paper]:

- **Chinese Exclusion (1882)**: Fiscal arguments included "they drive down wages," "they don't assimilate," "they burden public services." Same structure as modern arguments. California imposed specific "foreign miners' tax" on Chinese workers. [SOURCE: Cambridge JEH paper on anti-Chinese taxation in Gold Rush California]
- **Irish/Italian immigration (1880s-1920s)**: "Hopeless burden" framing in The Atlantic and other publications of the era. Same cost-benefit accounting logic, same cultural anxieties dressed as economics.
- **1924 Immigration Act**: Explicitly race-based quotas. Justified with fiscal and cultural arguments.
- **Prop 187 (California, 1994)**: Denied public services to undocumented immigrants based on fiscal burden claims. Passed by voters, struck down by courts. In retrospect, California's economy thrived with large immigrant population. [TRAINING-DATA for retrospective assessment]

**Pattern**: The fiscal argument is perennial. The specific numbers change but the structure — "these particular immigrants cost more than they contribute" — has been deployed against every wave. The methodology has improved (NAS 2017 vs 1890s anecdotes) but the political deployment pattern is identical.
